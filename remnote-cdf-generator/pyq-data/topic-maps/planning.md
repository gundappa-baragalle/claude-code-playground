**[Q2769]** [2018]
> Despite being a high saving economy, capital formation may not result in significant increase in output due to

     A) weak administrative machinery
     B) illiteracy
     C) high population density
  ✅ D) high capital-output ratio

  ⚠️ **Trap:** High population density and illiteracy are commonly blamed for low output; but the specific economic concept here is capital-output ratio — how much capital is needed per unit of output.
  💡 **Eliminate:** High capital-output ratio means more capital is required to produce each unit of output, so even high savings do not translate to significant output increase — answer D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Capital-output ratio (COR) = Capital invested / Output produced; high COR implies inefficient capital use
     - India's ICOR (Incremental COR) is a key planning metric for investment requirements

**[Q4879]** [2009]
> Consider the following statements regarding Indian Planning : 1. The Second Five-Year Plan emphasized on the establishment of heavy industries. 2. The Third Five-Year Plan introduced the concept of import substitution as a strategy for industrialization. Which of the statements given above is/are correct ?

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
  ✅ C) Both 1 and 2
     D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students often mix up which Five-Year Plan introduced which concept; import substitution is associated with Second Plan era but formally conceptualized earlier; Third Plan continued this strategy.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Second Plan (Nehru-Mahalanobis model) emphasized heavy industry — Statement 1 is correct; Third Plan also stressed import substitution as industrialization strategy — Statement 2 is correct. Both are correct.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Second Five-Year Plan = Mahalanobis model emphasizing heavy industries
     - Import substitution industrialization was a key strategy in early Five-Year Plans

**[Q1763]** [2000]
> Economic liberalisation in India started with

  ✅ A) substantial changes in industrial licensing policy
     B) the convertibility of Indian rupee
     C) doing away with procedural formalities for foreign direct investment
     D) significant reduction in tax rates

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Rupee convertibility and FDI procedural changes were also part of 1991 reforms — candidates may pick these as the starting point rather than industrial licensing changes.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Economic liberalization in India formally began with the Industrial Policy of July 1991, which abolished industrial licensing for most sectors — this was the foundational reform.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Industrial Policy 1991 abolished licensing for most industries except 18 strategic sectors
     - 1991 LPG reforms: Liberalization, Privatization, Globalization — licensing change was the first pillar

     A) inclusive growth and poverty reduction
     B) inclusive and sustainable growth

  ✅ D) faster, sustainable and more inclusive growth

**[Q1445]** [2010] In the context of India's Five Year Plans, a shift in the pattern of industrialization, with lower emphasis on heavy industries and more on infrastructure begins in

     A) Fourth Plan
  ✅ B) Sixth Plan
     C) Eighth Plan
     D) Tenth Plan

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may associate the liberalization era (8th Plan, 1992-97) with the shift to infrastructure. The Sixth Plan is the correct answer for the shift from heavy industry emphasis.

**[Q951]** [2004] Consider the following statements: 1. The highest deciding body for planning in India is the Planning Commission of India. 2. ### GS3

**[2018]** Q11. How are the principles followed by the NITI Aayog different from those followed by the erstwhile Planning Commission in India? (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: VS | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Planning reforms, Cooperative federalism, Development policy

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The transition from Planning Commission to NITI Aayog represents a fundamental shift from centralized command planning to cooperative, competitive federalism.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Planning Commission: Five Year Plans, financial allocation powers, centralized planning, one-size-fits-all approach
      • NITI Aayog: advisory body, no fund allocation; emphasis on states as partners, not recipients
      • Cooperative federalism: NITI promotes state-led development; State Best Practices, Aspirational Districts Programme
      • Bottom-up approach: policy formation involves states' inputs; sub-groups of Chief Ministers
      • Think tank role: research and advocacy rather than command; NITI's 3-year action agenda, 7-year strategy, 15-year vision
      • Criticism: loss of financial leverage reduces NITI's ability to enforce development priorities
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** NITI Aayog's evolution reflects India's development philosophy shift toward market-led growth with states as primary implementers, though its advisory-only role limits transformative impact.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Economy, GS2 – Governance, GS2 – Polity
  🌐 Contemporary: NITI Aayog's Viksit Bharat@2047 framework makes this directly relevant.
