**[Q1116]** [2006]
> Assertion (A) : Muhammad bin Tughlaq issued a new gold coin which was called Dinar by Ibn Batutah. Reason (R) : Muhammad bin Tughlaq wanted to issue token currency in gold coins to promote trade with West Asian and North African countries.

     A) Both A and R are individually true R is the correct explanation of A.
     B) Both A and R are individually true R is not the correct explanation of A.
  ✅ C) A is true but R is false.
     D) A is false but R is true.

  ⚠️ **Trap:** The Assertion about Muhammad bin Tughlaq issuing a gold coin called Dinar is true. But the Reason is wrong — he issued token currency in COPPER/BRASS, not gold, to substitute silver tankas (not to promote West Asian trade).
  💡 **Eliminate:** Muhammad bin Tughlaq's famous token currency experiment used bronze/copper coins, not gold. The Dinar was indeed a gold coin he issued but for different reasons. A is true, R is false — answer is C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Muhammad bin Tughlaq issued token bronze currency to replace silver tanka
     - Ibn Battutah called his gold coin 'Dinar'
     - Token currency failed due to counterfeiting

**[Q625]** [2001]
> The Mongols under Gengis Khan invaded India during the reign of

     A) Balban
     B) Feroze Tughlaq
  ✅ C) Iltutmish
     D) Muhammad bin Tughlaq

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Balban and Muhammad bin Tughlaq are associated with subsequent Mongol invasions; the first major Mongol invasion under Genghis Khan is specifically during Iltutmish's reign.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Genghis Khan's Mongols invaded the Punjab region in 1221 during Iltutmish's reign — Iltutmish wisely refused to give asylum to Jalal ud-Din Mangburni to avoid direct Mongol confrontation.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Mongols under Genghis Khan invaded India in 1221 during Iltutmish's reign (Slave Dynasty)
     - Iltutmish refused shelter to Khwarezm Shah's son Jalal ud-Din to avoid Mongol wrath

**[Q3056]** [2000]
> Match List I with List II and select the correct answer using the codes given below the Lists List I | List II I. Iqta | (A) Marathas II. Jagir | (B) Delhi Sultans III. Amaram | (C) Mughals IV. Mokasa | (D) Vijayanagara

     A) I-C, II-B, III-A, IV-D
     B) I-B, II-C, III-D, IV-A
  ✅ C) I-B, II-C, III-A, IV-D
     D) I-C, II-B, III-D, IV-A

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Jagir is associated with Mughals, not Marathas; Mokasa is associated with Marathas or Vijayanagara; Iqta is Delhi Sultanate's specific land assignment system.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Iqta (Delhi Sultans), Jagir (Mughals), Amaram (Vijayanagara), Mokasa (Marathas) — Matching: I-B, II-C, III-D, IV-A is correct.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Iqta = revenue assignment system of Delhi Sultanate (assigned to muqtis/walis)
     - Jagir = Mughal land revenue assignment to nobles (jagirdars); Amaram = Vijayanagara military service tenure

**[Q386]** [1998]
> Assertion (A) : At first the Turkish administration in India was essentially military. Reason (R) : The country was parcelled out as ‘Iqtas’ among leading military leaders.

  ✅ A) Both A and R are true, and R is the correct explanation of A
     B) Both A and R are true, but R is NOT a correct explanation of A
     C) A is true, but R is false
     D) A is false, but R is true

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Both assertion and reason are true, but students must determine if the Iqta system directly explains military administration — it does, making A correct.
  💡 **Eliminate:** The Iqta system assigned land revenue to military commanders in lieu of salary — this directly explains why early Turkish administration was essentially military in character.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Iqta system: land grants to military commanders for administration and revenue collection
     - Early Delhi Sultanate was military state — Turkish nobles held both military and administrative powers

**[Q47]** [1997]
> After consolidating his power, Balban assumed the grand title of

     A) Tute-i-Hind
     B) Kaisr-i-Hind
  ✅ C) Zil-i-Ilahi
     D) Din-i-Ilahi

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Din-i-Ilahi (Akbar's syncretic religion) and Zil-i-Ilahi both sound similar and divine. Tute-i-Hind ('Parrot of India') is Amir Khusrau's title. Students confuse these between Balban and Akbar.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Balban's title: 'Zil-i-Ilahi' = Shadow of God on Earth — reflects his divine kingship theory. Tute-i-Hind = Amir Khusrau (poet). Din-i-Ilahi = Akbar (religion). Kaisr-i-Hind = British medal (Mahatma Gandhi returned it).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Zil-i-Ilahi = Shadow of God on Earth — Balban's claim to divine authority
     - Balban's theory: king is god's representative, established court protocol (sijda, paibos)
     - Tute-i-Hind = Amir Khusrau

**[Q1152]** [1995]
> The word ‘Hindu’ as reference to the people of Hind (India) was first used by

     A) the Greeks
     B) the Romans
     C) the Chinese
  ✅ D) the Arabs

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Greeks and Romans had contact with India; Chinese travelers like Xuanzang visited India; all seem plausible. The term 'Hindu' derived from the Persian/Arabic pronunciation of 'Sindhu' river.
  💡 **Eliminate:** The word 'Hindu' was first used by Arabs (and Persians) referring to people east of the Indus (Sindhu) river. Arab geographers used 'Al-Hind' for India. Answer: D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - 'Hindu' derives from Persian/Arabic 'Sindhu' → 'Hindu' (people of the Indus region)
     - Arabs first used 'Hindu' as a geographic-ethnic descriptor
     - Greeks called the region 'Indos/India'

**[2023]** Q12. What were the major technological changes introduced during the Sultanate period? How did those technological changes influence the Indian society? (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Technology transfer in medieval India, Sultanate's economic impact, Cultural synthesis

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The Delhi Sultanate period witnessed transformative technological introductions that reshaped India's agricultural productivity, urban economy, and social fabric.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Agricultural technology: Persian wheel expanded irrigation capacity
      • Construction technology: true arch, dome, squinch — architectural revolution
      • Textile: spinning wheel (charkha) transformed cloth production
      • Social impact: new occupational groups, guild structures, urban growth
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The Sultanate's technological contributions represent a significant chapter in India's history of innovation through cross-cultural exchange.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 — Medieval History, GS1 — Art & Architecture
  🌐 Contemporary: Technology transfer and innovation through cultural exchange remain relevant in today's globalisation discourse.

**[2018]** Q2. Assess the importance of the accounts of the Chinese and Arab travellers in the reconstruction of the history of India. (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Importance of foreign accounts in Indian historiography, Limitations of medieval sources

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Chinese and Arab travellers left invaluable accounts of India's social, religious, and political life, filling critical gaps in indigenous historical records.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Fa Hien and Xuanzang documented Buddhist India — monastic life, university education (Nalanda), political conditions
      • Al-Biruni's Kitab-ul-Hind is the most systematic account of Hindu religion, philosophy and science
      • Ibn Battuta described the Delhi Sultanate's administration, trade routes, and customs
      • Limitations: travellers' cultural biases, language barriers, selective coverage of courts/religious centers
      • Corroborative value: confirm or challenge domestic sources like inscriptions and chronicles
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While foreign travel accounts must be read critically for bias, they remain indispensable primary sources for reconstructing India's social and cultural history.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 Culture — India's historical cultural diversity, Art & Architecture — descriptions of temples and monuments
  🌐 Contemporary: Historiographic debates on medieval India's characterization continue to be politically sensitive.
