**[Q3443]** [2023]
> In the recent years Chad, Guinea, Mali and Sudan caught the international attention for which one of the following reasons common to all of them?

     A) Discovery of rich deposits of rare earth elements
     B) Establishment of Chinese military bases
     C) Southward expansion of Sahara Desert
  ✅ D) Successful coups

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may be distracted by options about Chinese bases or rare earth elements (both partially plausible for Africa), but all four countries had military coups — the unifying factor.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Chad (2021), Guinea (2021), Mali (2021), Sudan (2021-2023) all experienced successful military coups — the common thread. Answer: D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Wave of coups in West/Central Africa 2020-2023: Mali (twice), Guinea, Burkina Faso, Niger, Chad, Sudan
     - These coups reflect dissatisfaction with civilian governments and French military presence in the Sahel

**[Q3432]** [2023] Which one of the following countries has been suffering from decades of civil strife and food shortages and was in news in the recent past for its very severe famine?

     A) Angola
     B) Costa Rica
     C) Ecuador
  ✅ D) Somalia

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Angola has had civil strife but its food situation improved post-2002 peace deal; Costa Rica and Ecuador are stable democracies — Somalia is the clearly correct answer.

### GS1

**[2014]** Q25. How does India see its place in the economic space of rising natural resource rich Africa? (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHERE | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: India's economic interests in Africa, Resource security, Development partnership

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Africa's vast natural resource wealth combined with India's growing energy and mineral needs has made the continent a strategic priority in India's foreign policy.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • India's energy security: oil imports from Nigeria, Angola, Libya
      • Minerals: manganese, cobalt, phosphate from Africa for Indian industry
      • India-Africa Forum Summit as institutional mechanism for engagement
      • Lines of credit through EXIM Bank for infrastructure projects
      • Competition and complementarity with China's presence in Africa
      • Diaspora of Indian origin in East and Southern Africa as soft power
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's engagement in Africa must move beyond resource extraction to building genuine development partnerships that leverage India's technological and human capital strengths.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: India's foreign policy, GS3: Energy security, Economy: External sector
  🌐 Contemporary: India's Africa strategy has gained urgency with China's aggressive BRI expansion across Africa.

### GS2

**[2025]** Q9. India-Africa digital partnership is achieving mutual respect, co-development and long-term institutional partnerships. Elaborate. (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: India-Africa digital partnership, South-South cooperation, Technology diplomacy

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's digital partnership with Africa is redefining cooperation through co-development of digital public infrastructure rather than aid-dependent models.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • India's e-ITEC training programmes have trained thousands of African civil servants in digital governance
      • UPI rollout to African countries, pan-Africa e-network for telemedicine and tele-education
      • Aadhaar-like digital ID frameworks shared with African nations under DPI model
      • Mutual respect: India's South-South approach avoids conditionality unlike Western/Chinese models
      • Institutional partnerships: G20 DPI framework, India-Africa Forum Summits, ITEC
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India-Africa digital partnership, rooted in mutual respect and demand-driven co-development, represents a scalable model for inclusive technology diplomacy in the Global South.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 India Foreign Policy, GS3 Digital Economy, SDG 17 Partnerships
  🌐 Contemporary: India's G20 DPI initiative (2023) placed India-Africa digital cooperation at the center of global development discourse.

**[2021]** Q9. "If the last few decades were of Asia's growth story, the next few are expected to be of Africa's." In the light of this statement, examine India's influence in Africa in recent years.  (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: India-Africa partnership, Africa's growth potential, South-South cooperation

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** As Africa emerges as the next frontier of global growth, India's engagement on the continent has evolved from a historical solidarity-based relationship to a multi-dimensional strategic partnership.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Development partnership: Lines of Credit ($29 billion), e-Vidya Bharti (tele-education), e-Arogya Bharati (telemedicine), Vaccine Maitri during COVID
      • Trade: India-Africa trade crossed $100 billion; India is Africa's 3rd largest trading partner
      • Strategic: India advocated African Union's permanent G20 membership (achieved 2023); support at UNSC
      • People-to-people: ITEC programme trains African professionals; Indian diaspora as bridge
      • Challenges: China's deeper infrastructure investments; India's capacity constraints in project execution
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's influence in Africa rests on its credibility as a developmental partner — but translating goodwill into concrete infrastructure and trade outcomes is the critical next step.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 IR, GS3 Economy, Global Issues
  🌐 Contemporary: African Union's G20 membership in 2023 under India's presidency was a landmark diplomatic achievement.

**[2015]** Q17. Increasing interest of India in Africa has its pros and cons. Critically examine. (200 words) 12.5
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: IMPACT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: South-south cooperation, Development diplomacy, India's global outreach

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's growing interest in Africa reflects genuine strategic and economic imperatives, bringing both significant opportunities and complex challenges.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Pros: trade and investment opportunities, raw materials access, UN vote support, diaspora links
      • Pros: development partnerships — ITEC, Pan-African e-network, vaccine supply
      • Cons: competition with China (who has deeper financial pockets)
      • Cons: capacity to deliver promised credit lines limited
      • Cons: Africa's infrastructure needs vs India's project execution delays
      • Way forward: focus on quality over quantity, technology transfer, skill development
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's Africa engagement must evolve beyond summits and credit lines to deliver tangible development outcomes that distinguish India from China's transactional approach.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: IR, Economy: External sector, Society: Indian Diaspora
  🌐 Contemporary: AU's G20 membership (2023, pushed by India) and India-Africa trade summit discussions are directly relevant.

### Common Trap Patterns on This Topic
- All four leaders are real but obscure — test-takers confuse Nigeria's Obasanjo (military background turned civilian president) with other military rulers. Zambia's Mwanawasa is least known.
- Zimbabwe's crisis is multifaceted; students may mark option A (ethnic conflict) or C (drought). The primary cause was Mugabe's forced land redistribution from white farmers to Black Zimbabweans.
- Options A and B suggest South Africa sided with one ethnic group, which seems plausible given South Africa's own ethnic history; the correct answer involves a diplomatic/peacekeeping role.
- Liberia is often confused with Libya or other African conflict zones. The civil war context (Charles Taylor era) is specific and may be confused with other African conflicts.
- Hema-Lendu conflict is specific to DRC (Ituri region) — students may guess Nigeria or Indonesia given those countries' own ethnic conflicts.
