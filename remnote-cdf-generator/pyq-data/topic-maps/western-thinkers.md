**[2024]** Q3. Given below are three quotations of great thinkers. What do each of these quotations convey to you in the present context ?  (c) "In law, a man is guilty when he violates the rights of others. In ethics, he is guilty if he only thinks of doing so." - Immanuel Kant (Answer in 150 words)  10
  *Paper: GS4 | Dim: VS | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Kant's ethical philosophy, Relationship between law and morality, Intent vs act in ethics

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Kant's quote illuminates the fundamental distinction between legal and moral responsibility: law punishes acts while ethics judges the very intent behind them.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Legal guilt requires overt violation of rights — act-based
      • Moral guilt begins with intention — even unconcealed thought is ethically culpable
      • Implications for public servants: corrupt intent is unethical even without completed act
      • Real-world application: corrupt officer who didn't manage to take bribe is still morally guilty
      • Kantian ethics elevates inner virtue over outward compliance — crucial for civil service character
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Kant's insight demands that civil servants cultivate not just legal compliance but genuine inner virtue, where even the thought of corruption is recognized as ethically unacceptable.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS4 - Ethics Fundamentals, GS4 - Probity in governance
  🌐 Contemporary: Anti-corruption frameworks that rely only on punishing overt acts miss the Kantian dimension — organizational culture must also deter corrupt intent.

**[2020]** Q3. (b) 'The will to power exists, but it can be tamed and be guided by rationality and principles of moral duty.' Examine this statement in the context of international relations. (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS4 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Ethics of power in international relations, Rational taming of power, Moral duty in statecraft

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The will to power—whether in individuals or states—can indeed be tamed by rationality and moral duty, but this requires conscious institutional design and ethical leadership.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Will to power: states naturally seek to maximize power (Realism—Morgenthau, Mearsheimer)
      • Taming through rationality: cost-benefit calculus of war vs. cooperation; deterrence theory
      • Moral duty constraint: UN Charter; Responsibility to Protect; India's non-violence tradition
      • Example: post-WWII European integration—former adversaries chose cooperation over domination
      • Limitation: moral duty works only when institutionalized (UN, ICJ, treaties)
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The ethical taming of power in international relations requires both internalized moral values and robust international institutions that make principled conduct strategically rational.
  🔗 Interlinkages: International Relations: Global Issues, International Relations: International Organizations
  🌐 Contemporary: Russia-Ukraine conflict demonstrates the limits of moral constraints on state power when institutional frameworks are bypassed.

**[2018]** Q6. (a) What do each of the following quotations mean to you in the present context ?  "The true rule, in determining to embrace, or reject any thing, is not whether it has any evil in it; but whether it has more evil than good. There are few things wholly evil or wholly good. Almost every thing, especially of governmental policy, is an inseparable compound of the two; so that our best judgment of the preponderance between them is continually demanded."—Abraham Lincoln (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS4 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Pragmatic ethics in governance, Balancing competing goods

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Lincoln's ethical insight captures the fundamental challenge of governance: most policies involve imperfect choices between competing goods and evils, requiring wise judgment rather than rigid adherence to absolute principles.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Meaning: moral judgment requires continuous evaluation of the preponderance of good over evil — not pursuit of perfection but of the better option
      • Government policy application: dam displacing communities vs irrigation for thousands; forest protection vs tribal livelihoods
      • Practical wisdom (phronesis): Aristotle's concept of situational ethical judgment
      • Civil service application: balancing individual rights against public welfare, development against environmental protection
      • Avoiding both extremes: neither paralysis (refusal to act because no option is perfect) nor recklessness (ignoring costs)
      • Democratic deliberation: plural stakeholder consultation ensures balanced assessment of competing goods and evils
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Lincoln's framework provides civil servants with a practical ethical compass — not moral absolutism, but principled judgment that honestly weighs the preponderance of good over evil in complex governance situations.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS4 Ethics — Thinkers, Applied Ethics — Governance, Case Studies
  🌐 Contemporary: Policy debates around environmental clearances, data privacy vs security, and social welfare vs fiscal prudence all require Lincoln's kind of balanced ethical assessment.

**[2016]** Q3. (a) Analyse John Rawls's concept of social justice in the Indian context. (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS4 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Rawls and Indian social justice, Affirmative action ethics, Constitutional justice

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** John Rawls's theory of justice provides a compelling philosophical foundation for India's constitutional commitment to social justice, particularly affirmative action for historically marginalized communities.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Veil of ignorance: rational persons would choose equal basic liberties and prefer the difference principle
      • Indian context: reservations for SC/ST/OBC reflect difference principle—inequalities structured to benefit least advantaged
      • Critique: Rawls's liberal individualism sits uneasily with India's community-based identity politics
      • Application: MGNREGA, food security—structural transfer of resources to least advantaged
      • Limitation: caste as identity doesn't fully align with Rawls's class-neutral framework
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Rawlsian justice offers a powerful but imperfect lens for evaluating India's social justice policies, requiring adaptation to India's caste-based social realities.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Society: Indian Society (Caste), Polity: Constitutional Framework (DPSP)
  🌐 Contemporary: Debates about extending reservations to economically weaker sections (EWS) and sub-categorization within SC/ST quotas invoke Rawlsian principles.

**[2015]** Q2. Given are two quotations of moral thinkers/philosophers. For each of these, bring out what it means to you in the present context :  (b) "We can easily forgive a child who is afraid of the dark; the real tragedy of life is when men are afraid of the light." (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS4 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Fear of truth and knowledge, Intellectual courage, Platonic ethics

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Plato's reflection on the tragedy of men fearing the light speaks to humanity's deeper tendency to prefer comfortable ignorance over transformative truth.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Children fear darkness (unknown) naturally—this is understandable and temporary
      • Adults fearing light (truth, accountability, change) is a moral tragedy—it is willful blindness
      • In governance: bureaucrats who suppress information, politicians who avoid accountability
      • In civil society: citizens who prefer fake news to inconvenient truths
      • Remedy: cultivation of intellectual courage, critical thinking, freedom of expression
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The greatest ethical challenge for modern democracies is cultivating citizens and leaders who embrace the light of truth rather than retreat into the comforting darkness of ignorance.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Ethics: Ethics Fundamentals, Governance: E-Governance & Transparency
  🌐 Contemporary: Post-truth era and misinformation crisis make Plato's warning about fearing the light more relevant than ever.

**[2014]** Q3. (b) "Human beings should always be treated as 'ends' in themselves and never as merely 'means'" Explain the meaning and significance of this statement, giving its implications in the modern techno-economic society. (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS4 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Human dignity in ethics, Technology-economy nexus and human values

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Kant's Formula of Humanity establishes the inviolable dignity of every human being, a principle increasingly challenged by the instrumentalizing tendencies of modern techno-economic society.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Meaning: humans possess intrinsic worth — they must never be reduced to tools for others' ends
      • Deontological basis: moral duty independent of consequences — right action respects persons unconditionally
      • Modern techno-economic violations: gig economy workers treated as algorithms, surveillance capitalism harvesting personal data, sweatshop labor
      • Implications: corporate ethics (labour rights), AI ethics (algorithmic bias), data privacy, consumer rights
      • Public policy dimension: welfare policies must respect human dignity — not just GDP metrics
      • Contrast with utilitarian calculus that may sacrifice individuals for aggregate good
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** As technology accelerates economic instrumentalization, Kant's principle serves as an ethical guardrail reminding societies to place human dignity above profit and efficiency.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS4 Ethics Fundamentals, S&T — AI Ethics, Economy — Labour
  🌐 Contemporary: AI replacing jobs, gig economy exploitation, and data harvesting by corporations make Kant's principle increasingly urgent for policy and governance.

**[2013]** Q6. Given below are three quotations of great moral thinkers/philosophers. For each of these quotations, bring out what it means to you in the present context :   (b) "Nearly all men can withstand adversity, but if you want to test a man's character, give him power."—Abraham Lincoln (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS4 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Power and character, Ethics of leadership

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Lincoln's insight that power is the truest test of character cuts to the heart of democratic governance — where unchecked authority routinely corrupts even individuals who displayed virtue in adversity.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Meaning: adversity calls for endurance and resilience, but power tests deeper values — whether one remains just, humble, and accountable when unchecked
      • Examples of power corrupting: historical despots; modern scams involving officials with absolute discretion
      • Constitutional safeguards: separation of powers, judicial review, elections, RTI — institutional checks on power
      • Civil service relevance: district magistrate or IPS officer with wide discretionary powers — integrity tested when no accountability
      • Counter-examples: civil servants who used power responsibly (T.N. Seshan electoral reforms, E. Sreedharan infrastructure integrity)
      • Leadership ethics: power as a means to serve, not as an end in itself
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Ethical governance requires structural checks on power alongside the cultivation of leaders whose character remains steadfast under the most corrupting test — authority itself.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS4 Ethics — Thinkers, Governance — Civil Services, Polity — Checks and Balances
  🌐 Contemporary: In an era of executive dominance and weakening oversight institutions, Lincoln's insight serves as a timely reminder for democratic governance.
