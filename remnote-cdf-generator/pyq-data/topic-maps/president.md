**[Q3693]** [2025]
> Consider the following statements with regard to pardoning power of the President of India :  I. The exercise of this power by the President can be subjected to limited judicial review. II. The President can exercise this power without the advice of the Central Government.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

  ✅ A) I only
     B) II only
     C) Both I and II
     D) Neither I nor II

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may think the President exercises pardoning power independently — but the Supreme Court has held that pardoning power is exercisable only on Cabinet advice, subject to limited judicial review.
  💡 **Eliminate:** The SC held pardoning power (Article 72) can be subject to limited judicial review (I correct). The President CANNOT exercise pardoning power without Cabinet advice (II wrong). Answer: A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Article 72: President's pardoning power — exercised on Cabinet advice (binding)
     - SC in Epuru Sudhakar case held pardoning power subject to limited judicial review

**[Q4675]** [2024]
> With reference to the Parliament of India, consider the following statements:  1. Prorogation of a House by the President of India does not require the advice of the Council of Ministers. 2. Prorogation of a House is generally done after the House is adjourned sine die but there is no bar to the President of India prorogating the House which is in session. 3. Dissolution of the Lok Sabha is done by the President of India who, save in exceptional circumstances, does so on the advice of the Council of Ministers.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 only
     B) 1 and 2
  ✅ C) 2 and 3
     D) 3 only

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 1 is the trap: prorogation does require Cabinet advice — the President acts on the advice of CoM for prorogation. Statement 1 is incorrect.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Prorogation requires Cabinet advice (Statement 1 wrong). Statement 2 (President can prorogue even a sitting House) and Statement 3 (dissolution on CoM advice) are correct. Answer: C (2 and 3).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Prorogation: terminates a session; requires Council of Ministers' advice — not unilateral presidential act
     - President can prorogue a House even while in session (before adjournment sine die)

**[Q3454]** [2023]
> Consider the following statements :  1. If the election of the President of India is declared void by the Supreme Court of India, all acts done by him/her in the performance of duties of his/her office of President before the date of decision become invalid. 2. Election for the post of the President of India can be postponed on the ground that some Legislative Assemblies have been dissolved and elections are yet to take place. 3. When a Bill is presented to the President of India, the Constitution prescribes time limits within which he/she has to declare his/her assent.  How many of the above statements are correct?

     A) Only one
     B) Only two
     C) All three
  ✅ D) None

  ⚠️ **Trap:** All three statements are incorrect: (1) acts done before void declaration remain valid (de facto doctrine); (2) Presidential election cannot be postponed due to dissolved assemblies; (3) Constitution prescribes no specific time limit for Presidential assent.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Statement 1: Acts of a de facto President remain valid even after election is voided (Article 71); Statement 2: Article 62 — election must be held even if assemblies dissolved; Statement 3: Constitution sets no time limit for Presidential assent. All wrong — Answer: D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Article 71: acts done before Supreme Court voids Presidential election remain valid
     - Article 62: Presidential election must occur before expiry or as soon as vacancy arises — cannot be postponed
     - Constitution has no specific time limit for Presidential assent to bills (unlike Governor's assent)

**[Q2751]** [2018]
> With reference to the election of the President of India, consider the following statements:  1. The value of the vote of each MLA varies from State to State. 2. The value of the vote of MPs of the Lok Sabha is more than the value of the vote of MPs of the Rajya Sabha.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

  ✅ A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
     C) Both 1 and 2
     D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 is a classic trap — students assume LS MPs have higher vote value; in Presidential elections, all elected MPs (LS and RS) have the SAME vote value, calculated uniformly.
  💡 **Eliminate:** MLA vote value varies by state (state population / number of MLAs × 1/1000) — statement 1 correct. But LS and RS MPs have THE SAME vote value (determined to balance MLA votes total) — statement 2 wrong.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Presidential election: MLA vote value = state population/(total MLAs × 1000)
     - All elected MPs (LS and RS) have same vote value — calculated to equal total MLA vote values
     - Nominated members (LS, RS, state legislative assemblies) cannot vote in Presidential election

**[Q2217]** [2014]
> Consider the following statements :  1. The President shall make rules for the more convenient transaction of the business of the Government of India, and for the allocation among Ministers of the said business. 2. All executive actions of the Government of India shall be expressed to be taken in the name of the Prime Minister.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

  ✅ A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
     C) Both 1 and 2
     D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 is wrong — all executive actions are expressed in the name of the PRESIDENT (not Prime Minister) under Article 77; Statement 1 is correct (Article 77(3)).
  💡 **Eliminate:** Article 77(1): Executive actions of Government of India expressed in President's name; Article 77(3): President makes rules for transaction of business — Statement 1 correct, Statement 2 wrong; answer is A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Article 77(3): President makes rules for business allocation among Ministers
     - Article 77(1): All executive actions in name of President (not PM)

**[Q360]** [1997]
> Which one of the following are/is stated in the Constitution of India ? I. The President shall not be a member of either House of Parliament. II. The Parliament shall consist of the President and two Houses. Choose the correct answer from the codes given below :

     A) Neither I nor II
  ✅ B) Both I and II
     C) I alone
     D) II alone

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students often confuse that the President is 'part of Parliament' but not a 'member' — the distinction trips many. Both statements sound plausible individually.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Article 60/79 clearly states Parliament consists of the President and two Houses; Article 59 bars the President from being a member of either House — both are correct.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Article 79: Parliament = President + Lok Sabha + Rajya Sabha
     - Article 59: President cannot be member of either House

**[Q731]** [1996]
> Which one of the following is part of the electoral college for the election of the President of India but does not .form part of the forum for his impeachment ?

     A) Lok Sabha
     B) Rajya Sabha
     C) State Legislative Councils
  ✅ D) State Legislative Assemblies

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may think State Legislative Councils are excluded from both processes; actually they are excluded from BOTH (not part of electoral college and not part of impeachment). The question asks what is part of electoral college but NOT impeachment.
  💡 **Eliminate:** State Legislative Assemblies are part of President's electoral college (Article 54) but NOT part of the impeachment process (Article 61 — only Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha participate). Answer: D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Presidential election electoral college: elected MPs + elected MLAs (State Legislative Assemblies)
     - Presidential impeachment: only Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha — State Assemblies excluded

**[2025]** Q3. Compare and contrast the President's power to pardon in India and in the USA. Are there any limits to it in both the countries? What are 'preemptive pardons'? (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: VS | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Clemency powers, Comparative constitutionalism, Rule of law

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The power to pardon, vested in the executive, serves as a necessary safety valve in the criminal justice system, though its scope, limits, and accountability differ significantly between India and the USA.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • India (Article 72): President can grant pardon, reprieve, respite or remit punishment; covers death sentences, court martial cases, union law offences; exercised on Cabinet advice; judicially reviewable for arbitrariness
      • USA (Article II §2): President can pardon for federal offences only; absolute power — not subject to judicial review; cannot pardon state crimes or impeachment outcomes
      • Limits in India: cannot pardon state offences (Governor's domain); SC can review for malafides
      • Limits in USA: cannot pardon self (debated); cannot pardon in cases of impeachment; limited to federal crimes
      • Preemptive pardons: pardons granted before charges/conviction (e.g., Ford pardoning Nixon); legally controversial but upheld in USA; no explicit provision in India
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While both systems vest clemency in the executive, India subjects it to constitutional accountability, whereas the US President's unreviewable pardon power raises serious rule of law concerns, especially when exercised preemptively.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 Polity, Comparative Constitution, GS4 Ethics
  🌐 Contemporary: Donald Trump's preemptive pardons (2025) and India's Supreme Court scrutiny of presidential pardons keep this topic current.

**[2022]** Q14. Critically examine the procedures through which the Presidents of India and France are elected. (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Presidential election, Comparative polity, Democratic systems

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The elections of the Presidents of India and France reflect fundamentally different democratic philosophies — indirect representation vs direct popular mandate.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • India: Electoral College comprising elected MPs (LS + RS) and elected MLAs; votes are weighted by state population and assembly size
      • India: Article 54-55; proportional representation ensuring federal balance
      • France: Direct universal adult suffrage; two-round system — if no majority in Round 1, top two candidates face run-off
      • Critical difference: India President is constitutional head (ceremonial); France President is powerful executive head
      • Criticism of Indian system: President may not reflect popular will; French system more directly democratic
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While India's system prioritizes federalism and parliamentary supremacy, France's direct election gives the President a strong popular mandate essential for its semi-presidential system.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Comparative polity, Federalism, Parliamentary vs Presidential systems
  🌐 Contemporary: Debate on direct election of Indian President resurfaces during constitutional reform discussions.

**[2015]** Q5. Resorting to ordinances has always raised concern on violation of the spirit of separation of powers doctrine. While noting the rationales justifying the power to promulgate ordinances, analyze whether the decisions of the Supreme Court on the issue have further facilitated resorting to this power. Should the power to promulgate ordinances be repealed?  (200 words) 12.5
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Ordinance power, Separation of powers, Executive vs legislature

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The President's ordinance-making power under Article 123 was conceived as an emergency legislative mechanism, but its frequent and controversial use has consistently raised concerns about bypassing parliamentary deliberation and violating the spirit of separation of powers.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Article 123: President can promulgate ordinances when Parliament is not in session if immediate action required
      • Rationale: urgency during recess, executive accountability through subsequent Parliamentary approval
      • Concerns: ordinances used to avoid legislative scrutiny, re-promulgation to circumvent Parliament (DC Wadhwa)
      • SC in DC Wadhwa: fraud on constitution to repeatedly re-promulgate same ordinance
      • Krishna Kumar Singh: ordinances have temporary force, their irrevocable effects can survive even if lapsed
      • Should power be repealed? — Extreme position; instead: restrict re-promulgation, mandate justification
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Rather than repealing the ordinance power, constitutional reform should codify strict conditions for use, prohibit re-promulgation and require Speakers to certify genuine urgency before Presidential assent to ordinances.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Polity — separation of powers, Polity — Parliament's role in checking executive, Governance — legislative quality
  🌐 Contemporary: Farm ordinances (2020), banking sector ordinances and electoral law ordinances reflect continued executive preference for this route.

**[2014]** Q5. Instances of President's delay in commuting death sentences has come under public debate as denial of justice. Should there be a time limit specified for the President to accept/reject such petitions? Analyse. (200 words) 12.5
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Presidential clemency, Judicial oversight, Death penalty reform

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The President's power to grant mercy under Article 72 serves as a vital humanitarian safeguard in India's criminal justice system, but inordinate delays in exercising this power have raised serious constitutional and human rights concerns.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Article 72 empowers President to pardon, commute, reprieve, respite or remit punishment
      • No constitutional time limit exists for deciding mercy petitions
      • SC in Shatrughan Chauhan (2014): undue delay in disposing mercy petition is ground for commutation
      • Mental illness, long incarceration, solitary confinement are additional mitigating factors
      • Arguments for time limit: prevents cruel, inhuman treatment; ensures certainty; ends prolonged uncertainty
      • Arguments against: President needs time for thorough review; no international standard
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** A statutory or constitutional time limit for Presidential action on mercy petitions would align India's clemency framework with constitutional values of dignity, justice and prohibition of arbitrariness.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Polity — President's constitutional role, Governance — criminal justice reform, International Relations — death penalty and human rights treaties
  🌐 Contemporary: India maintains death penalty for rarest of rare crimes; continued debates on its reform make this topic highly relevant.
