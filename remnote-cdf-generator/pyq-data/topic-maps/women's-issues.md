**[Q3577]** [2018]
> International Labour Organization's Conventions 138 and 182 are related to

  ✅ A) Child labour
     B) Adaptation of agricultural practices to global climate change
     C) Regulation of food prices and food security
     D) Gender parity at the workplace

  ⚠️ **Trap:** ILO deals with many workplace issues including gender parity and climate-agriculture adaptation making other options plausible.
  💡 **Eliminate:** ILO Convention 138: minimum age for work (child labour); Convention 182: worst forms of child labour — both specifically about child labour protection.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - ILO Convention 138 (1973): minimum age for employment to end child labour
     - ILO Convention 182 (1999): immediate elimination of worst forms of child labour
     - India ratified both Conventions in 2017

     D) Chaunsathyogini temple, Bheraghat

**[2024]** Q19. Globalization has increased urban migration by skilled, young, unmarried women from various classes. How has this trend impacted upon their personal freedom and relationship with family?
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: IMPACT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Globalization and women's migration, Personal freedom and family relations, Gender and development

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Globalization-induced urban migration of skilled young women has created a complex dynamic of expanded personal freedom alongside strained family relationships and new social vulnerabilities.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Increased economic independence — financial autonomy enhances decision-making power
      • Delayed marriage and childbearing — greater control over reproductive choices
      • Weakening of joint family control — but also loss of support system
      • Exposure to urban hazards — safety concerns, exploitation, loneliness
      • Generational conflict — traditional expectations clash with new lifestyles
      • Positive feedback: remittances empower families in origin areas
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While urban migration empowers women through economic participation, India needs robust safety nets, affordable housing, and workplace protections to ensure this trend benefits women holistically.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 — society and women, GS2 — governance and welfare schemes for women
  🌐 Contemporary: Women's LFPR in India remains low; urban migration patterns are shifting post-COVID with reverse migration trends.

**[2023]** Q9. Explain why suicide among young women is increasing in Indian society.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Women's mental health, Suicide, Gendered social pressures

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The rising suicide rates among young women in India reflect the fatal intersection of patriarchal social pressures, limited mental health support, and the failure of family and community institutions to adapt to women's changing aspirations.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Marital and family pressures: domestic violence, dowry demands, adjustment pressures in marital homes
      • Rising aspirations vs constrained opportunities: educated women facing patriarchal barriers suffer acute frustration
      • Social media: cyberbullying, body image pressure, online harassment disproportionately affect young women
      • Mental health stigma: women less likely to seek help; family denies mental illness out of shame
      • Academic pressure: competitive education system with inadequate pastoral care
      • Economic vulnerability: women with no independent income trapped in abusive situations
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Reversing the trend of female suicides requires simultaneously addressing patriarchal social structures, expanding mental health services, and creating safe channels for women to seek help without stigma.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Women's issues, GS2: Governance — mental health, Social welfare
  🌐 Contemporary: National Suicide Prevention Strategy 2022 is India's first dedicated policy framework but lacks gender-specific components.

**[2019]** Q19. What are the continued challenges for women in India against time and space?
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHAT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Women's challenges, Gender equality, Social change

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Despite constitutional guarantees of equality and decades of affirmative policies, Indian women continue to face a constellation of overlapping challenges that transcend time period and spatial location.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Time dimension: historical patriarchy remains in marriage, property, inheritance; child marriage persists
      • Space dimension: urban vs rural — different but equally limiting constraints on women's mobility and opportunities
      • Economic: low FLFPR, gender pay gap, concentration in informal sector
      • Physical safety: gender-based violence, harassment limiting women's mobility and professional choices
      • Digital: women's exclusion from digital economy and online harassment
      • Political: underrepresentation in Parliament and state legislatures despite 33% reservation bill passage
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Addressing women's challenges requires simultaneous action on economic, social, legal, and spatial dimensions, recognising that marginalisation operates differently across India's diverse geographies and communities.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Society, GS2: Governance — women's welfare, GS3: Labour market
  🌐 Contemporary: Nari Shakti Vandan Act 2023 (33% reservation) represents a major but long-delayed political intervention.

**[2018]** Q18. 'Women's movement in India has not addressed the issues of women of lower social strata.' Substantiate your view.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Women's movement, Intersectionality, Class and caste in feminism

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's women's movement, while achieving significant victories in law and policy, has been justly criticised for its tendency to foreground the concerns of educated, upper-caste, urban women at the expense of those most marginalised.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Urban-rural divide: movement focused on dowry deaths, domestic violence, divorce rights — less on rural women's land rights
      • Caste-blindness: mainstream feminism slow to address caste-based sexual violence (Mathura case vs Bhanwari Devi)
      • Economic marginalisation: SEWA's work on informal sector women's rights was largely outside mainstream movement
      • Tribal women: displacement, forest rights, sexual violence by security forces — absent from mainstream agenda
      • Intersectionality: Dalit feminists like Gopal Guru argued mainstream feminism 'appropriates Dalit women's struggles'
      • Post-2012 Nirbhaya: movement became more inclusive but still urban-centered
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** An intersectional approach that centres the experiences of Dalit, tribal, and rural women is essential for the Indian women's movement to truly represent all women rather than a privileged subset.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Society, GS2: Governance — vulnerable sections, Sociology
  🌐 Contemporary: Hathras and Manipur sexual violence cases revealed how caste mediates women's access to justice and public solidarity.

**[2015]** Q12. Discuss the positive and negative effects of globalization on women in India.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: IMPACT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Globalization and women, Gender and economy, Dual effects

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Globalization has been both liberator and oppressor for Indian women — expanding economic opportunities and cultural freedoms while simultaneously creating new forms of exploitation and vulnerability.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Positive effects: expanded employment (garment, BPO, service sectors); women's labour force participation in urban areas increased; global exposure to gender equality norms through media, NGOs, international conventions
      • Positive — education and aspiration: globalization raised aspirations; more girls pursuing higher education and professional careers; digital access provides information and networking
      • Negative — exploitation: garment sector feminization without adequate wages, safety, or rights (Rana Plaza disaster); domestic work migration and trafficking
      • Negative — cultural: commodification of women's bodies in advertising; cultural globalization eroding traditional support systems without replacing them
      • Negative — digital divide: rural women excluded from digital economy benefits; online harassment disproportionately affects women
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Globalization's impact on Indian women is a story of simultaneous progress and peril — capturing its benefits while mitigating its harms requires gender-sensitive policy at every level.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 Society, GS3 Economy, GS2 Governance
  🌐 Contemporary: NITI Aayog Gender Index tracks India's progress; India's LFPR for women at 37% (PLFS 2024) shows gradual improvement.

**[2014]** Q11. How does patriarchy impact the position of a middle class working woman in India?
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Gender inequality in workplace, Patriarchy and professional women, Work-life balance

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Patriarchy, embedded in social norms, family expectations, and institutional practices, continues to constrain the professional and personal autonomy of middle-class working women in India.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Double burden: women expected to manage domestic responsibilities alongside professional work — no equitable division of household labor
      • Glass ceiling: patriarchal attitudes in organizations limit women's promotion to senior positions
      • Gender pay gap: women in similar roles often earn less, reflecting patriarchal undervaluation of women's work
      • Safety concerns: fear of workplace harassment and unsafe commutes constrain women's employment choices
      • Marriage and childbearing: career interruptions disproportionately affect women due to patriarchal expectations of primary caregiving
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Dismantling patriarchy requires policy interventions — equal pay legislation, parental leave, childcare support — alongside deeper social transformation in gender norms.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 – Indian Society, GS2 – Social Justice, GS3 – Labour market
  🌐 Contemporary: Women's labor force participation in India remains among the lowest globally, with patriarchy cited as a key structural barrier.

**[2014]** Q12. Why do some of the most prosperous regions of India have an adverse sex ratio for women? Give your arguments.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Sex ratio and prosperity paradox, Son preference, Gender discrimination

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The adverse sex ratio in India's most prosperous states reveals that economic development alone cannot overcome deep-rooted patriarchal son preference.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Paradox: Punjab, Haryana (high per capita income) vs Kerala (lower income, better sex ratio)
      • Cultural explanation: dowry, land inheritance, old-age care through sons
      • Technology enabler: ultrasound made sex-selective abortion feasible
      • Policy response: PCPNDT Act, Beti Bachao Beti Padhao scheme
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Addressing the sex ratio deficit in prosperous regions demands tackling patriarchal cultural norms directly, independent of economic growth trajectories.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 — Society, Women, GS2 — Governance
  🌐 Contemporary: Beti Bachao Beti Padhao outcomes remain mixed despite years of implementation — cultural change is slow.

**[2014]** Q14. Discuss the various economic and socio-cultural forces that are driving increasing feminization of agriculture in India.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Women in agriculture, Rural development, Gender and economy

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Increasing feminization of agriculture in India reflects a complex interplay of economic distress, male migration, and evolving gender roles in rural areas.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Economic forces: farm distress, declining agricultural profitability push men to non-farm work; women fill the void without commensurate land rights
      • Male out-migration (rural-urban): leaves women as de facto farm managers, especially in states like Uttarakhand, J&K, Andhra Pradesh
      • MGNREGA: increased women's labor force participation in agriculture-adjacent work
      • Socio-cultural forces: gradual shift in gender norms; women-led SHGs gaining agency over agricultural decisions
      • But without land ownership, credit access, or extension services, feminization becomes a burden — 'feminization of poverty' risk
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Making feminization of agriculture truly empowering requires addressing land rights, credit access, and extension services for women farmers.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Indian Society and women's role, GS2: Welfare schemes for women, GS3: Agriculture and rural development
  🌐 Contemporary: PM-KISAN and other schemes now increasingly target women farmers; PM Fasal Bima Yojana coverage for women is expanding.
