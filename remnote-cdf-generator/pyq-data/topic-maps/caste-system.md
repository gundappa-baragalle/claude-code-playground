**[Q2901]** [2019]
> Consider the following statements about Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups (PVTGs) in India :  1. PVTGs reside in 18 States and one Union Territory. 2. A stagnant or declining population is one of the criteria for determining PVTG status. 3. There are 95 PVTGs officially notified in the country so far. 4. Irular and Konda Reddi tribes are included in the list of PVTGs.  Which of the statements given above are correct?

     A) 1, 2 and 3
     B) 2, 3 and 4
  ✅ C) 1, 2 and 4
     D) 1, 3 and 4

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 1 about 18 States and 1 UT — the number of states may have changed. Statement 3 (95 PVTGs) is a known figure but some candidates confuse it with 75 PVTGs (the correct official number).
  💡 **Eliminate:** PVTGs reside in 18 States and 1 UT (S1 correct). Stagnant/declining population is a PVTG criterion (S2 correct). There are 75 officially notified PVTGs, NOT 95 (S3 wrong). Irular and Konda Reddi are PVTGs (S4 correct). Answer: C (1, 2 and 4).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - 75 PVTGs are officially notified in India (not 95)
     - Criteria for PVTG: pre-agricultural technology, stagnant/declining population, extremely low literacy, subsistence economy

**[Q276]** [1996]
> In the Indian context the term De-notified tribes’ refers to

     A) tribes which are aboriginals
     B) nomadic tribes
     C) tribes practising shifting cultivation
  ✅ D) tribes which were earlier classified as criminal tribes

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Options A (aboriginals), B (nomadic), and C (shifting cultivation) all describe tribal characteristics. The specific term 'de-notified' relates to the Criminal Tribes Act colonial history.
  💡 **Eliminate:** De-notified tribes were those notified under the Criminal Tribes Act 1871 (colonial era) as 'criminal by birth.' After Independence, the Act was repealed in 1952, 'de-notifying' them. Correct: D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Criminal Tribes Act 1871 — British branded entire tribes as hereditary criminals
     - De-notified Tribes — 'de-notified' after repeal of Criminal Tribes Act 1952; still face stigma

**[2024]** Q20. Critically analyse the proposition that there is a high correlation between India's cultural diversities and socio-economic marginalities. (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: IMPACT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Caste and development, Social marginalization, Cultural diversity and inequality

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's remarkable cultural diversity — spanning caste, religion, ethnicity, and language — does correlate with socio-economic marginality, though the relationship is complex and not deterministic.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • SCs and STs face structural poverty — SECC data shows 79% of SC households among poorest quintile
      • Religious minorities (Muslims) face occupational segregation and lower HDI outcomes
      • Language-based exclusion — tribal communities in forest areas lacking access to state services in their language
      • Counter-examples: dominant OBC communities, prosperous Jain/Parsi minorities — diversity doesn't automatically mean marginality
      • Constitutional framework — reservations, PESA, minority rights — attempts to break the correlation
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While high correlation exists between cultural distinctiveness and marginality for the most excluded groups, India's diversity also contains examples of prosperous minority cultures — policy must address structural discrimination rather than treat diversity itself as the problem.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 Society, GS2 Governance — welfare of vulnerable sections, GS3 Economy — inclusive growth
  🌐 Contemporary: Caste Census debate and OBC sub-categorisation Supreme Court ruling make this immediately relevant.

**[2024]** Q9. Intercaste marriages between castes which have socio-economic parity have increased, to some extent, but this is less true of interreligious marriages. Discuss. (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Social change in India, Caste and religion as social barriers, Role of economic parity in social integration

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** While modernization has somewhat loosened caste endogamy especially among economically equal groups, religious identity continues to impose stronger barriers on interreligious marriages in India.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Economic parity reduces caste hierarchy concerns, enabling more intercaste unions particularly in urban educated settings
      • Religious identity is tied to community survival, cultural continuity, and personal laws making interreligious marriages more contested
      • Legal framework: Special Marriage Act provides for intercaste and interreligious marriages but social acceptance lags behind
      • Data point: NFHS data shows intercaste marriages rising gradually but interreligious marriages remain below 5%
      • Role of khap panchayats, community pressure and honour violence in discouraging both types
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** True social integration requires not just legal enablement but sustained efforts to reduce both caste prejudice and communal identity rigidities through education and inter-community dialogue.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 Society, GS2 Polity — personal laws, GS4 Ethics — social values
  🌐 Contemporary: Debate on Uniform Civil Code and inter-community marriages makes this topic highly relevant.

**[2024]** Q18. Despite comprehensive policies for equity and social justice, underprivileged sections are not yet getting the full benefits of affirmative action envisaged by the Constitution. Comment. (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Affirmative action, Social justice, Underprivileged sections

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Despite the Constitution's comprehensive framework for equity and social justice, the persistence of deprivation among underprivileged sections reveals deep implementation failures and structural barriers that formal affirmative action cannot overcome alone.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Reservation implementation gaps: unfilled reserved posts, lack of promotional reservations effectively
      • Creamy layer exclusion: benefits concentrated among more privileged within SC/ST/OBC categories
      • Social discrimination persists: untouchability in rural areas, caste atrocities continue despite legal prohibition
      • Economic exclusion: land ownership concentrated among upper castes; capital access barriers for SC/ST entrepreneurs
      • Intersectionality: SC women, disabled SC/ST face compounded marginalisation beyond what reservations address
      • Quality of access: nominal inclusion without quality — underfunded schools, poorly staffed health centres in SC/ST areas
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Realising the Constitution's social justice vision requires moving beyond reservations to address structural economic exclusions, ensure quality public services in marginalised communities, and eliminate social discrimination through cultural change.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: Social justice, GS1: Indian society, GS2: Governance — vulnerable sections
  🌐 Contemporary: Supreme Court's sub-categorisation judgment within SC reservations reflects ongoing debates about who benefits from affirmative action.

**[2023]** Q19. Why is caste identity in India both fluid and static? (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Caste fluidity and rigidity, Social mobility in India, Caste identity in democratic politics

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Caste in India presents a paradox — it is simultaneously a dynamic, adaptive social category and a deeply entrenched structural hierarchy that resists transformation.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Fluid dimensions: Sanskritization allows lower castes to adopt upper caste practices for status mobility; new caste identities form through political mobilization (OBC category)
      • Dominant caste concept: economic and political power shifts even within hierarchical framework
      • Static dimensions: Endogamy (same-caste marriage) persists even among educated urban populations
      • Hereditary occupation and ritual pollution concepts persist in rural areas despite legal prohibition
      • Caste-based discrimination in housing, employment and education remains structurally embedded
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Caste's simultaneous fluidity and rigidity reflects its complex role as both a lived social reality and a strategic political tool in India's democratic polity.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 Society, GS2 Polity — reservations, GS4 Ethics — discrimination
  🌐 Contemporary: Caste census debate and demands for sub-categorization of reservations make this topic politically significant.

**[2022]** Q18. Analyse the salience of 'sect' in Indian society vis-a-vis caste, region and religion.  (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Indian social structure, Caste and sect, Social identity

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The concept of 'sect' (sampradaya) in Indian society represents a voluntary religious community that operates alongside and sometimes in tension with the ascriptive hierarchies of caste, region, and religion.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Sect defined: voluntary community based on shared religious doctrine, devotional practice, and guru loyalty
      • Relation to caste: sects like Lingayats challenged caste hierarchy through spiritual equality
      • Sect vs region: sects create pan-regional identities (Vaishnavism across states) cutting across regional identities
      • Sect vs religion: sect straddles religions — many dargahs and deras attract both Hindus and Muslims
      • Contemporary sects: Radhasoami, Satnami, Dera Sacha Sauda — attract lower castes promising caste-free spiritual community
      • Political salience: sect leaders wield electoral influence when sect membership is large and cohesive
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Sects occupy a distinctive and underappreciated position in India's social fabric — simultaneously reinforcing and challenging caste, regional, and religious boundaries, making them important actors in India's plural democracy.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Indian society, Caste system, Religion and politics
  🌐 Contemporary: Dera Sacha Sauda controversy and its political affiliations illustrate the complex interplay of sect, caste, and democratic politics.

**[2017]** Q10. What are the two major legal initiatives by the State since Independence, addressing discrimination against Scheduled Tribes (STs)?  (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Tribal rights, Legal protection, Affirmative action

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Since independence, India has enacted two landmark legal frameworks specifically targeting the systemic discrimination and marginalisation faced by Scheduled Tribes.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989: criminalises specific acts of humiliation, violence, and economic exploitation against STs; special courts for fast-track trials
      • Forest Rights Act, 2006: recognises STs' pre-existing rights over forest land and community forest resources; reverses colonial-era dispossession
      • Constitutional provisions: Fifth and Sixth Schedules for administration, Article 19(5) for movement restrictions in tribal areas
      • PESA Act 1996: self-governance through gram sabhas in scheduled areas
      • Challenges: implementation gaps, land alienation continues, ST atrocities persist
      • Scheduled Tribes and OBC welfare schemes under Tribal Sub-Plan
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While the SC/ST Atrocities Act and Forest Rights Act represent significant legislative milestones, their full potential depends on robust implementation and addressing institutional barriers to tribal justice.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: Governance — vulnerable sections, GS2: Polity — fundamental rights, Environment
  🌐 Contemporary: Supreme Court rulings on Forest Rights Act implementation and 2018 SC/ST Act amendment controversy highlight ongoing tensions.

**[2015]** Q13. Debate the issue of whether and how contemporary movements for assertion of Dalit identity work towards annihilation of caste. (200 words) 12.5
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: VS | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Dalit identity movements and caste annihilation, Social justice and representation, Contemporary Dalit assertion

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Contemporary movements for Dalit identity assertion have reinvigorated the struggle for dignity and rights, but the question of whether they advance or diverge from Ambedkar's larger goal of annihilating caste remains critically contested.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Ways identity movements work toward caste annihilation: Visibility and pride counter internalized inferiority; political representation (BSP) translates identity into power; legal activism challenges atrocities; education and economic empowerment break structural barriers
      • Dalit Panthers (1972): Inspired by Black Panthers — radical assertion of Dalit identity as political subject, not just victims; challenged both casteism and class exploitation
      • Limitations of identity approach: Risk of reinforcing caste identities rather than dissolving them; electoral politics often consolidates caste blocs rather than transcending caste; intra-Dalit hierarchies (between sub-castes) may persist
      • Ambedkar's vision: Identity assertion alone insufficient — required conversion (Buddhism as escape from Hindu caste hierarchy), inter-caste marriage, and structural constitutional protections
      • Bhim Army and new movements: Combine identity assertion with direct action against atrocities — bridging Ambedkarite legacy with contemporary mobilization
      • Counter-arguments: Some scholars argue that asserting Dalit identity strategically (as a transitional measure) is a necessary step toward eventual caste dissolution
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While contemporary Dalit identity movements are vital for political empowerment and social dignity, true annihilation of caste requires moving beyond identity politics toward structural transformation of economic opportunity, inter-caste social mixing, and ultimately the dissolution of caste as an organizing social principle.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 — Indian society and caste, GS2 — Constitutional provisions on SC/ST
  🌐 Contemporary: Hathras case, Bhim Army mobilizations, and sub-categorization within SC reservations keep Dalit identity politics in national focus.

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*

  - 📋 **Body:**

  🌐 Contemporary: Targeted gender programmes for SC and ST communities remain essential given their differing vulnerabilities.
