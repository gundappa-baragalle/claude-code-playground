**[Q3346]** [2023]
> Consider the following statements :  1. Statement-I : India, despite having uranium deposits, depends on coal for most of its electricity production. 2. Statement-II: Uranium, enriched to the extent of at least 60%, is required for the production of electricity.  Which one of the following is correct in respect of the above statements?

     A) Both Statement-I and Statement-II are correct and Statement-II is the correct explanation for Statement-I
     B) Both Statement-I and Statement-II are correct and Statement-II is not the correct explanation for Statement-I
  ✅ C) Statement-I is correct but Statement-II is incorrect
     D) Statement-I is incorrect but Statement-II is correct

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement II says uranium must be enriched to 60% for electricity production — this is false; nuclear power reactors use low-enriched uranium (3-5% U-235). 60%+ enrichment is for weapons-grade material.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Statement I is correct — India despite uranium deposits depends on coal for most electricity (coal ~70% of power). Statement II is wrong — nuclear power uses ~3-5% enriched uranium (not 60%). Answer: Statement I correct, II incorrect.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Nuclear power plants use Low-Enriched Uranium (LEU) with 3-5% U-235; weapons require >90% (HEU)
     - India generates ~70% electricity from coal; nuclear contributes only ~3% of total electricity despite significant uranium deposits

**[Q2992]** [2020]
> In India, why are some nuclear reactors kept under "IAEA Safeguards" while others are not?

     A) Some use uranium and others use thorium
  ✅ B) Some use imported uranium and others use domestic supplies
     C) Some are operated by foreign enterprises and others are operated by domestic enterprises
     D) Some are State-owned and others are privately-owned

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Options A and C present plausible-sounding but incorrect rationales; the actual criterion is whether reactors use imported (safeguarded) or domestic (unsafeguarded) uranium fuel.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Under India's nuclear deal framework, reactors using imported uranium are placed under IAEA safeguards as a condition for the import; domestic uranium-fueled reactors remain outside safeguards.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - India-US civil nuclear deal (123 Agreement) led to India separating civilian and military nuclear facilities
     - Reactors using imported uranium must accept IAEA safeguards; those using domestic uranium need not

**[Q2753]** [2018]
> In the Indian context, what is the implication of ratifying the 'Additional Protocol' with the 'International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA)'?

  ✅ A) The civilian nuclear reactors come under IAEA safeguards.
     B) The military nuclear installations come under the inspection of IAEA.
     C) The country will have the privilege to buy uranium from the Nuclear Suppliers Group (NSG).
     D) The country automatically becomes a member of the NSG.

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Option C (NSG membership privilege) and D (automatic NSG membership) are classic traps; Additional Protocol is about IAEA safeguards on declared civilian nuclear facilities.
  💡 **Eliminate:** India's Additional Protocol with IAEA: civilian nuclear reactors/facilities come under IAEA safeguards/inspections. Military nuclear installations remain outside IAEA purview (option B wrong).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - IAEA Additional Protocol: strengthens safeguards — more intrusive inspections of declared civilian nuclear facilities
     - India's civilian nuclear programme under IAEA safeguards post Indo-US Nuclear Deal (2008)
     - Military nuclear installations NOT under IAEA Additional Protocol

**[Q2551]** [2016]
> India is an important member of the International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor'. If this experiment succeeds, what is the immediate advantage for India?

     A) It can use thorium in place of uranium for power generation
     B) It can attain a global role in satellite navigation
     C) It can drastically improve the efficiency of its fission reactors in power generation
  ✅ D) It can build fusion reactors for power generation

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Option A (thorium) is a classic India-specific trap — India has thorium reserves and ITER relates to nuclear, tempting candidates to link the two. But ITER is about fusion, not thorium fission.
  💡 **Eliminate:** ITER is an international nuclear fusion experiment. Success would enable fusion reactors for power generation — the immediate advantage for India. Answer: D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - ITER (International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor) aims to demonstrate commercial fusion power
     - Fusion uses hydrogen isotopes (deuterium/tritium), not uranium/thorium

**[Q4899]** [2012]
> To meet its rapidly growing energy demand, some opine that India should pursue research and development on thorium as the future fuel of nuclear energy. In this context, what advantage does thorium hold over uranium?  1. Thorium is far more abundant in nature than uranium. 2. On the basis of per unit mass of mined mineral, thorium can generate more energy compared to natural uranium. 3. Thorium produces less harmful waste compared to uranium.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 and 3 only
     C) 1 and 3 only
  ✅ D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 requires technical knowledge — thorium needs conversion to fissile U-233 and cannot be directly compared in energy output per unit of mined mineral without nuance.
  💡 **Eliminate:** All three statements are correct about thorium: 4x more abundant than uranium (1), higher theoretical energy potential per unit ore (2), produces less long-lived radioactive waste (3). Answer: D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Thorium 4x more abundant than uranium in Earth's crust
     - India has 25-30% of world's thorium reserves (Monazite sands)

**[Q4327]** [2011]
> The function of heavy water in a nuclear reactor is to

  ✅ A) Slow down the speed of neutrons
     B) Increase the speed of neutrons
     C) Cool down the reactor
     D) Stop the nuclear reaction

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may confuse the function of heavy water with control rods; control rods absorb neutrons to stop the reaction while heavy water (moderator) slows neutrons.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Heavy water acts as a moderator — slows down fast neutrons to thermal speed for sustained chain reaction. Not a coolant per se (though it can be used as coolant too), not a neutron absorber.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Heavy water (D₂O): moderator in CANDU-type and PHWR reactors like India's NPCIL reactors
     - Moderator slows neutrons from fast to thermal speed enabling U-238 fission chain reaction

**[Q479]** [1999]
> Cobalt-60 is commonly used in radiation therapy because it emits

     A) alpha rays.
     B) beta rays.
  ✅ C) gamma rays.
     D) X-rays.

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Beta rays have medical applications in cancer (beta therapy) making them tempting; X-rays are used in cancer imaging/treatment creating confusion with gamma rays from Cobalt-60.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Cobalt-60 is a gamma emitter — gamma rays have high penetrating power needed to reach deep tumors; alpha and beta rays have limited tissue penetration.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Cobalt-60 emits gamma rays (1.17 and 1.33 MeV) used in teletherapy (external beam radiation)
     - Gamma rays can penetrate tissue deeply — essential for treating deep-seated tumors

**[Q1679]** [1995]
> The difference between a nuclear reactor and an atomic bomb is that

     A) no chain reaction takes place in nuclear reactor while in the atomic bomb there is a chain reaction
  ✅ B) the chain reaction in nuclear reactor is controlled
     C) the chain reaction in nuclear reactor is not controlled
     D) no chain reaction takes place in atomic bomb while it takes place in nuclear reactor

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may confuse by thinking both use chain reactions — the key difference is that in a reactor the chain reaction is controlled, while in an atomic bomb it is uncontrolled.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Both nuclear reactor and atomic bomb use fission chain reactions — the crucial difference is control: reactor = controlled (sustained power); bomb = uncontrolled (explosive). Answer: B.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Nuclear reactor uses controlled chain reaction — moderated by control rods to maintain steady power output
     - Atomic bomb triggers supercritical uncontrolled chain reaction releasing energy almost instantaneously

**[Q1684]** [1995]
> ‘Yellow cake’, an item of smuggling across borders is

     A) a crude form of heroin
     B) a crude form of cocaine
  ✅ C) uranium oxide
     D) unrefined gold

  ⚠️ **Trap:** The colloquial term 'yellow cake' and its association with smuggling may lead candidates to guess drugs (heroin/cocaine) rather than uranium oxide.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Yellow cake is uranium oxide (U3O8) — the intermediate product of uranium milling, smuggled because it is a nuclear material precursor. Answer: C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Yellow cake (uranium oxide/U3O8) is the concentrated uranium product from milling uranium ore
     - It is called yellow cake due to its colour and cake-like form; a key step in nuclear fuel cycle

### GS1

**[2013]** Q24. With growing scarcity of fossil fuels, the atomic energy is gaining more and more significance in India. Discuss the availability of raw material required for the generation of atomic energy in India and in the world. (200 words) 10
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Nuclear energy and India's energy security, Uranium and thorium as atomic raw materials, Three-stage nuclear programme

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** As fossil fuels deplete, nuclear energy offers a crucial alternative; India's raw material availability — especially thorium — positions it uniquely for long-term atomic energy self-sufficiency.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • World uranium: Kazakhstan, Canada, Australia are top producers; India has limited uranium in Jharkhand, Andhra Pradesh
      • India's thorium: Monazite sands in Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Odisha — world's largest reserves (~25%) in beach sands
      • Three-stage programme: Stage 1 (PHWRs using natural uranium), Stage 2 (FBRs generating plutonium), Stage 3 (Thorium-U233 cycle)
      • Current status: Stage 1 operational; Stage 2 Prototype Fast Breeder Reactor at Kalpakkam near completion; Stage 3 future
      • Indo-US nuclear deal (2008): Enabled import of uranium for existing reactors, freeing domestic uranium for weapons
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's massive thorium reserves make it a potential global leader in next-generation nuclear energy, but realizing this requires completing the three-stage programme and mastering closed fuel cycle technology.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 Energy security, GS3 Science and Technology, India's nuclear doctrine
  🌐 Contemporary: India's push for 100 GW nuclear capacity by 2047 depends critically on completing Stage 2-3 of the atomic programme.

      • India's contributions: cryostat vessel, in-wall shielding blocks, cooling water system, cryolines — manufactured by ITER-India under DAE

  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Energy Security, GS2 – International Cooperation, GS3 – Science and Technology

**[2018]** Q16. With growing energy needs should India keep on expanding its nuclear energy programme? Discuss the facts and fears associated with nuclear energy. (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: VS | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Nuclear energy debate, Energy security, Technological risk assessment

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's growing energy needs necessitate a balanced evaluation of nuclear energy's potential alongside the legitimate concerns about its safety and waste management.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Facts in favour: low lifecycle carbon emissions, base-load reliability, energy density high, India's thorium reserves
      • India-US Civil Nuclear Deal (2008): enabled nuclear fuel imports; expanded programme
      • Fears: Chernobyl/Fukushima accidents; radioactive waste disposal; proliferation risks
      • India's safety record: no major incidents at DAE plants; AERB oversight
      • PHWR advantage: uses natural uranium; less enrichment dependency
      • Way forward: expand nuclear with enhanced safety standards; fast-track FBR; prioritize coastal sites
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Given India's energy deficit and climate commitments, nuclear expansion with robust safety frameworks and waste management is a strategic imperative.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Science & Technology, GS3 – Energy, GS2 – International Relations
  🌐 Contemporary: Global nuclear renaissance and India's clean energy targets make nuclear debate highly relevant.

**[2017]** Q16. Give an account of the growth and development of nuclear science and technology in India. What is the advantage of fast breeder reactor programme in India? (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Nuclear energy development, Energy security, Scientific capability

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's nuclear science programme has evolved through a strategic three-stage plan aimed at harnessing its vast thorium reserves for long-term energy security.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Growth: Apsara (1956, Asia's first research reactor) → Bhabha's 3-stage plan → Pokhran tests → India-US Nuclear Deal 2008
      • Current capacity: ~7,480 MW nuclear power from 22 operational reactors; target 22,480 MW by 2031
      • Fast Breeder Reactor advantage: breeds more fissile material (Pu-239) than it consumes; utilizes Stage 1 waste as fuel
      • Closed fuel cycle: FBR enables reprocessing and recycling, reducing nuclear waste
      • Thorium road: Stage 3 will use India's 5.32 lakh tonne thorium reserves — energy independence
      • PFBR Kalpakkam: 500 MW FBR; when operational, enables Stage 2 transition
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's FBR programme is central to its long-term energy security strategy, converting thorium abundance into nuclear fuel self-sufficiency.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Science & Technology, GS3 – Energy Security, GS2 – International Relations
  🌐 Contemporary: India's net-zero 2070 pledge and clean energy transition make nuclear expansion critical.
