**[Q2045]** [2018]
> With reference to the Indian Regional Navigation Satellite System (IRNSS), consider the following statements :  1. IRNSS has three satellites in geostationary and four satellites in geosynchronous orbits. 2. IRNSS covers entire India and about 5500 sq. km beyond its borders. 3. India will have its own satellite navigation system with full global coverage by the middle of 2019.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

  ✅ A) 1 only
     B) 1 and 2 only
     C) 2 and 3 only
     D) None

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 says IRNSS covers 5500 sq km beyond borders — the correct figure is ~1500 km radius beyond India's borders (a vast area, not sq km). Statement 3 about global coverage by 2019 is incorrect.
  💡 **Eliminate:** IRNSS (NavIC) has 3 geostationary + 4 geosynchronous = 7 satellites. Statement 1 is correct. Statements 2 and 3 have factual errors. Answer is A (1 only).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - IRNSS/NavIC: 3 geostationary + 4 geosynchronous orbit satellites = 7 total
     - NavIC covers India and ~1500 km beyond borders; not designed for global coverage

**[Q2803]** [2018]
> With reference to India's satellite launch vehicles, consider the following statements:  1. PSLVs launch the satellites useful for Earth resources monitoring whereas GSLVs are designed mainly to launch communication satellites. 2. Satellites launched by PSLV appear to remain permanently fixed in the same position in the sky, as viewed from a particular location on Earth. 3. GSLV Mk III is a four-staged launch vehicle with the first and third stages using solid rocket motors; and the second and fourth stages using liquid rocket engines.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

  ✅ A) 1 only
     B) 2 and 3
     C) 1 and 2
     D) 3 only

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 falsely implies PSLV places satellites in geostationary orbit (fixed position) — PSLV primarily uses sun-synchronous/polar orbit; statement 3 wrongly describes GSLV Mk III as four-staged.
  💡 **Eliminate:** PSLV launches Earth observation satellites; GSLV launches communication satellites (statement 1 correct); PSLV-launched satellites are NOT geostationary — they orbit, not 'fixed' (statement 2 wrong); GSLV Mk III is a 3-stage vehicle, not 4-stage (statement 3 wrong) — answer A (1 only).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - PSLV (Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle) places satellites in low Earth or sun-synchronous orbit for Earth observation
     - GSLV Mk III (now LVM3) is a 3-stage vehicle: solid boosters + liquid stage + cryogenic stage

**[Q2562]** [2016]
> Consider the following statements: The Mangalyaan launched by ISRO  1. is also called the Mars Orbiter Mission 2. made India the second country to have a spacecraft orbit the Mars after USA 3. made India the only country to be successful in making its spacecraft orbit the Mars in its very first attempt  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 and 3 only
  ✅ C) 1 and 3 only
     D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 is the trap — candidates may think India was second after USA, forgetting Soviet Union/Russia also reached Mars. Statement 1 (also called MOM) is correct.
  💡 **Eliminate:** MOM = Mars Orbiter Mission (S1 correct). India was the 4th entity (after USSR, USA, ESA) but first Asian nation, and FIRST to succeed in maiden attempt (S3 correct). S2 is wrong. Answer: C (1 and 3 only).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - MOM/Mangalyaan launched Nov 2013, entered Mars orbit Sept 2014
     - India is the only country to succeed in Mars orbit insertion on its very first attempt

**[Q2303]** [2009]
> India has recently landed its Moon Impact Probe on the Moon. Among the following countries, which one landed such probe on the Moon earlier ?

     A) Australia
     B) Canada
     C) China
  ✅ D) Japan

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may guess China as the country that recently (2007-08) launched a moon impact probe — Japan launched SELENE/Kaguya in 2007 which impacted the Moon; China's Chang'e-1 also impacted in 2009 but Japan was earlier.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Chandrayaan-1's Moon Impact Probe landed Nov 2008. Japan's SELENE (Kaguya) impacted the Moon in June 2009 — but Japan launched SELENE in September 2007 and was the most recent probe before India's MIP. Answer is D (Japan).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - India's Chandrayaan-1 carried Moon Impact Probe (MIP) — November 2008
     - Japan's SELENE/Kaguya spacecraft was in lunar orbit from 2007
     - China's Chang'e-1 also orbited/impacted Moon (2007-09)

**[Q1073]** [2002]
> With reference to Indian satellites and their launchers, consider the following statements: 1. All the INSAT-series of satellites were launched abroad 2. PSLVs were used to launch IRS-series of satellites 3. India used the indigenously built cryogenic engines for the first time for powering the third stage of GSLV 4. GSAT, launched in the year 2001, has payloads to demonstrate digital broadcasts and internet services Which of these statements are correct?

     A) 1, 2, 3 and 4
     B) 2, 3 and 4
  ✅ C) 1, 2 and 4
     D) 1 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 1 (all INSAT satellites launched abroad) is incorrect — some INSAT satellites were launched by ISRO's GSLV. Statement 3 about indigenously built cryogenic engines in GSLV is also wrong — India used Russian cryogenic engines initially.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Statements 2 (PSLV launched IRS satellites) and 4 (GSAT 2001 with digital broadcast payloads) are correct. Statements 1 and 3 are wrong. Answer is C (1, 2, and 4).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - PSLV: workhorse for IRS satellites
     - India's indigenous cryogenic engine (CE-7.5) tested much later
     - GSAT-1 launched 2001 by GSLV-D1

### GS1

**[2018]** Q4. Why is Indian Regional Navigational Satellite System (IRNSS) needed? How does it help in navigation? (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Strategic autonomy, Space technology applications, Navigation security

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's IRNSS (NavIC) represents a strategic assertion of technological sovereignty in navigation, reducing dependence on foreign GPS systems.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Need: During Kargil War (1999), US denied precise GPS data — strategic vulnerability exposed
      • System: 7 satellites (3 GEO + 4 GSO) covering India + 1500 km maritime boundary
      • Applications: military navigation, disaster management, vehicle tracking, fisheries (coastal alerts)
      • Advantages: encrypted military-grade signals, India-controlled, independent of foreign approval
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** NavIC epitomizes India's dual-use space strategy — civilian utility combined with strategic military independence.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 Science & Technology, GS3 Internal Security — strategic autonomy, IR — India's space diplomacy
  🌐 Contemporary: NavIC chips are now mandated in mobile phones sold in India (from 2023), expanding civilian use.

### GS3

**[2023]** Q16. What is the main task of India's third moon mission which could not be achieved in its earlier mission? List the countries that have achieved this task. Introduce the sub-systems in the spacecraft launched and explain the role of the 'Virtual Launch Control Centre' at the Vikram Sarabhai Space Centre which contributed to the successful launch from Sriharikota. (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: India's lunar exploration programme, Space technology capabilities

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Chandrayaan-3's historic soft landing on the lunar south pole marked India's emergence as a leading space power, demonstrating indigenous technological capability.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Main task unachieved earlier: soft landing on the Moon (Chandrayaan-2 Vikram lander crashed in 2019)
      • Countries that achieved lunar soft landing: USA, USSR/Russia, China — India became 4th
      • Sub-systems: Propulsion Module, Lander Module (Vikram), Rover (Pragyan)
      • VLCC at VSSC, Thiruvananthapuram: enabled remote command and control, reducing dependency on single control centre
      • Mission improvements over Chandrayaan-2: stronger legs, multiple landing sensors, larger landing zone
      • Scientific significance of lunar south pole: water ice deposits, permanent shadow regions
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Chandrayaan-3 not only redeemed India's lunar ambitions but also demonstrated ISRO's capacity for learning from failure and achieving technological excellence.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 S&T — Space, GS2 IR — International Organizations (Outer Space Treaty), Economy — Space economy
  🌐 Contemporary: Chandrayaan-3's success has propelled India into the global space race and opened avenues for international collaboration in lunar exploration.

**[2019]** Q6. What is India's plan to have its own space station and how will it benefit our space programme? (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: India's space ambitions, Strategic autonomy in space, Long-term space programme

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's plan for an indigenous space station represents a major strategic leap toward space autonomy and scientific capability development.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Plan: 20-tonne Bharatiya Antriksh Station by 2035; expandable modular design
      • Gaganyaan as precursor: human spaceflight capability demonstrated; crew module technology developed
      • Benefits: long-duration microgravity research (materials science, biology, medicine)
      • Strategic: reduces dependence on ISS; strategic deterrence in space; technology demonstration platform
      • Scientific: Earth observation, astronomy, space weather monitoring from vantage point
      • Spinoffs: life support technology, robotics, reusable launch vehicles advancement
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's own space station will not only advance scientific capabilities but also cement its position as a major space power with full end-to-end human spaceflight capability.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Science & Technology, GS2 – International Relations, GS3 – Infrastructure
  🌐 Contemporary: Gaganyaan test flights and Bharatiya Antriksh Station announcement make India's space plans actively relevant.

**[2017]** Q7. India has achieved remarkable successes in unmanned space missions including the Chandrayaan and Mars Orbitter Mission, but has not ventured into manned space missions. What are the main obstacles to launching a manned space mission, both in terms of technology and logistics? Examine critically. (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Manned space mission obstacles, India's space technology gaps, Strategic importance of human spaceflight

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Despite India's impressive unmanned space achievements, the leap to manned missions demands mastering qualitatively different technologies and logistical systems that remain works in progress.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Technology gaps: reliable life support systems, crew escape modules, and reentry heat shields are still being developed
      • Human-rated launch vehicle: GSLV-Mk III needs human rating certification for safety margins
      • Astronaut training: requires long-duration microgravity adaptation, EVA training and psychological conditioning
      • Cost: manned missions exponentially costlier than unmanned; require crew recovery infrastructure
      • Logistics: medical support, mission control upgrades, international coordination for rescue
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Successful manned spaceflight demands India close its technology gaps in human-rated systems while building international partnerships for crew safety and mission assurance.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Science and Technology, Defence, Economy
  🌐 Contemporary: Gaganyaan mission is scheduled for launch with Indian astronaut after 2024 uncrewed test flights.

**[2015]** Q11. What do you understand by 'Standard Positioning Systems' and 'Precision Positioning Systems' in the GPS era? Discuss the advantages India perceives from its ambitious IRNSS programme employing just seven satellites. (200 words) 12.5
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Space technology, Strategic autonomy, Navigation systems

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's IRNSS programme represents a dual-use navigation system with Standard Positioning Service for civilians and Precision Positioning Service for military applications.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • SPS: open to civilians, accuracy ~20m, used in commercial apps, vehicle tracking, aviation
      • PPS: encrypted, restricted to authorized users (military), accuracy ~<1m
      • IRNSS advantage: India controls the system — unlike GPS (US-controlled), Kargil lesson applied
      • 7 satellites: 3 GEO + 4 GSO — provides continuous coverage over Indian subcontinent
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** IRNSS/NavIC ensures India's strategic independence in navigation while enabling diverse civilian applications from fisheries safety to disaster management.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 Science & Technology, GS3 Security, GS1 Geography — satellite geography
  🌐 Contemporary: NavIC integration in smartphones (2023 mandate) and role in border surveillance make it increasingly strategic.
