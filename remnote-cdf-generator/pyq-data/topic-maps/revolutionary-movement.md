**[Q3071]** [2021]
> In the context of Colonial India, Shah Nawaz Khan, Prem Kumar Sehgal and Gurbaksh Singh Dhillon are remembered as

     A) leaders of Swadeshi and Boycott Movement
     B) members of the Interim Government in 1946
     C) members of the Drafting Committee in the Constituent Assembly
  ✅ D) officers of the Indian National Army

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Shah Nawaz Khan's INA association may be confused with his later political role in the Interim Government by some students.
  💡 **Eliminate:** All three — Shah Nawaz Khan, Prem Kumar Sehgal, Gurbaksh Singh Dhillon — were INA officers tried in the famous Red Fort INA Trials (1945-46); this is a textbook fact.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - INA Trials (1945-46) at Red Fort involved Shah Nawaz Khan, Sehgal, and Dhillon
     - Their trial became a nationalist rallying cause; Bhulabhai Desai defended them

**[Q2237]** [2014]
> The Ghadr (Ghadar) was a

  ✅ A) revolutionary association of Indians with headquarters at San Francisco
     B) nationalist organization operating from Singapore
     C) militant organization with headquarters at Berlin
     D) communist movement for India's freedom with headquarters at Tashkent

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may confuse Ghadar with the Berlin India Committee or Home Rule League — Ghadar was specifically a San Francisco-based revolutionary movement for Indian independence.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Ghadar Party was founded in 1913 by Lala Har Dayal and others in San Francisco, USA — a revolutionary overseas Indian movement targeting British rule.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Ghadar Party was founded in 1913 at San Francisco by Lala Har Dayal
     - It published the revolutionary newspaper 'Ghadar' (Revolution)
     - It sought to incite mutiny in the Indian Army during WWI

### GS1

**[2014]** Q7. In what ways did the naval mutiny prove to be the last nail in the coffin of British colonial aspirations in India? (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Naval mutiny 1946, British withdrawal, End of colonial rule

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The Royal Indian Navy Mutiny of February 1946 shattered the myth of the imperial military's unshakeable loyalty — confronting Britain with the prospect of losing its armed coercive force in India and accelerating the decision to transfer power.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Scale: 20,000 ratings, 78 ships, shore establishments from Karachi to Calcutta; spontaneous uprising triggered by racial discrimination and poor conditions
      • Political impact: mutiny coincided with INA trials' public sympathy — demonstrated nationalist sentiment had penetrated the military
      • British reassessment: PM Attlee later acknowledged mutiny among factors prompting 1947 decision; loss of military loyalty made continued colonial rule untenable
      • Unity symbol: Hindu and Muslim sailors fought together, waving Congress and Muslim League flags simultaneously — briefly transcending communal divisions
      • Last nail in coffin: combined with INA trials, 1946 provincial elections showing Congress strength, and global decolonization pressures — mutiny completed the picture of imperial decline
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The naval mutiny demonstrated that colonial rule ultimately rests on the consent of the governed, including those in uniform — when that consent fractures, imperial power must negotiate or collapse.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 Modern India, Internal Security, World History
  🌐 Contemporary: The mutiny's legacy informs debates on military professionalism, civil-military relations, and the ethics of political consciousness in armed forces.
