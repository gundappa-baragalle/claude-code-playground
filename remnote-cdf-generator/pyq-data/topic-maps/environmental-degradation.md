**[Q3208]** [2022]

**[Q2707]** [2018]
> The term "sixth mass extinction/sixth extinction" is often mentioned in the news in the context of the discussion of

     A) Widespread monoculture practices in agriculture and large-scale commercial farming with indiscriminate use of chemicals in many parts of the world that may result in the loss of good native ecosystems.
     B) Fears of a possible collision of a meteorite with the Earth in the near future in the manner it happened 65 million years ago that caused the mass extinction of many species including those of dinosaurs.
     C) Large scale cultivation of genetically modified crops in many parts of the world and promoting their cultivation in other parts of the world which may cause the disappearance of good native crop plants and the loss of food biodiversity.
  ✅ D) Mankind's over-exploitation/misuse of natural resources, fragmentation/loss of natural habitats, destruction of ecosystems, pollution and global climate change.

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Options A and C (monoculture, GM crops) sound like specific environmental threats and students may pick them; the sixth mass extinction is broadly caused by human activities across multiple fronts.
  💡 **Eliminate:** The sixth mass extinction (Anthropocene extinction) is driven by habitat loss, overexploitation, pollution, invasive species, and climate change together — option D is the comprehensive correct answer.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Sixth mass extinction: ongoing loss of species at 100-1000x natural background rate
     - Main drivers: habitat destruction, overexploitation, pollution, climate change, invasive species
     - First five mass extinctions: geological/astronomical causes; sixth is human-caused (Anthropocene)

**[Q2730]** [2018]
> Which of the following has/have shrunk immensely/dried up in the recent past due to human activities?  1. Aral Sea 2. Black Sea 3. Lake Baikal  Select the correct answer using the code given below:

  ✅ A) 1 only
     B) 2 and 3
     C) 2 only
     D) 1 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Lake Baikal (world's deepest lake) seems like it should be under threat; it faces pollution and local environmental pressures but has NOT 'dried up/shrunk immensely'. Black Sea is a sea not a lake.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Aral Sea has drastically shrunk due to Soviet-era irrigation diversions — one of history's greatest ecological disasters. Black Sea and Lake Baikal have not shrunk immensely. Answer: 1 only.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Aral Sea: once world's 4th largest lake, now <10% of original size due to Amu Darya/Syr Darya diversion
     - Shrinkage began 1960s when Soviet irrigation projects diverted inflows for cotton
     - Baikal and Black Sea face pollution challenges but not comparable shrinkage

**[Q2794]** [2018]
> Which of the following is/are the possible consequence/s of heavy sand mining in riverbeds?  1. Decreased salinity in the river 2. Pollution of groundwater 3. Lowering of the water-table  Select the correct answer using the code given below:

     A) 1 only
  ✅ B) 2 and 3 only
     C) 1 and 3 only
     D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 1 (decreased salinity) is counterintuitive — heavy sand mining actually increases salinity intrusion in rivers near coasts; students may mark it correct thinking sand holds salt.

  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Sand mining lowers riverbed levels, causing water table to drop and increasing saltwater intrusion in coastal rivers
     - Illegal sand mining is one of the most widespread environmental crimes in India, depleting riverbeds

**[2021]** Q6. What are the environmental implications of the reclamation of water bodies into urban land use? Explain with examples. (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Urban encroachment on water bodies, Environmental implications, Urban ecology

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The reclamation of water bodies for urban land use represents a critical environmental challenge, disrupting hydrological cycles and urban ecosystems as cities expand rapidly.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Loss of groundwater recharge: water bodies serve as recharge zones; their reclamation reduces aquifer replenishment (example: Bengaluru's lakes)
      • Increased flood risk: natural retention capacity lost — extreme rainfall causes urban flooding (Chennai 2015, Hyderabad 2020)
      • Biodiversity loss: lakes and wetlands support migratory birds, aquatic species — their destruction eliminates ecological habitats
      • Urban heat island effect: water bodies moderate temperature; their loss intensifies urban heat
      • Social costs: communities dependent on water bodies for livelihoods (fishing, washing) lose sustenance
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Integrating water body conservation into urban master plans through regulatory enforcement and community participation is essential for ecologically sustainable urbanization.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Drainage and water bodies, GS3: Environment, GS2: Urban local bodies
  🌐 Contemporary: Urban flooding in Indian cities and Supreme Court orders on lake restoration make this highly relevant.

  **Answer Framework:**

  - 🔑 **Intro:** The stunning landscapes of Jammu & Kashmir, Himachal Pradesh, and Uttarakhand attract millions of tourists annually, but unregulated tourism is increasingly pushing these fragile Himalayan ecosystems toward their ecological limits.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Evidence of stress: Solid waste accumulation on trekking routes (Rohtang, Kedarnath); groundwater depletion near hill stations; deforestation for hotel construction; traffic congestion causing air/noise pollution

  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The Himalayan states urgently need legally enforceable carrying capacity assessments and transition to sustainable tourism models that balance economic development with the irreplaceable ecological heritage these mountains represent.

  🏷️ Themes: Mining and environment, Sustainable resource extraction, Regulatory frameworks

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Mining, while fundamental to industrial and economic development, is one of the most environmentally disruptive activities necessitating stringent regulatory and remedial measures.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Environmental hazards: deforestation and habitat destruction, soil erosion and land degradation, water contamination (acid mine drainage), air pollution (dust and gases), displacement of communities

  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Transitioning to sustainable mining through stringent EIA enforcement, community benefit sharing via DMF, and mandatory ecological restoration can reconcile resource needs with environmental integrity.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: Governance and regulatory bodies, GS3: Industry and infrastructure, GS3: Conservation of natural resources

**[2025]** Q13. Examine the factors responsible for depleting groundwater in India. What are the steps taken by the government to mitigate such depletion of groundwater? (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Groundwater crisis, Agricultural water policy, Water governance

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's groundwater crisis stems from structural over-extraction driven by subsidized power for irrigation, threatening long-term agricultural and drinking water security.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Factors: subsidized electricity enabling free-running borewells (Punjab, Haryana), paddy cultivation in water-scarce areas, lack of volumetric pricing
      • Industrial extraction: quarrying, mining, and industrial use deplete shallow aquifers
      • Urbanization: impervious surfaces reduce natural recharge; sewage contamination of aquifers
      • Over-irrigation: rice cultivation in northwestern plains is primary driver
      • Government steps: Atal Bhujal Yojana (participatory groundwater management), NAQUIM (aquifer mapping), Jal Shakti Abhiyan
      • Regulatory: Model Groundwater (Sustainable Management) Act 2016; Aquifer Management Plans; pricing reform
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Reversing groundwater depletion requires a combination of demand management through pricing, supply augmentation through recharge, and regulatory enforcement.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Environment, GS3 – Agriculture, GS2 – Governance
  🌐 Contemporary: GRACE satellite data showing India's groundwater crisis and Atal Bhujal Yojana implementation make this urgently relevant.

**[2013]** Q19. What are the consequences of Illegal mining ? Discuss the Ministry of Environment and Forests' concept of GO AND NO GO zones for coal mining sector. [200 words] 10
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Consequences of illegal mining, Go and No-Go zone concept, Balancing development and environment

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Illegal mining extracts natural resources outside legal and environmental frameworks, causing irreversible ecological damage while the No-Go zone concept attempts to draw inviolate boundaries for conservation.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Consequences of illegal mining: environmental — deforestation, soil erosion, water table depletion, air/water pollution; social — displacement of communities, loss of livelihoods; economic — revenue loss to state (illegal sand mining alone causes thousands of crore losses); safety — unregulated mines collapse (e.g., Meghalaya coal mines)
      • Go zones: areas where coal exists but forest cover is ≤10%; mining permitted with environmental safeguards and clearances
      • No-Go zones (now called inviolate areas): forest cover >10% over coal-bearing areas; mining prohibited; originally proposed by MoEF to balance environment and mining
      • Controversy: coal ministry objected as significant reserves lie in No-Go zones; compromise approach — ecological sensitivity criteria for classification
      • Current status: inviolate area concept evolved; Supreme Court and NGT actively monitor compliance
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The Go/No-Go framework represents a pragmatic attempt to reconcile India's energy security with ecological protection, but effective enforcement remains the key challenge.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 — Environment, minerals, energy security, GS2 — Governance, regulatory bodies
  🌐 Contemporary: Coal mine expansion vs forest conservation remains a live debate in India's energy transition.

- **Question types (Prelims):** Factual: 7, Conceptual: 4
- **Recent frequency (2020–2025):** 10 questions

### Common Trap Patterns on This Topic
- Students confuse 50 or 150 years with 100 years; Meadows' 1972 'Limits to Growth' report is a specific reference.
- Shifting cultivation (jhum) may seem eco-friendly as it's a traditional practice; high-yielding varieties might seem sustainable due to higher productivity per acre.
- Candidates may think salinization raises water table (common misconception) or fills air spaces — the key effect is making soils impermeable by clogging pores with salt crystals.
- Students unfamiliar with virtual water may be tempted by option A (heavy water) or C (rainwater harvesting) — both plausible-sounding distractors.
- Terrace cultivation actually PREVENTS soil erosion (it is a conservation measure); students may select it as a cause of erosion confusing it with cutting of slopes.
