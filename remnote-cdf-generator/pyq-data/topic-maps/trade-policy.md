**[Q4969]** [2017]
> The term 'Domestic Content Requirement' is sometimes seen in the news with reference to

  ✅ A) Developing solar power production in our country
     B) Granting licences to foreign T.V. channels in our country
     C) Exporting our food products to other countries
     D) Permitting foreign educational institutions to set up their campuses in our country

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Domestic Content Requirement sounds like it could apply to any sector with local content rules including TV licensing or education; the solar power context (Jawaharlal Nehru National Solar Mission DCR dispute) is the specific news context.
  💡 **Eliminate:** India's DCR under the Jawaharlal Nehru National Solar Mission required use of domestically made solar panels/cells — WTO dispute arose over this; answer is A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - India's solar DCR policy led to WTO dispute with USA in 2016
     - DCR mandates a certain percentage of components to be sourced locally

**[Q2483]** [2016]
> In the context of which of the following do you sometimes find the terms 'amber box, blue box and green box' in the news?

  ✅ A) WTO affairs
     B) SAARC affairs
     C) UNFCCC affairs
     D) India-EU negotiations on FTA

  ⚠️ **Trap:** UNFCCC uses similar color-based classification for emissions commitments, making option C tempting; the box system is specific to WTO agricultural subsidy categorization.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Amber, Blue and Green boxes are WTO terminology for domestic agricultural support categories under the Agreement on Agriculture — answer is A (WTO affairs).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Green Box: subsidies with minimal trade distortion (R&D, food security stocks); Amber Box: trade-distorting subsidies; Blue Box: production-limiting programs
     - India's food subsidy classification in WTO is a recurring trade dispute

**[Q2533]** [2016]
> The term 'Base Erosion and Profit Shifting' is sometimes seen in the news in the context of

     A) mining operation by multinational companies in resource-rich but backward areas
  ✅ B) curbing of the tax evasion by multinational companies
     C) exploitation of genetic resources of a country by multinational companies
     D) lack of consideration of environmental costs in the planning and implementation of developmental projects

  ⚠️ **Trap:** BEPS could be associated with mining erosion or environmental cost shifting; the 'profit shifting' component should clarify it is about corporate tax avoidance.
  💡 **Eliminate:** BEPS (Base Erosion and Profit Shifting) refers to MNC tax avoidance strategies that shift profits to low-tax jurisdictions — answer B (curbing tax evasion by MNCs).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - BEPS is an OECD/G20 initiative to prevent MNCs from using tax planning to avoid taxes in countries where they generate economic value
     - India is an active participant in BEPS project and has implemented several BEPS actions

**[Q1577]** [2011]
> In terms of economy, the visit by foreign nationals to witness the XIX Common Wealth Games in India amounted to

  ✅ A) Export
     B) Import
     C) Production
     D) Consumption

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Option D (consumption) is tempting since foreign visitors consuming Indian services creates economic activity; however, in national accounting, foreigners paying for Indian services constitutes export of services — an invisible export.
  💡 **Eliminate:** When foreigners visit India and spend money on Indian services (hotels, transport, tourism), India earns foreign exchange — this is classified as export of services (invisible export) in BOP accounts. Answer: A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Tourism receipts are classified as 'export of services' in the current account of Balance of Payments
     - India's tourism receipts constitute a significant invisible export earning foreign exchange

**[Q4323]** [2011]
> A "closed economy" is an economy in which

     A) the money supply is fully controlled
     B) deficit financing takes place
     C) only exports take place
  ✅ D) neither exports nor imports take place

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may confuse 'closed economy' with planned economy (money supply controlled) or with autarky; the defining feature is no exports AND no imports.
  💡 **Eliminate:** A closed economy has no international trade — neither exports nor imports. Deficit financing and money supply control are features of different economic categories, not closed economies.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Closed economy = autarky: no international trade
     - Example: North Korea; contrast with open economy like India post-1991

**[Q1072]** [2002]
> A country is said to be in debt trap if

     A) it has to abide by the conditionalities imposed by the International Monetary Fund
  ✅ B) it has to borrow to make interest payments to on outstanding loans
     C) it has been refused loans or aid by creditors abroad
     D) the World Bank charges a very high rate of interest on outstanding as well as new loans

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may confuse debt trap with conditionality (IMF conditions) or credit denial — the technical definition specifically involves borrowing to pay interest.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Debt trap = borrowing new loans to pay interest on existing loans, creating a spiral. This is Option B — a precise economic definition.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Debt trap: when interest payments > revenue growth, requiring new borrowing to service old debt
     - Sri Lanka 2022 crisis: classic debt trap scenario

**[Q946]** [2000]
> Assertion (A) : The rate of growth of India’s exports has shown an appreciable increase after 1991. Reason (R) : The Govt. of India has resorted to devaluation.

  ✅ A) Both A and R are true, and R is the correct explanation of A
     B) Both A and R are true, but R is not a correct explanation of A
     C) A is true, but R is false
     D) A is false, but R is true

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Both A (export growth after 1991) and R (devaluation) are true and seem related; students may hesitate thinking devaluation alone doesn't explain sustained export growth — but it was the primary catalyst.
  💡 **Eliminate:** A is true; R is true (rupee was devalued in 1991 by ~18%); devaluation directly makes exports competitive — R correctly explains A; select option A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - 1991 rupee devaluation: ~18% devaluation made Indian exports more competitive globally
     - Post-1991 liberalization combined with devaluation drove export growth in services and manufactured goods

**[Q280]** [1996]
> Assertion (A) : An important policy instrument of economic liberalization is reduction in import duties on capital goods. Reason (R) : Reduction in import duties would help the local entrepreneurs to improve technology to face the global markets. In the context of the above two statements, which one of the following is correct ?

  ✅ A) Both A and R are true and R is the correct explanation of A
     B) Both A and R are true and R is not a correct explanation of A
     C) A is true but R is false
     D) A is false but R is true

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Both A and R are true and R correctly explains A — reduction in import duties on capital goods is indeed an economic liberalization policy instrument, and the reason (improving technology for global competitiveness) correctly explains it.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Both statements are independently true AND R is the correct explanation for A — reducing duties on capital goods was a key instrument in 1991 reforms precisely to enable technology upgradation for export competitiveness. Correct: A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - 1991 reforms — customs duty reduction on capital goods to enable technology import
     - Reasoning: domestic industry needs modern technology to compete globally after trade liberalization

**[Q701]** [1996]
> Hawala transactions relate to payments

  ✅ A) received in rupees against overseas currencies and vice versa without going through the official channels
     B) received for sale/transfer of shares without going through the established stock exchanges
     C) received as commission for services rendered to overseas investors/buyers/ sellers in assisting them to get over the red tape and/or in getting preferential treatment
     D) made to political parties or to individuals for meeting election expenses

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may confuse Hawala with stock market manipulation or commission payments; the defining feature of Hawala is informal cross-border money transfer bypassing official banking channels.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Hawala = informal value transfer system where money is received in one country's currency and paid in another's without physical money crossing borders or going through official banking. Answer: A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Hawala is an informal remittance system based on trust and a network of brokers (hawaladars)
     - Hawala transactions bypass official banking channels and currency controls — illegal in India

**[2025]** Q2. What are the challenges before the Indian economy when the world is moving away from free trade and multilateralism to protectionism and bilateralism? How can these challenges be met? (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Deglobalization and India's trade strategy, Protectionism impact on emerging economies, India's FTA strategy in bipolar world

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The global shift from free trade to protectionism poses both structural challenges and strategic opportunities for India's economic integration.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Challenges: Export market uncertainty, supply chain disruption, WTO dispute settlement weakening
      • Opportunity: China+1 strategy enabling India to attract manufacturing investment
      • India's FTA push (UAE, Australia, UK negotiations) as bilateral hedge against multilateral collapse
      • Need to upgrade domestic manufacturing competitiveness, reduce logistics costs
      • Strategic autonomy vs integration dilemma — India's cautious trade policy stance
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India must pragmatically combine selective bilateral agreements, domestic industrial competitiveness, and strategic supply chain positioning to navigate the post-globalization trade landscape.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: India's foreign policy, GS1: Economic geography, Internal Security: supply chain security
  🌐 Contemporary: Highly relevant given US tariff actions in 2025 and India's FTA negotiations.

**[2018]** Q12. How would the recent phenomena of protectionism and currency manipulations in world trade affect macroeconomic stability of India? (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: IMPACT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Global trade tensions and India, Macroeconomic stability, Protectionism and export competitiveness

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Rising global protectionism and deliberate currency manipulation by major economies pose significant threats to India's macroeconomic stability through trade channel disruptions.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Protectionism: Higher tariffs/NTBs in US, EU reduce demand for Indian exports (IT, textiles, pharma) — compresses export earnings, widens current account deficit
      • Currency manipulation: When trading partners artificially depress their currencies, Indian exports become uncompetitive; Indian firms face cheaper imports undermining domestic industry
      • Capital flows: Trade uncertainty reduces FDI and FPI inflows; currency volatility makes rupee vulnerable
      • Macroeconomic consequences: widening CAD, inflationary pressure through import cost rise, RBI forced to intervene depleting forex reserves
      • India's response levers: WTO dispute resolution, bilateral agreements, promoting domestic manufacturing (Make in India), diversifying export destinations
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India must build export resilience through product and market diversification while engaging multilaterally to preserve rule-based trade order.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: International organisations (WTO), GS3: Indian economy and external sector, IR: India-US/China trade relations
  🌐 Contemporary: US tariffs under Trump 2.0 era and China's currency management directly threaten India's macroeconomic stability.

**[2013]** Q7. Examine the impact of liberalization on companies owned by Indians. Are they competing with the MNCs satisfactorily ? Discuss. [200 words] 10
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Impact of liberalization on Indian companies, Competitive response to MNCs, Sectoral success and failure

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The 1991 liberalization opened India's economy to global competition, fundamentally transforming Indian companies from protected domestic players to aspiring global competitors.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Initial impact: Indian companies faced pricing pressure, quality competition, and market share loss to MNCs in consumer goods, FMCG, auto sectors
      • Success stories: IT sector (TCS, Infosys, Wipro — world leaders), pharma (Sun Pharma, Cipla — generic drug exports), auto (Tata Motors — Jaguar Land Rover acquisition)
      • Areas of challenge: manufacturing still trails MNCs in technology intensity; retail, aviation sectors struggle vs global competition
      • Adaptation strategies: joint ventures, technology tie-ups, acquisitions abroad (Tata's Corus, Mittal Steel), quality upgradation
      • Overall assessment: Indian companies compete satisfactorily in services and selected manufacturing; hardware, semiconductors, defence manufacturing still lag
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Liberalization has broadly strengthened Indian companies through competitive pressure, but closing the gap with MNCs in technology-intensive manufacturing requires sustained policy support.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 — Economy, external sector, GS2 — Governance, industrial policy
  🌐 Contemporary: China-plus-one strategy and PLI scheme offer new opportunities for Indian manufacturers to compete globally.
