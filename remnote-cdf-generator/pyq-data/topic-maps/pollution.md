**[Q3657]** [2025]
> Consider the following statements :  Statement I : Activated carbon is a good and an attractive tool to remove pollutants from effluent streams and to remediate contaminants from various industries. Statement II : Activated carbon exhibits a large surface area and a strong potential for adsorbing heavy metals. Statement III : Activated carbon can be easily synthesized from environmental wastes with high carbon content.  Which one of the following is correct in respect of the above statements?

  ✅ A) Both Statement II and Statement III are correct and both of them explain Statement I
     B) Both Statement II and Statement III are correct but only one of them explains Statement I
     C) Only one of the Statements II and III is correct and that explains Statement I
     D) Neither Statement II nor Statement III is correct

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may doubt that activated carbon is synthesized from environmental waste — but it can be made from agricultural waste (rice husk, coconut shells) with high carbon content.
  💡 **Eliminate:** All three statements about activated carbon are correct: large surface area (II), adsorbs heavy metals (II), and can be made from carbon-rich environmental waste (III). Both II and III explain its pollution-removal properties (I). Answer: A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Activated carbon has extremely high surface area (~500-1500 m²/g)
     - Can be produced from agricultural waste (coconut shell, rice husk)

**[Q3301]** [2022]
> Consider the following:  1. Carbon monoxide 2. Nitrogen oxide 3. Ozone 4. Sulphur dioxide  Excess of which of the above in the environment is/are cause(s) of acid rain?

     A) 1, 2 and 3
  ✅ B) 2 and 4 only
     C) 4 only
     D) 1, 3 and 4

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Carbon monoxide and ozone are air pollutants but do not cause acid rain; ozone in particular is a secondary pollutant and oxidant, not an acid rain precursor.

     - Acid rain chemistry: SO2 + H2O → H2SO4; NOx + H2O → HNO3

  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's National Water Policy and environmental laws provide a framework for water resource management and pollution control, with the Ganga serving as both the most critical and most challenging case study.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • National Water Policy (2012): water as national resource; priority — drinking water > domestic > agriculture > hydropower > industry; integrated water resource management (IWRM)
      • Ganga pollution causes: untreated sewage from 97 towns, industrial effluents (tanneries in Kanpur), agricultural runoff, solid waste, religious activities
      • Strategies for Ganga: NMCG (National Mission for Clean Ganga); STP (Sewage Treatment Plants) — gap between capacity and installation; industrial effluent treatment mandates; e-flows notification
      • Namami Gange Programme (2014) — Rs 20,000 crore flagship program; real-time monitoring, ghat development, afforestation
      • Hazardous Waste Management: Environment (Protection) Act 1986 and Hazardous and Other Wastes Rules 2016; Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR); authorized disposal sites
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Effective Ganga rejuvenation requires multi-pronged action — legal enforcement, technological upgradation, behavioral change, and political will — underpinned by a robust national water policy.
