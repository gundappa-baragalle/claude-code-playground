**[2025]** Q20. Does tribal development in India centre around two axes, those of displacement and of rehabilitation? Give your opinion.  (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: IMPACT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Tribal displacement, Land rights and development, Rehabilitation policy

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Tribal development in India has historically been caught between two contradictory forces: forced displacement due to development projects and inadequate rehabilitation, creating a cycle of marginalization.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Mining, dams and industrial projects primarily located in tribal areas
      • Displacement without adequate R&R has impoverished tribal communities
      • Forest Rights Act and PESA meant to protect but weakly implemented
      • Rehabilitation policies often inadequate — compensation without livelihood restoration
      • The 'development refugee' phenomenon — tribals displaced multiple times
      • Need for consent-based development models (FPIC — Free, Prior and Informed Consent)
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Genuine tribal development requires moving beyond displacement-rehabilitation cycles toward participatory, rights-based and culturally sensitive development frameworks.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Governance — Forest Rights Act implementation, Economy — mining and industrial policy, Polity — Fifth Schedule areas and PESA
  🌐 Contemporary: PM JANMAN scheme (2023) for particularly vulnerable tribal groups and ongoing forest land diversion debates keep this issue current.

  **Answer Framework:**

  - 🔑 **Intro:** The replacement of physical cuddling by mobile phones represents a fundamental shift in primary socialization, with significant implications for children's emotional, cognitive and social development.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Physical touch and cuddling are essential for secure attachment (Bowlby's attachment theory) and emotional regulation

  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Balancing digital engagement with meaningful physical interaction is essential to ensure healthy socialization of children in the digital age.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 Society, GS2 Health policy, GS4 Ethics — family values
  🌐 Contemporary: WHO and UNICEF guidelines on screen time for children are increasingly debated globally.

**[2021]** Q20. How does Indian society maintain continuity in traditional social values? Enumerate the changes taking place in it. (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Continuity of traditional values, Social change in India, Modernisation vs tradition

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Indian society exhibits a remarkable coexistence of continuity in traditional values and dynamic transformation, shaped by its civilisational depth and exposure to modern forces.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Continuity mechanisms: Family system — joint family as primary social unit preserves values of collectivism, respect for elders, ritual observance; Religious institutions — temples, mosques, churches, gurudwaras transmit values intergenerationally; Oral traditions — folk songs, epics, storytelling; Festivals — Diwali, Eid, Onam reinforce community bonds; Educational institutions including gurukuls and madrasas
      • Values preserved: Ahimsa, respect for elders (Guru-Shishya tradition), hospitality (Atithi Devo Bhava), tolerance (Sarva Dharma Samabhava), family loyalty
      • Changes in social values: Urbanisation leading to nuclear families; declining joint family prevalence; women entering workforce in larger numbers; inter-caste and inter-religious marriages increasing; digital/social media reshaping social norms
      • Caste system changes: Constitutional abolition of untouchability; affirmative action enabling upward mobility; Dalit assertion movements; but caste identity still relevant in politics and marriage
      • Globalisation's impact: Western consumerism influencing lifestyle; exposure to global values of individualism; reverse impact — global yoga culture, Ayurveda popularisation
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Indian society's resilience lies in its ability to adapt — selectively absorbing modern values while retaining the ethical and spiritual core that defines its civilisational identity.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Salient features of Indian society, GS1: Role of women and women's organisations, GS1: Globalisation and social change
  🌐 Contemporary: Post-COVID social patterns show both acceleration of change (digital socialization) and return to tradition (spiritual seeking).

**[2021]** Q9. Examine the uniqueness of tribal knowledge system when compared with mainstream knowledge and cultural systems. (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: VS | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Tribal knowledge uniqueness, Knowledge systems comparison, Cultural diversity

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Tribal knowledge systems represent a distinct epistemological tradition rooted in oral transmission, ecological observation, and community experience, differing fundamentally from the mainstream codified knowledge systems.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Oral and experiential: transmitted through stories, rituals, and practice rather than texts
      • Place-specific and ecological: deep knowledge of local biodiversity, seasons, soil — different from universalizing science
      • Community-owned: collective IP rather than individual authorship; no IPR protection by default
      • Holistic worldview: integrates spiritual, social, and ecological dimensions, unlike compartmentalized mainstream knowledge
      • Challenges: biopiracy, lack of documentation, marginalization in formal education
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Recognizing and integrating tribal knowledge systems requires both legal protection (like PVP&FR Act) and genuine institutional respect for indigenous epistemologies.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Indian Society, GS3: IPR and traditional knowledge, GS2: Tribal welfare governance
  🌐 Contemporary: Protection of traditional knowledge from biopiracy (e.g., neem, turmeric patents) remains an active policy concern.

**[2020]** Q19. Customs and traditions suppress reason leading to obscurantism. Do you agree? (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Tradition vs modernity, Social reform, Scientific temper in Indian society

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The relationship between customs, traditions, and rational thought is not uniformly suppressive — it depends on whether traditions are uncritically perpetuated or subjected to reasoned evaluation.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Cases where traditions suppress reason: caste untouchability, sati, child marriage, superstitions — leading to obscurantism
      • Indian social reform tradition: Ram Mohan Roy, Ambedkar, Phule challenged irrational customs through reason and social mobilization
      • Tradition can also complement reason: Ayurveda, yoga, environmental conservation practices show rational underpinnings
      • Constitutional response: Article 51A(h) — duty to develop scientific temper, humanism and spirit of inquiry
      • Balanced view: selective critique rather than wholesale rejection — traditions should be evaluated on rationality and justice
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While certain customs undeniably breed obscurantism, wholesale condemnation of tradition ignores its cultural function; the key is applying rational critique to preserve the just and reform the unjust.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Indian society and modernization, GS4: Ethics — values and morality, GS2: Governance — social welfare
  🌐 Contemporary: Debates around triple talaq, temple entry, and khap panchayats reflect ongoing tensions between tradition and rational constitutional values.

**[2019]** Q18. Do we have cultural pockets of small India all over the nation? Elaborate with examples. (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHAT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Cultural heterogeneity within India, Regional cultural pockets, Unity in diversity

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India is not a monolithic cultural entity but a vibrant mosaic of micro-cultures, each region functioning as a 'mini-India' with its own distinct language, food, art, and social practices.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Linguistic pockets: 22 official languages, 1600+ dialects — every 50 km language changes (linguistic survey of India)
      • Culinary pockets: Rajasthani dal-baati vs Bengali fish curry vs Tamil sambar — food as cultural identity
      • Religious pockets: Buddhist monasteries in Ladakh, temple culture in Tamil Nadu, Sikh heritage in Punjab, churches in Kerala
      • Tribal cultural pockets: Warli art (Maharashtra), Madhubani (Bihar), Gond (MP) — living traditions
      • Festival pockets: Bihu (Assam), Onam (Kerala), Pongal (Tamil Nadu), Durga Puja (Bengal) — same calendar, different celebrations
      • Urban cultural pockets: Kolkata's intellectual-artistic identity, Mumbai's cosmopolitanism, Hyderabad's Nizam culture
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** These cultural pockets strengthen rather than fragment Indian identity — they represent living heritage that constitutes India's greatest social asset.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 — Indian society, art and culture, GS2 — Regionalism, federalism
  🌐 Contemporary: Preserving local cultural pockets while resisting cultural homogenization from globalization is a key challenge.

**[2017]** Q9. In the context of the diversity of India, can it be said that the regions form cultural units rather than the States? Give reasons with examples for your viewpoint.  (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Cultural diversity, Regionalism, Indian identity

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's cultural geography does not map neatly onto its political geography — cultural regions defined by language, cuisine, folk arts, and religious traditions often transcend state boundaries.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Rajasthan and Gujarat share Rajputana cultural traits despite being different states
      • Bhojpuri cultural zone spans UP, Bihar, Jharkhand — multiple states, one cultural identity
      • Northeast: Bodo community across Assam-Arunachal; Naga tribes across Nagaland-Manipur
      • Tamil cultural region: Tamil Nadu + Sri Lanka Tamil diaspora + Tamils in Malaysia
      • Counter-argument: linguistic states have created political cultural identities — Maharashtra, Karnataka pride
      • States over time acquire cultural identities through administration, education, cultural institutions
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While cultural regions predate political states and often cut across them, the administrative process of statehood has itself become a powerful force in shaping cultural identities over time.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Indian geography, GS1: Society — regionalism, Post-independence state formation
  🌐 Contemporary: Demands for separate statehood often invoke cultural-regional identity distinct from existing state boundaries.

**[2016]** Q10. Why are the tribals in India referred to as the 'Scheduled Tribes'? Indicate the major provisions enshrined in the Constitution of India for their upliftment. (200 words) 12.5
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHAT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Tribal rights, Constitutional safeguards, Affirmative action

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The designation 'Scheduled Tribes' reflects the constitutional recognition of India's indigenous communities who have historically faced geographical isolation and socio-economic marginalisation.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Definition criteria: primitive traits, distinctive culture, geographical isolation, shyness of contact, backwardness
      • Article 342: President specifies Scheduled Tribes after consulting state governor
      • Reservation in Parliament, State Legislatures, government jobs and education
      • Fifth Schedule: administration of tribal areas in 10 states through Tribes Advisory Councils
      • Sixth Schedule: autonomous councils in Assam, Meghalaya, Tripura, Mizoram
      • PESA Act 1996: gram sabhas given powers over resources in tribal areas
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The constitutional framework for Scheduled Tribes reflects India's commitment to protecting its most vulnerable communities through both protective discrimination and institutional autonomy.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: Polity — Fundamental Rights, Governance — vulnerable sections, Forest rights
  🌐 Contemporary: Forest Rights Act implementation and mining in tribal areas remain flashpoints for tribal rights versus development conflicts.
