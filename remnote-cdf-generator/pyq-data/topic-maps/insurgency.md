### GS3

**[2025]** Q19. What are the major challenges to internal security and peace process in the North-Eastern States? Map the various peace accords and agreements initiated by the government in the past decade. (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: NE insurgency challenges, Peace process and accords in NE India

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's Northeast represents one of the most complex internal security challenges, with ethnic aspirations, border porosity, and historical grievances fuelling prolonged insurgencies.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Major challenges: ethnic insurgencies (NSCN, ULFA, Bodo factions), drug trafficking from Myanmar (Golden Triangle), porous international borders (Myanmar, Bangladesh, China)
      • Ethnic conflicts: Manipur Meitei-Kuki violence (2023), Nagaland settlement challenges
      • Peace accords in last decade: Bodo Peace Accord (2020), NLFT accord (Tripura), ATTF accord, NSCN (NK) accord
      • NSCN (IM) framework agreement (2015): Unique Naga identity and history — final settlement pending
      • Development approach: Act East Policy, PM Development Initiative for NE (PM-DevINE), connectivity projects
      • AFSPA: gradually withdrawn from some areas of Assam, Manipur as security improves
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Durable peace in NE India requires simultaneous political dialogue, developmental investment, and sensitive handling of ethnic identity claims within India's constitutional framework.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 Internal Security, GS2 Polity — Federalism, IR — India & Neighbours
  🌐 Contemporary: The 2023 Manipur ethnic violence and pending Naga solution highlight the continuing urgency of NE peace processes.

**[2017]** Q10. The North-East region of India has been infested with insurgency for a very long time. Analyze the major reasons for the survival of armed insurgency in this region. (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Ethnic insurgency, Border management, Dialogue and peace process

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The North-East's insurgency is rooted in deep-seated ethnic aspirations, unresolved historical grievances, and geographical isolation that has sustained armed movements over decades.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Historical grievances: forcible merger, ignored tribal identities, linguistic and cultural distinctness
      • Porous international borders with Myanmar and Bangladesh provide sanctuary and arms supply routes
      • Ethnic fragmentation (200+ tribes) creates competing aspirations and multiple insurgent factions
      • Underdevelopment, unemployment, and state neglect fuel recruitment
      • Prolonged AFSPA creates civil-military trust deficit; lack of political dialogue sustains militancy
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Sustainable peace requires a comprehensive approach combining political dialogue, socio-economic development, border management, and gradual withdrawal of special security laws.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 (centre-state relations), GS1 (ethnic movements), IR (Act East Policy, India-Myanmar border)
  🌐 Contemporary: Naga peace talks, Bodoland Territorial Council settlement, and post-coup Myanmar instability remain live issues.

**[2015]** Q17. Human rights activists constantly highlight the view that the Armed Forces (Special Powers) Act, 1958 (AFSPA) is a draconian act leading to cases of human rights abuses by the security forces. What sections of AFSPA are opposed by the activists? Critically evaluate the requirement with reference to the view held by the Apex Court. (200 words) 12.5
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: IMPACT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Security vs human rights, AFSPA debate, Security legislation

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** AFSPA has been both a necessary security tool in disturbed areas and a contested legislation due to documented human rights abuses.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Opposed sections: Sec 4 (powers to shoot on reasonable suspicion), Sec 6 (immunity from prosecution without Centre's sanction)
      • Activists' arguments: extrajudicial killings, enforced disappearances, fake encounters; no effective accountability
      • Apex Court view (2016): AFSPA not a blanket license to kill; armed forces can be prosecuted for excesses; no blanket immunity for violations
      • Government's position: operational necessity in disturbed areas with active insurgency
      • Jeevan Reddy Committee (2005) recommended repeal; not implemented
      • Partial withdrawal: Northeast states have seen gradual AFSPA rollback as security improves
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** A gradual, condition-based phased withdrawal of AFSPA with enhanced accountability mechanisms offers a balanced path between security imperatives and rights protection.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Internal Security, GS2 – Governance, GS4 – Ethics
  🌐 Contemporary: Partial withdrawal from some Northeastern states in 2022 and ongoing Manipur conflict make AFSPA highly debated.
