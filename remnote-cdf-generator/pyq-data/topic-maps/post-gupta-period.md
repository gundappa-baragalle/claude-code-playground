**[Q70]** [2021]
> With reference to the history of ancient India, which of the following statements is/are correct?  1. Mitakshara was the civil law for upper castes and Dayabhaga was the civil law for lower castes. 2. In the Mitakshara system, the sons can claim right to the property during the lifetime of the father, whereas in the Dayabhaga system, it is only after the death of the father that the sons can claim right to the property. 3. The Mitakshara system deals with the matters related to the property held by male members only of a family, whereas the Dayabhaga system deals with the matters related to the property held by both male and female members of a family.  Select the correct answer using the code given below.

     A) 1 and 2
  ✅ B) 2 only
     C) 1 and 3
     D) 3 only

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 1 is false — Mitakshara and Dayabhaga are NOT divided by caste; they are regional schools (Mitakshara: most of India; Dayabhaga: Bengal/Assam). Statement 3 is also false — Dayabhaga does not specifically cover female members.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Only Statement 2 is correct: under Mitakshara, sons get coparcenary rights by birth (during father's lifetime); under Dayabhaga, rights arise only after father's death. Answer: B (2 only).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Mitakshara: sons acquire rights by birth (coparcenary)
     - Dayabhaga: rights arise only on father's death
     - Mitakshara applies in most of India; Dayabhaga in Bengal/Assam

**[Q2238]** [2014]
> With reference to India's culture and tradition, what is ‘Kalaripayattu’?

     A) It is an ancient Bhakti cult of Shaivism still prevalent in some parts of South India
     B) It is an ancient style bronze and brasswork still found in southern part of Coromandel area
     C) It is an ancient form of dance-drama and a living tradition in the northern part of Malabar
  ✅ D) It is an ancient martial art and a living tradition in some parts of South India

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Options A and C suggest it is a religious cult or dance-drama — Kalaripayattu is purely a martial art from Kerala, not a bhakti cult or performance art.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Kalaripayattu is one of the world's oldest martial arts, originating in Kerala — a living tradition of unarmed and armed combat techniques.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Kalaripayattu is an ancient martial art from Kerala
     - It is considered one of the oldest martial arts in the world
     - It influences classical dance forms like Kathakali through body movement training

**[Q2239]** [2014]
> Consider the following pairs:  1. Garba - Gujarat 2. Mohiniattam - Odisha 3. Yakshagana - Karnataka  Which of the pairs given above is/are correctly matched?

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 and 3 only
  ✅ C) 1 and 3 only
     D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Mohiniattam is from Kerala, not Odisha (Odisha is associated with Odissi dance) — students may confuse these two classical forms from eastern and southern coastal regions.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Garba-Gujarat and Yakshagana-Karnataka are correct. Mohiniattam belongs to Kerala, not Odisha. Answer is C (pairs 1 and 3 only).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Garba is the folk/classical dance of Gujarat
     - Yakshagana is a traditional theatre form of Karnataka
     - Mohiniattam is a classical dance from Kerala — NOT Odisha (Odissi is from Odisha)

**[Q773]** [2003]
> Emperor Harsha’s southward march was stopped on the Narmada river by

     A) Pulakesin-I
  ✅ B) Pulakesin-II
     C) Vikramaditya-I
     D) Vilcramaditya-II

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Pulakesin I is the founder of Chalukya dynasty; Pulakesin II is who stopped Harsha — candidates may confuse the two.

     - Harsha's empire was confined to northern India; Narmada was the boundary

  ✅ A) Bhubaneswar

**[Q229]** [1995]
> The Nagara, the Dravida and the Vesara are

     A) the three main racial groups of the Indian subcontinent
     B) the three main linguistic divisions into which the languages of India can be classified
  ✅ C) the three main styles of Indian temple architecture
     D) the three main musical gharanas prevalent in India

  ⚠️ **Trap:** The terms Nagara, Dravida, Vesara sound like they could be racial or linguistic categories given India's diversity context.

**[2024]** Q11. "Though the great Cholas are no more yet their name is still remembered with great pride because of their highest achievements in the domain of art and architecture." Comment. (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHAT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Chola art and architecture legacy, South Indian cultural history, Temple architecture

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Though the Chola dynasty faded, their architectural masterpieces and artistic innovations have ensured their name is remembered with enduring pride across millennia.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Dravidian temple architecture: Brihadeeswara, Gangaikondacholapuram
      • Bronze sculpture: Nataraja as pinnacle of Chola aesthetics
      • Naval architecture and trade: maritime spread of culture
      • Urban planning: temple towns as socio-economic centres
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The Cholas' greatest empire was not territorial but cultural — built in stone, bronze, and the enduring spirit of artistic excellence.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 — Ancient History, Art & Architecture, GS1 — Cultural Heritage
  🌐 Contemporary: Chola temples are central to India's UNESCO World Heritage and cultural tourism strategy.

**[2024]** Q2. Estimate the contribution of Pallavas of Kanchi for the development of art and literature of South India. (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Ancient South Indian art, Patronage of learning, Dravidian culture

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The Pallavas of Kanchi made contributions to South Indian art and literature that were foundational — their architectural innovations and literary patronage set the template for subsequent South Indian cultural achievements.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Architecture: developed Dravidian style — monolithic rathas at Mahabalipuram, Shore Temple — precursors to Chola temples
      • Cave temples: Mahendravarman I innovated rock-cut cave temples — Mandagappattu, Mahabalipuram
      • Literature: Mahendravarman I wrote 'Mattavilasa Prahasana' in Sanskrit — satire on Buddhist, Shaiva, Jain ascetics
      • Tamil literature: Pallava period saw flourishing of Bhakti literature — Nayanmars (Shaivite) and Alvars (Vaishnavite)
      • Sculpture: Arjuna's Penance at Mahabalipuram — world's largest open-air rock-cut bas-relief
      • Education: Kanchi was a centre of Buddhist, Jain, and Sanskrit learning
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The Pallava contribution to South Indian art and literature was generative rather than merely decorative — they created the aesthetic vocabulary that Chola and subsequent dynasties elaborated and expanded.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Ancient India — art & architecture, South Indian history, Cultural heritage
  🌐 Contemporary: Mahabalipuram's UNESCO status and its role as diplomatic backdrop (India-China summit 2019) reflect Pallava heritage's contemporary relevance.

**[2020]** Q1. The rock-cut architecture represents one of the most important sources of our knowledge of early Indian art and history. Discuss. (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Art history, Cultural evolution, Rock-cut traditions

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Rock-cut architecture, carved directly into living rock, constitutes one of India's most durable artistic legacies, providing invaluable insights into ancient religious, social, and aesthetic traditions.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Historical knowledge: rock-cut monuments (Ajanta, Ellora, Bhimbetka) document evolution from prehistoric cave paintings to classical Buddhist and Hindu art
      • Religious diversity: Buddhist chaityas/viharas (Ajanta), Jain caves (Udayagiri), Hindu temples (Ellora Kailasa) show religious pluralism
      • Artistic evolution: transitions from Hinayana to Mahayana iconography visible in Ajanta murals
      • Trade connections: patronage by merchants along trade routes reflects economic history
      • Architectural innovation: corbelling, pillar design, and structural techniques reveal evolving engineering knowledge
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Rock-cut monuments serve as unparalleled windows into ancient India's religious pluralism, artistic sophistication, and the socio-economic networks that sustained them.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 Art and Culture, Indian History, Tourism and Heritage
  🌐 Contemporary: UNESCO World Heritage status of Ajanta, Ellora, Mahabalipuram underscores global significance.

**[2018]** Q1. Safeguarding the Indian art heritage is the need of the moment. Discuss. (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Art heritage conservation, Cultural identity, Policy for heritage protection

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's millennia-old art heritage, from rock paintings and temple sculptures to miniature paintings, represents an irreplaceable civilizational legacy that demands urgent and systematic protection.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Scale of heritage: 3,693 centrally protected monuments, countless unprotected local sites facing neglect and encroachment
      • Threat of trafficking: illicit trade in Indian antiquities; high-profile repatriation cases (Nataraja, Idol Wing cases) highlight the problem
      • Neglect and vandalism: insufficient conservation funds, inadequate security at heritage sites, urban development pressure
      • Need for community involvement: local communities as custodians of living heritage traditions
      • Policy measures needed: strengthening ASI, updating Antiquities Act, using technology (3D scanning, digital archives) for preservation
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Safeguarding India's art heritage requires a multi-pronged approach combining legislative strengthening, technological conservation, community participation, and international cooperation on repatriation.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 – Indian Art & Culture, GS2 – Governance, GS3 – Economy (heritage tourism)
  🌐 Contemporary: Several Indian antiquities have been repatriated from the US and UK — heritage diplomacy is an active policy area.

**[2013]** Q2. (a) Discuss the 'Tandava' dance as recorded in early Indian inscriptions. (100 words) 5
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHAT | 5m | ~100w*
  🏷️ Themes: Tandava in early inscriptions, Shiva as cosmic dancer in temple sculpture, Religious dance traditions in ancient India

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~100 words: intro 30w / body 80w / conclusion 25w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Tandava, the fierce and cosmic dance of Shiva, is attested in early Indian literary and epigraphic sources as a divine performance that symbolizes the cycles of creation and destruction.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Literary origins: Mahabharata mentions Shiva's Ananda Tandava; Ramayana refers to Shiva's dance; Kalidasa's Kumarasambhava elaborates the dance's divine significance
      • Inscriptional evidence: Pallava inscription at Mahabalipuram (7th CE) describes Shiva's dance; Mattavilasa Prahasana of Mahendravarman I is contextual
      • Dance forms: Ananda Tandava (blissful); Rudra Tandava (fierce); Sandhya Tandava (twilight) — different moods of the cosmic dance
      • Nataraja iconography: Chola bronze Nataraja (10th-12th CE) — Shiva dancing in ring of fire, symbolising cosmic cycle; Tamil inscriptions describe dance ritual in temples
      • Cultural legacy: Tandava became foundational to Indian classical dance traditions including Bharatanatyam
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Early inscriptions preserve Tandava not merely as artistic expression but as theological assertion — Shiva's dance as the rhythm of the universe, encoded in stone and later in living dance traditions.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 Art & Culture, GS1 History, Religion
  🌐 Contemporary: Nataraja image is recognized globally as a symbol of Indian civilization; now displayed at CERN representing cosmic dance of particles.

### Common Trap Patterns on This Topic
- Statement 1 is false — Mitakshara and Dayabhaga are NOT divided by caste; they are regional schools (Mitakshara: most of India; Dayabhaga: Bengal/Assam). Statement 3 is also false — Dayabhaga does not specifically cover female members.
- Rajasekhara was a poet/playwright, NOT a jurist. Students may confuse literary figures with legal scholars. Jimutavahana (Dayabhaga) and Vijnanesvara (Mitakshara) are the two most famous jurists.
- Ajivika is often confused with Shaivism due to its ascetic traditions, but it is a distinct heterodox sect (not Shaiva). Mattamayura is an obscure Shaiva Tantric sub-sect.
- The terms Nagara, Dravida, Vesara sound like they could be racial or linguistic categories given India's diversity context.
- Bhakti (devotion) sounds most associated with liberation in popular understanding. Jnana (knowledge) is also a strong distractor.
