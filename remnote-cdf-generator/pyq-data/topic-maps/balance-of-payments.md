**[Q2915]** [2019]
> Consider the following statements :  1. Most of India's external debt is owed by governmental entities. 2. All of India's external debt is denominated in US dollars.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
     C) Both 1 and 2
  ✅ D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Both statements are wrong — most of India's external debt is owed by the private sector (commercial borrowings), not government; and India's external debt is denominated in multiple currencies (USD, SDR, Euro, JPY, etc.), not just USD.
  💡 **Eliminate:** India's external debt: private sector accounts for the majority (commercial borrowings); denominated in USD, SDR, Euro, Yen and others. Both statements false — answer is D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - India's external debt: ~50-55% owed by private sector (commercial borrowings, NRI deposits)
     - External debt denominated in multiple currencies: USD (~55%), SDR, EUR, JPY, INR
     - Government (sovereign) external debt is a minority of total external debt

**[Q2492]** [2016]
> Which of the following best describes the term 'import cover', sometimes seen in the news?

     A) It is the ratio of value of imports to the Gross Domestic Product of a country
     B) It is the total value of imports of a country in a year
     C) It is the ratio between the value of exports and that of imports between two countries
  ✅ D) It is the number of months of imports that could be paid for by a country's international reserves

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Import cover sounds like it should relate to a ratio of imports to GDP or exports, but it is specifically about months of import coverage from foreign exchange reserves.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Import cover = number of months of imports a country can pay for using its international reserves — answer D. This is a key forex reserve adequacy indicator.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - India typically maintains 8-10 months of import cover in its forex reserves
     - Import cover is a key metric for assessing balance of payments vulnerability

**[Q2456]** [2015]
> The problem of international liquidity is related to the non-availability of

     A) goods and services
     B) gold and silver
  ✅ C) dollars and other hard currencies
     D) exportable surplus

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Option B (gold and silver) is a classic trap referencing the historical gold standard; the modern international liquidity problem is about hard currencies not being available.
  💡 **Eliminate:** International liquidity refers to the availability of internationally accepted currencies (dollars, euros, etc.) for settling international transactions; gold standard no longer applies.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - International liquidity: availability of hard currencies (USD, EUR, GBP) for international payments
     - SDRs (Special Drawing Rights) were created by IMF to supplement international liquidity
     - Countries with insufficient reserves face balance of payments crises

**[Q2194]** [2014]
> With reference to Balance of Payments, which of the following constitutes/constitute the Current Account?  1. Balance of trade 2. Foreign assets 3. Balance of invisibles 4. Special Drawing Rights  Select the correct answer using the code given below.

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 and 3
  ✅ C) 1 and 3
     D) 1, 2 and 4

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Foreign assets and SDRs are part of Capital Account, not Current Account; students confuse which account includes invisibles (services, transfers) vs capital flows.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Current Account = Balance of Trade (goods) + Balance of Invisibles (services, transfers, remittances); foreign assets and SDRs are capital account items — answer is C (1 and 3).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Current Account = Trade balance + Invisibles (services, remittances, transfers)
     - Capital Account = FDI, FII, ECB, SDR allocations, foreign assets

**[Q2102]** [2013]
> Which one of the following groups of items is included in India's foreign-exchange reserves?

     A) Foreign-currency assets, Special Drawing Rights (SDRs) and loans from foreign countries
  ✅ B) Foreign-currency assets, gold holdings of the RBI and SDRs
     C) Foreign-currency assets, loans from the World Bank and SDRs
     D) Foreign-currency assets, gold holdings of the RBI and loans from the World Bank

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Foreign loans are a trap — they are not part of foreign exchange reserves (reserves are what you have, not what you owe). World Bank loans are liabilities, not reserves.
  💡 **Eliminate:** India's forex reserves = Foreign currency assets + Gold holdings of RBI + SDRs + Reserve tranche position in IMF — loans from foreign countries/World Bank are NOT included.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - India's forex reserves comprise: FCAs, gold, SDRs, and RTP in IMF
     - SDRs (Special Drawing Rights) are allocated by IMF and form part of reserves
     - Foreign loans are liabilities, not assets — not part of reserves

**[Q2136]** [2013]
> Which of the following constitute Capital Account?  1. Foreign Loans 2. Foreign Direct Investment 3. Private Remittances 4. Portfolio Investment  Select the correct answer using the codes given below.

     A) 1, 2 and 3
  ✅ B) 1, 2 and 4
     C) 2, 3 and 4
     D) 1, 3 and 4

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Private remittances are a current account item (transfers), NOT capital account — students often include them in capital account. Foreign loans, FDI, and portfolio investment are capital account items.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Capital Account = Foreign Loans + FDI + Portfolio Investment + External Commercial Borrowings. Private Remittances go to Current Account (transfer account). Answer is B (1, 2, 4).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Capital Account comprises FDI, portfolio investment, foreign loans, and banking capital
     - Remittances are Current Account items (private transfers)
     - India has one of the world's largest current account remittance inflows

**[Q1609]** [2011]
> Consider the following actions which the Government can take:  1. Devaluing the domestic currency. 2. Reduction in the export subsidy. 3. Adopting suitable policies which attract greater FDI and more funds from FIIs.  Which of the above action/actions can help in reducing the current account deficit?

     A) 1 and 2
     B) 2 and 3
     C) 3 only
  ✅ D) 1 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Reduction in export subsidy (Statement 2) seems counterintuitive for reducing CAD — actually reducing export subsidy may reduce exports making CAD worse, not better. This is the key trap in this question.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Devaluation (1) makes exports cheaper abroad and imports costlier — reduces CAD. FDI/FII inflows (3) finance CAD on capital account, reducing need to deplete reserves. Export subsidy reduction (2) worsens or has no direct beneficial impact on CAD. Answer: D (1 and 3).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Currency devaluation improves current account by making exports more competitive and imports more expensive (Marshall-Lerner condition)
     - FDI and FII inflows finance CAD on the capital account side of BOP

**[Q1120]** [2006]
> Assertion (A) : ‘Balance of Payments’ represents a better picture of a country’s economic transactions with the reset of the world than the ‘Balance of Trade’. Reason (R) : ‘Balance of Payments’ takes into account the exchange of both visible and invisible items whereas ‘Balance of Trade’ does not.

  ✅ A) Both A and R are individually true R is the correct explanation of A.
     B) Both A and R are individually true R is not the correct explanation of A.
     C) A is true but R is false.
     D) A is false but R is true.

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Both statements appear true and the reason seems to explain the assertion well. Ensure you verify that BoP truly includes invisible items (services, remittances) while BoT does not — this is the exact distinction.
  💡 **Eliminate:** BoP = BoT (visible) + invisible items (services, remittances, capital flows). This directly explains why BoP gives a fuller picture. Both A and R are true, and R correctly explains A → Answer A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Balance of Payments includes both current and capital account
     - Balance of Trade covers only visible (merchandise) exports and imports
     - Invisible items include services, remittances, financial transfers

**[2015]** Q7. Craze for gold in Indians has led to a surge in import of gold in recent years and put pressure on balance of payments and external value of rupee. In view of this, examine the merits of the Gold Monetization Scheme. (200 words) 12.5
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: IMPACT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Import management, BoP sustainability, Gold sector reforms

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's gold import craze creates persistent pressure on the current account and exchange rate, making the Gold Monetization Scheme a necessary intervention.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • BoP pressure: gold imports widen current account deficit, weaken rupee
      • GMS merits: monetizes idle gold (~25,000 tonnes held by households and temples), earns interest
      • Reduces physical import demand by recycling existing gold into the financial system
      • Temple gold utilization: significant religious gold deposits could finance import reduction
      • Challenges: poor participation, cumbersome melting process, low interest rate, trust deficit
      • Complementary scheme: Sovereign Gold Bond offers capital appreciation without physical import
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While GMS has merit in principle, its design flaws need correction to achieve meaningful reduction in gold import dependency.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Economy, GS2 – Governance, GS1 – Society
  🌐 Contemporary: Gold remains a strategic import challenge; RBI's gold accumulation and SGB revisions keep this relevant.
