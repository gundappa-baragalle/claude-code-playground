**[Q3428]** [2023]
> Consider the following statements :  1. Statement-I : India's public sector health care system largely focuses on curative care with limited preventive, promotive and rehabilitative care. 2. Statement-II: Under India's decentralized approach to health care delivery, the States are primarily responsible for organizing health services.  Which one of the following is correct in respect of the above statements?

     A) Both Statement-I and Statement-II are correct and Statement-II is the correct explanation for Statement-I
  ✅ B) Both Statement-I and Statement-II are correct and Statement-II is not the correct explanation for Statement-I
     C) Statement-I is correct but Statement-II is incorrect
     D) Statement-I is incorrect but Statement-II is correct

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement I (curative focus) seems like it could explain Statement II (decentralization) — but decentralization itself doesn't cause the curative focus; they are independent (though both correct) truths.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Both Statement I (public health largely curative) and Statement II (states responsible for health delivery) are correct; Statement II is NOT the explanation for Statement I — states being responsible doesn't cause the curative bias; answer is B.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - India's public health system is predominantly curative — primary health care (preventive, promotive) remains underfunded
     - Health is a State subject under the Constitution; states design and deliver most health services

**[Q3265]** [2022]
> With reference to Ayushman Bharat Digital Mission, consider the following statements:  1. Private and public hospitals must adopt it. 2. As it aims to achieve universal health coverage, every citizen of India should be part of it ultimately. 3. It has seamless portability across the country.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 and 2 only
  ✅ B) 3 only
     C) 1 and 3 only
     D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 1 (mandatory adoption) and 2 (universal coverage) sound like logical policy goals, but ABDM is voluntary, not mandatory, and universal coverage is aspirational.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Only Statement 3 is correct — ABDM (Ayushman Bharat Digital Mission) offers seamless portability across states via health ID. It is voluntary (not mandatory), and universal coverage is a goal, not a guarantee.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - ABDM (PM-DISHA) creates unique Health IDs for citizens enabling seamless health records across India
     - ABDM is voluntary — no compulsion for hospitals or citizens to adopt it currently

**[Q2958]** [2019]
> Which one of the following statements is not correct?

     A) Hepatitis B virus is transmitted much like HIV.
  ✅ B) Hepatitis B, unlike Hepatitis C, does not have a vaccine.
     C) Globally, the number of people infected with Hepatitis B and C viruses are several times more than those infected with HIV.
     D) Some of those infected with Hepatitis B and C viruses do not show the symptoms for many years.

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Option B is the NOT correct statement — Hepatitis B DOES have a vaccine (HBV vaccine exists since 1980s); it is Hepatitis C that historically had no vaccine (though treatment has improved dramatically).
  💡 **Eliminate:** Hepatitis B has a safe, effective vaccine — included in India's Universal Immunization Programme. Hepatitis C has no approved vaccine yet. Statement B reverses this fact. Answer is B.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Hepatitis B vaccine: available since 1981; highly effective; part of UIP in India
     - Hepatitis C: no vaccine yet — but highly effective direct-acting antiviral (DAA) drugs now available
     - HBV and HCV infections far outnumber HIV infections globally

**[Q2645]** [2017]
> Consider the following statements :  1. In tropical regions, Zika virus disease is transmitted by the same mosquito that transmits dengue. 2. Sexual transmission of Zika virus disease is possible.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
  ✅ C) Both 1 and 2
     D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates who are unfamiliar with Zika may assume mosquito-only transmission is the only route, missing the documented sexual transmission aspect.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Both statements are correct: Aedes aegypti mosquito transmits both dengue and Zika; WHO confirmed sexual transmission of Zika. Answer is C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Zika transmitted by Aedes aegypti (same as dengue, chikungunya, yellow fever)
     - Zika can also be sexually transmitted — rare but documented
     - Zika causes microcephaly in newborns — major concern in Brazil 2015-16

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** As India's healthcare system becomes increasingly marketized, the State must reassert its role to ensure equitable access, especially at the grassroots level.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Marketization impact: out-of-pocket expenditure (62%+ of total health spending) drives families into poverty
      • State's role: strengthen primary health care through Health and Wellness Centres

      • Measures: essential medicines policy, price caps on medical devices
      • Telemedicine and eSanjeevani for last-mile rural health access

  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Containing healthcare marketization requires the state to invest heavily in primary healthcare infrastructure while regulating private sector pricing and service standards.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: Social sector, Economy: Public Finance, GS1: Poverty and inequality
  🌐 Contemporary: Ayushman Bharat's expansion to 70+ age group (2024) and telemedicine growth post-COVID make this highly relevant.

**[2021]** Q6. "Besides being a moral imperative of a Welfare State, primary health structure is a necessary precondition for sustainable development." Analyze.  (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Primary health as welfare state obligation, PHC as foundation for sustainable development, Health-development nexus

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Strong primary health infrastructure is simultaneously a welfare state obligation and an economic precondition — a nation cannot achieve sustainable development without ensuring basic health security for all citizens.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Welfare state argument: Right to health (Art 21 read with Art 47 DPSP); state obligation to prevent premature mortality and morbidity
      • Economic argument: Healthy population = higher labor productivity, lower absenteeism, human capital accumulation (demographic dividend realization)
      • Sustainable development linkage: SDG 3 (Good Health) is both an end and enabler; health security prevents poverty trap from catastrophic health expenditure
      • PHC infrastructure gaps in India: doctor-population ratio below WHO norms, rural-urban disparity in facilities, ASHA understaffing
      • Way forward: Health and Wellness Centres under Ayushman Bharat to deliver comprehensive PHC; increase health expenditure to 2.5% of GDP
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Investing in robust primary health infrastructure is a moral imperative and an economic necessity — the foundation upon which India's sustainable development aspirations must be built.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 Society, GS3 Economy, GS2 Governance, Ethics GS4
  🌐 Contemporary: COVID-19 exposed India's weak PHC infrastructure; Ayushman Bharat Health and Wellness Centres are the key reform initiative.

**[2020]** Q6. In order to enhance the prospects of social development, sound and adequate health care policies are needed particularly in the fields of geriatric and maternal health care. Discuss. (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Geriatric health care needs, Maternal health as social development imperative, Health policy for demographic dividend and ageing

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Social development requires targeted health policies for vulnerable groups, particularly mothers and the elderly, whose health outcomes determine broader human development indices.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Geriatric care urgency: India's 60+ population expected to reach 300 million by 2050; chronic disease burden, mental health, pension-health linkages
      • Maternal health: MMR reduction critical for SDG 3; POSHAN Abhiyaan, Janani Suraksha Yojana, institutional deliveries

      • Policy gaps: geriatric wards rare in district hospitals; ASHA workers not trained for elderly care
      • Recommendations: National Programme for Health Care of Elderly expansion; strengthen PHC for maternal care
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Investing in geriatric and maternal health is both a moral imperative of the welfare state and a strategic investment in India's sustainable development trajectory.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 Society, GS3 Economy, GS2 Governance
  🌐 Contemporary: India's demographic transition — both youth bulge and ageing — makes simultaneous maternal and geriatric health policies essential.

**[2018]** Q7. Appropriate local community-level healthcare intervention is a prerequisite to achieve 'Health for All' in India. Explain. (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Primary healthcare, Community health, Universal health coverage

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Universal health coverage in India cannot be achieved through hospital-centric approaches alone; community-level healthcare interventions that address social determinants of health and provide first-contact care are the essential foundation.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Rationale: most diseases preventable/treatable at primary level — 80% of health needs met by primary care
      • ASHA workers: bridge between community and health system — immunization, RMNCH, NCDs screening
      • Sub-health centres, PHCs (Primary Health Centres): infrastructure being upgraded to Health and Wellness Centres
      • Social determinants: safe water, sanitation, nutrition — community interventions address these
      • Evidence: Kerala's community health model — highest health indicators through primary care focus
      • Ayushman Bharat: dual prong — 1.5 lakh HWCs for primary care + PMJAY for hospital coverage
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Achieving Health for All in India requires a comprehensive primary healthcare ecosystem anchored in community health workers, functional PHCs/HWCs, robust disease surveillance and addressing social determinants of health at the local level.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Governance — Ayushman Bharat, Society — women health workers (ASHA), Environment — WASH and health
  🌐 Contemporary: Ayushman Bharat Health and Wellness Centres converting to HWCs and ASHA workers' demand for permanent employment are current issues.

**[2015]** Q12. Public health system has limitations in providing universal health coverage. Do you think that the private sector could help in bridging the gap? What other viable alternatives would you suggest? (200 words) 12.5
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Universal health coverage, Public-private partnership, Health governance

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's public health system, despite constitutional obligations, struggles to provide universal coverage due to chronic underfunding, infrastructure gaps, and human resource shortages.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Public health limitations: low per-capita health expenditure (~$63), rural infrastructure deficit, doctor shortages, drug stockouts
      • Private sector role: 70% of outpatient, 60% of inpatient care — but out-of-pocket expenditure (~50%) impoverishes millions

      • Alternatives: community health workers (ASHAs), telemedicine, health insurance expansion, National Health Authority reforms
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Bridging the universal health coverage gap requires a calibrated public-private partnership where the state sets quality standards, regulates costs, and ensures equity while leveraging private capacity.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 Governance — health, GS3 Economy — health spending, GS2 — Ayushman Bharat
  🌐 Contemporary: India targets UHC under SDG 3; Ayushman Bharat PM-JAY covers 500 million beneficiaries as of 2024.
