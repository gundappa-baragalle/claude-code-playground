     - Non-violence was a cardinal principle of NCM

  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: VS | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Gandhi vs Ambedkar on caste, Dalit upliftment approaches, Social reform strategies

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Mahatma Gandhi and Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, though profoundly divergent in their approaches to caste and social reform, shared an unwavering commitment to the dignified upliftment of India's most marginalized communities.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Common goal: Both sought to end the oppression of untouchables/Dalits and ensure their dignity and social equality
      • Gandhi's approach: Harijan welfare through social reform within Hinduism; opposed untouchability as a sin against Hindu dharma; favoured gradual change through moral transformation
      • Ambedkar's approach: Saw caste as structural — cannot be reformed from within; advocated political rights (reservations, separate electorates), constitutional safeguards, and ultimately conversion from Hinduism
      • Divergence on Poona Pact (1932): Gandhi's fast against separate electorates for Untouchables; Ambedkar's agreement under duress — substituted reserved seats in joint electorates
      • Ambedkar's critique: Gandhi's Harijan (God's people) label patronizing; varna system itself perpetuates hierarchy
      • Legacy convergence: Both influenced Constitutional provisions — Fundamental Rights (Art. 17 abolishing untouchability) and reservations (Art. 15, 16)
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Despite tactical disagreements, Gandhi and Ambedkar's complementary legacies together shaped India's constitutional commitment to social justice and the ongoing project of eradicating caste discrimination.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 — Modern history, social reform, GS2 — Constitutional provisions on SC/ST
  🌐 Contemporary: The Gandhi-Ambedkar debate remains central to Indian political discourse on reservations and social justice.
