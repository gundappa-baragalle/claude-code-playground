**[Q4885]** [2010]
> Examine the following statements: 1. All colours are pleasant 2. Some colours are pleasant 3. No colour is pleasant 4. Some colours are not pleasant Given that the statement 4 is true, what can be definitely concluded?

     A) 1 and 2 are true
  ✅ B) 1 is false
     C) 2 is false
     D) 3 is true

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may confuse the logical implications; if 'some colours are not pleasant' (statement 4) is true, it contradicts 'all colours are pleasant' (statement 1), not necessarily statement 2.
  💡 **Eliminate:** If Statement 4 ('some colours are not pleasant') is true, then Statement 1 ('all colours are pleasant') must be FALSE. Statements 2 and 3 may or may not be true independently.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Particular negative (O) statement being true makes Universal affirmative (A) false
     - Logical square of opposition governs these relationships

**[Q4784]** [1996]
> In the given figure, the triangle represents girls, the square represents sports persons and the circle represents coaches. The portion in the figure which represents girls who are sports persons but not coaches is the one labelled

     A) A
  ✅ B) B
     C) D
     D) E

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Without the figure, students must reason: girls ∩ sports persons (but not coaches) = intersection of triangle and square excluding circle. This is typically a specific region in Venn diagrams.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Girls (triangle) ∩ Sports (square) but NOT coaches (circle) = the region inside both triangle and square but outside the circle. In standard Venn diagrams of this type, this is region B. Answer: B.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Venn diagram: triangle = girls, square = sports, circle = coaches
     - Girls who are sports persons but NOT coaches = triangle ∩ square - circle = region B

**[Q4772]** [1995]
> Examine the following statements : I watch TV only if I am bored. I am never bored when I have my brother’s company. Whenever I go to the theatre, I take my brother along. Which one of the following conclusions is valid in the context of the above statements ?

     A) If I am bored, I watch TV
     B) If I am bored, I seek my brother’s company
     C) If I am not with my brother, then I watch TV
  ✅ D) If I am not bored, I do not watch TV

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Option C ('if not with brother, watch TV') is tempting but commits the inverse fallacy; Option A ('if bored, watch TV') commits the converse fallacy; only the contrapositive of the first statement is valid.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Statement: 'Watch TV only if bored' means 'Watch TV → Bored'; contrapositive: 'Not bored → Not watch TV'. Option D states this correctly. Other options are invalid inferences.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - 'P only if Q' = 'P → Q'; contrapositive is 'not Q → not P'
     - Valid inference: not bored → don't watch TV (contrapositive of 'watch TV only if bored')

**[Q4532]** [2010] Half of the villagers of a certain village have their own houses. One-fifth of the villagers cultivate paddy. One-third of the villagers are literate. Four-fifth of the villagers are below twenty five. Then, which one of the following is certainly true?

     A) All the villagers who have their own houses are literate
  ✅ B) Some villagers under twenty five are literate
     C) A quarter of the villagers who have their own houses cultivate paddy
     D) Half of the villagers who cultivate paddy are literate

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may try to derive stronger conclusions than logically warranted; all given statements are fractions of the whole — only overlap guarantees work.

**[Q217]** [2001] Most guitarists are bearded males. If A represents all males, B represents bearded males and C represents all male guitarists, then the correct diagram for their relation (shaded portion) is

  ✅ A) I
     B) II
     C) III
     D) IV

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Since the premise says 'most' guitarists are bearded males (not 'all'), C must be a proper subset of B (bearded males), with C entirely within A. The overlap is partial, not complete.

**[Q3220]** [2001] Anand must be a vegetarian because he is a Buddhist. The argument assumes that

     A) most Buddhists are vegetarians
  ✅ B) all Buddhists are vegetarians
     C) only Buddhists are vegetarians
     D) most vegetarians are Buddhists

  ⚠️ **Trap:** The argument structure 'Anand is Buddhist, therefore vegetarian' assumes a universal claim — students may select 'most Buddhists' which only supports probability, not certainty.

**[Q823]** [1998] Examine the following statements : I. All members of Mohan’s family are honest. II. Some members of Mohan’s family are not employed. III. Some employed persons are not honest. IV. Some honest persons are not employed. Which of the following inferences can be drawn from the above statements ?

     A) All members of Mohan’s family are employed
  ✅ B) The employed members of Mohan’s family are honest
     C) The honest members of Mohan’s family are not employed
     D) The employed members of Mohan’s family are not honest

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement III says some employed are not honest; students may incorrectly apply this to Mohan's family members who are all honest by Statement I.

**[Q2601]** [1998] Examine the following three statements: I. Processed meat is a perishable food. II. All perishable foods are packed in sealed tins. III. Sealed tins sometimes do not contain processed meat. Which one of the following inferences can be drawn from the above statements?

     A) Sealed tins always contain perishable food
     B) Processed meat is sometimes not packed in sealed tins
  ✅ C) Processed meat is always packed in sealed tins
     D) Non-perishable foods are never packed in sealed tins

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may select A (sealed tins always contain perishable food) which reverses the logical implication, or D which introduces a claim about non-perishable foods not in the premises.

**[Q2595]** [1997] Examine the following statements regarding a set of balls : I. All balls are black. II. All balls are white. III. Only some balls are black. IV. No balls are black. Assuming that the balls can only be black or white, which two statements given above can both be true, but cannot both be false ? Choose the correct answer from the codes given below : Codes :

  ✅ A) I and IV
     B) I and III
     C) II and III
     D) III and IV

  ⚠️ **Trap:** The question asks which TWO statements can both be TRUE but cannot both be FALSE — candidates must test all pairs for simultaneous truth and mutual falsity.

**[Q265]** [1996] Two important characteristics of a hypothesis are that it should be testable and that it should be stated in a manner that it can be refuted. Which one of the following hypotheses, fulfils these characteristics ?

  ✅ A) Intelligent persons have good memory
     B) Some birds are animals
     C) Some businessmen are dishonest
     D) All men are mortal

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Options B, C, D are trivially true (birds are animals; some businessmen may be dishonest; all men are mortal) and thus NOT falsifiable. A good scientific hypothesis must be potentially disprovable.

**[Q572]** [1995] Examine the following statements : I. All children are inquisitive. II. Some children are inquisitive. III. No children are inquisitive. IV. Some children are not inquisitive. Among these statements, the two statements which cannot both be true simultaneously but can both be false would be

  ✅ A) I and III
     B) I and IV
     C) II and III
     D) III and IV

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students confuse 'subcontrary' (can't both be false but can both be true) with 'contradictory' (can't both be true or both be false). I (All) and III (No) are contradictories — they can't both be true OR false.

**[Q673]** [1995] Which of the following can be inferred from the statement that ‘Either John is stupid or John is lazy’ ? I. John is lazy / therefore, John is not stupid. II. John is not lazy / therefore, John is stupid. III. John is not stupid / therefore, John is lazy. IV. John is stupid / therefore, John is not lazy. Choose the correct answer from the codes given below : Codes :

     A) I and II
  ✅ B) II and III
     C) III and IV
     D) I and IV

  ⚠️ **Trap:** The statement uses inclusive 'or' (either...or), meaning at least one is true. Candidates may confuse inclusive vs exclusive 'or' — in logic, 'or' is inclusive unless stated as 'exclusive or'.

### Common Trap Patterns on This Topic
- Since the premise says 'most' guitarists are bearded males (not 'all'), C must be a proper subset of B (bearded males), with C entirely within A. The overlap is partial, not complete.
- Options B, C, D are trivially true (birds are animals; some businessmen may be dishonest; all men are mortal) and thus NOT falsifiable. A good scientific hypothesis must be potentially disprovable.

- The statement uses inclusive 'or' (either...or), meaning at least one is true. Candidates may confuse inclusive vs exclusive 'or' — in logic, 'or' is inclusive unless stated as 'exclusive or'.
- Candidates may draw Venn diagrams incorrectly by placing Carrot and Vegetable as overlapping circles instead of nested ones. Carrot is a subset of Vegetable which is a subset of Food.
