**[Q3601]** [2025]
> Consider the following statements :  I. India has joined the Minerals Security Partnership as a member. II. India is a resource rich country in all the 30 critical minerals that it has identified. Ill. The Parliament in 2023 has amended the Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act, 1957 empowering the Centra Government to exclusively auction mining lease and composite license for certain critical minerals.  Which of the statements given above are correct?

     A) I and II only
     B) II and III only
  ✅ C) I and III only
     D) I, II and III

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement II sounds plausible (India lacks critical minerals) but is a trap — India is NOT resource-rich in all 30 identified critical minerals.
  💡 **Eliminate:** India joined MSP (Statement I correct) and Parliament amended MMDR Act 2023 (Statement III correct); India is NOT self-sufficient in all 30 critical minerals (Statement II wrong). Answer: I and III.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - India is a member of Minerals Security Partnership (MSP)
     - MMDR Act amended in 2023 for critical mineral auctions

**[Q3687]** [2025]

     A) Only one
     B) Only two
  ✅ C) All the three

**[Q3587]** [2024]
> Recently, the term "pumped-storage hydropower" is actually and appropriately discussed in the context of which one of the following?

     A) Irrigation of terraced crop fields
     B) Lift irrigation of cereal crops
  ✅ C) Long duration energy storage
     D) Rainwater harvesting system

  ⚠️ **Trap:** The term 'pumped-storage' alongside 'hydropower' may mislead students toward irrigation or lift irrigation contexts given the water-pumping connotation.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Pumped-storage hydropower works by pumping water uphill when energy surplus and releasing it downhill when needed — making it a grid-scale long-duration energy storage solution.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Pumped-storage hydropower is the largest form of grid-scale energy storage globally
     - It acts as a giant rechargeable battery using water and gravity

**[Q2510]** [2016]
> 'Net metering' is sometimes seen in the news in the context of promoting the

  ✅ A) production and use of solar energy by the households/consumers
     B) use of piped natural gas in the kitchens of households
     C) installation of CNG kits in motor-cars
     D) installation of water meters in urban households

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Net metering includes 'metering' which students might associate with water or gas meters; it is specifically about electricity — consumers with solar panels send surplus power to the grid.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Net metering allows households with rooftop solar panels to sell surplus electricity to the grid, promoting solar energy use — answer A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Net metering allows prosumers (producer+consumer) to export surplus solar electricity to the grid and get credit
     - Net metering is a key policy incentive for rooftop solar adoption in India under Surya Ghar Muft Bijli Yojana

**[Q2599]** [2016]
> Which one of the following is a purpose of 'UDAY', a scheme of the Government?

     A) Providing technical and financial assistance to start-up entrepreneurs in the field of renewable sources of energy
     B) Providing electricity to every household in the country by 2018
     C) Replacing the coal-based power plants with natural gas, nuclear, solar, wind and tidal power plants over a period of time
  ✅ D) Providing for financial turnaround and revival of power distribution companies

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Option B (electricity to every household — SAUBHAGYA scheme) is easily confused with UDAY as both relate to electricity sector. Option A (renewables/startups) is also plausible.
  💡 **Eliminate:** UDAY = Ujwal DISCOM Assurance Yojana — specifically for financial turnaround of electricity distribution companies (DISCOMs) by reducing their debt burden. Answer: D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - UDAY (2015) helped state DISCOMs transfer debt to state governments at lower interest
     - Aimed to reduce AT&C (Aggregate Technical and Commercial) losses in power distribution

**[Q2082]** [2013]
> With reference to the usefulness of the by-products of sugar industry, which of the following statements is/are correct?  1. Bagasse can be used as biomass fuel for the generation of energy. 2. Molasses can be used as one of the feedstocks for the production of synthetic chemical fertilizers. 3. Molasses can be used for the production of ethanol.  Select the correct answer using the codes given below.

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 and 3 only
  ✅ C) 1 and 3 only
     D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 (molasses as synthetic fertiliser feedstock) is wrong — molasses is used for ethanol, not synthetic chemical fertilisers. Students may confuse molasses-to-ethanol with fertiliser production.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Bagasse → biomass fuel (1 correct); molasses → not fertilisers (2 wrong — it is used for ethanol/alcohol); molasses → ethanol (3 correct). Answer is C (1 and 3).

**[Q2336]** [2009]
> In the context of alternative sources of energy, ethanol as a viable bio-fuel can be obtained from:

     A) Potato
     B) Rice
  ✅ C) Sugarcane
     D) Wheat

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Potato and wheat contain starch that can be fermented to ethanol, but sugarcane is by far the most viable and widely used feedstock for ethanol production due to direct sucrose content.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Sugarcane is the most efficient ethanol feedstock globally (Brazil's model); its sucrose is directly fermentable without starch hydrolysis step — answer is C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Sugarcane is the primary ethanol feedstock globally (Brazil model)
     - India's Ethanol Blended Petrol (EBP) Programme uses sugarcane-derived ethanol

  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Energy security, Shale exploration challenges, Environmental vs energy trade-offs

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India possesses significant shale oil and gas reserves, but their exploitation remains constrained by technical, regulatory, environmental, and economic challenges.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Availability: Cambay, Gondwana, Krishna-Godavari, Cauvery basins have shale potential; estimated reserves significant
      • Technical challenge: Hydraulic fracturing (fracking) required — water-intensive in a water-scarce country
      • Environmental concerns: groundwater contamination risks, seismic activity, land disruption
      • Regulatory gaps: India lacks a dedicated shale gas regulatory framework and pricing policy
      • Economic viability: high extraction costs vs low global oil prices makes economics uncertain
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Tapping India's shale potential requires a comprehensive regulatory framework balancing energy security with environmental safeguards.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Energy, GS3 – Environment, GS2 – Policy

  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Achieving 50% renewable energy by 2030 is feasible with accelerated investment, storage solutions, and a determined shift of subsidies from fossil fuels to clean energy.

**[2015]** Q9. To what factors can the recent dramatic fall in equipment costs and tariff of solar energy be attributed? What implications does the trend have for the thermal power producers and the related industry? (200 words) 12.5
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHY | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Factors behind solar cost decline, Implications for thermal power producers, Energy transition dynamics

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The dramatic fall in solar energy costs over the past decade represents one of the most significant economic disruptions in the global energy sector, with far-reaching implications for traditional thermal power producers.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Factors behind cost decline: (1) Technology improvement — higher efficiency solar cells (mono-PERC, bifacial); (2) Manufacturing scale — Chinese mass production reduced module costs by 90%; (3) Competition — reverse auctions driving down tariffs; (4) Low interest rates reducing financing costs; (5) India's domestic manufacturing push reducing import dependence
      • Implications for thermal power producers: existing plants face revenue risk from merit-order displacement; new coal plants increasingly uneconomical; stranded asset risk (plants with 20-30 year lifespans); rising carbon taxes globally add to pressure
      • Related industry implications: coal mining (demand plateau/decline), coal-based equipment manufacturers, railway wagon manufacturers (coal transport)
      • Transition management needed: just transition for coal workers and communities; managing plant retirement schedules; ensuring grid stability during transition
      • India context: solar capacity target 500 GW by 2030; Coal India's declining growth projections; NTPC pivoting to renewables
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The solar cost revolution has made the energy transition economically inevitable — the challenge for thermal producers and policymakers is managing the transition smoothly while ensuring energy security and worker welfare.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 — Energy, environment, economy, GS2 — Governance, industrial policy
  🌐 Contemporary: India's solar capacity exceeded 80 GW; solar is now cheapest electricity source, fundamentally altering India's energy mix.

**[2013]** Q17. (b) What do you understand by Run-of-river hydroelectricity project ? How is it different from any other hydroelectricity project ? [100 words] 5
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 5m | ~100w*
  🏷️ Themes: Run-of-river hydroelectricity concept, Differences from conventional hydropower, Environmental advantages

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~100 words: intro 30w / body 80w / conclusion 25w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** A run-of-river (RoR) hydroelectricity project generates power from the natural flow of a river without creating large storage reservoirs, offering a lower-impact alternative to conventional dam-based hydropower.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • What is RoR: diverts a portion of river flow through a channel/tunnel to a powerhouse; uses kinetic energy of flowing water; minimal storage (only pondage for diurnal variation)
      • Difference from conventional hydropower: no large dam; no significant storage reservoir; power output varies with river flow (seasonal variation); less land submergence and displacement
      • Environmental advantages: lower ecological footprint, less methane from submerged vegetation, minimal displacement, maintains river ecology downstream
      • Limitations: no flood control benefit; power generation dependent on rainfall/snow melt (intermittent); limited peaking capacity
      • Examples in India: Tehri (storage) vs Nathpa Jhakri (partly RoR) vs Kishanganga (RoR, contested with Pakistan)
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Run-of-river projects offer a cleaner hydropower option but require careful hydrological assessment to ensure reliable power output given their dependence on natural river flow.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 — Energy, environment, GS1 — Physical geography, rivers
  🌐 Contemporary: Himachal Pradesh and Uttarakhand's hydropower expansion relies heavily on RoR projects; Kishanganga case (India-Pakistan) remains significant.

- Both 100,000 MW (installed capacity) and 660 billion kWh (generation) seem plausible for 2008. India's actual installed capacity was ~140,000-150,000 MW in 2008, making S1 wrong.

- Statement 2 (molasses as synthetic fertiliser feedstock) is wrong — molasses is used for ethanol, not synthetic chemical fertilisers. Students may confuse molasses-to-ethanol with fertiliser production.
- Students may guess the B.K. Chaturvedi Committee dealt with Centre-State relations or tax reforms — it was specifically about petroleum product pricing reforms.
- Potato and wheat contain starch that can be fermented to ethanol, but sugarcane is by far the most viable and widely used feedstock for ethanol production due to direct sucrose content.
