**[2025]** Q8. In line with the Directive Principles of State Policy enshrined in the Indian Constitution, the government has a constitutional obligation to ensure basic needs "Roti, Kapda aur Makan (Food, Clothes and Shelter)" – for the under-privileged. Pursuing this mandate, the district administration proposed clearing a portion of forest land to develop housing for the homeless and economically weaker sections of the society.  The proposed land, however, is an ecologically sensitive zone densely populated with age-old trees, medicinal plants and vital biodiversity. Besides, these forests help to regulate micro-climate and rainfalls; provide habitat for wildlife, support soil fertility and prevent land/soil erosion and sustain livelihoods of tribal and nomadic communities.  Inspite of the ecological and social costs, the administration argues in favour of the said proposal by highlighting that this very initiative addresses fundamental human rights as a critical welfare priority. Besides, it fulfils the government's duty to uplift and empower the poor through inclusive housing development. Further, these forest areas have become unsafe due to wild-animal threats and recurring human-wild life conflicts. Lastly, clearing forest-zones may help to curb anti-social elements allegedly using these areas as hideouts, thereby enhancing law and order.  (a) Can deforestation be ethically justified in the pursuit of social welfare objectives like, housing for the homeless ?  (b) What are the socio-economic, administrative and ethical challenges in balancing environmental conservation with human development ?  (c) What substantial alternatives or policy interventions can be proposed to ensure that both environmental integrity and human dignity are protected ? (Answer in 250 words) 20
  *Paper: GS4 | Dim: HOW | 20m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Deforestation and social welfare ethics, Environmental vs human rights balance

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The proposal to clear ecologically sensitive forest land for housing illustrates the most complex ethical terrain in governance — where fundamental human rights and environmental imperatives collide.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • (a) Can deforestation be ethically justified?: Only if no alternatives exist (last resort principle), harm is proportionate, and affected communities (tribals) consent — Forest Rights Act makes tribal consent mandatory; ecological destruction for housing violates intergenerational equity and environmental ethics
      • (b) Challenges: socioeconomic — alternative sites, transition costs; administrative — land availability, regularization of occupation; ethical — whose rights take priority, intergenerational equity vs immediate human need, tribal livelihood vs homeless rights
      • (c) Policy alternatives: use of degraded/wasteland for housing; vertical construction (apartments); PPP models for urban housing; slum rehabilitation in existing settlements; EWS housing in existing peri-urban areas; community forest management allows coexistence of tribal livelihood and housing
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Deforestation for housing is ethically unjustifiable when alternatives exist — the state must pursue integrated solutions that protect forest ecosystems, tribal rights, and the dignity of the homeless simultaneously.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS4 Ethics — Case Studies, GS3 Environment, Society — Vulnerable Sections
  🌐 Contemporary: India's forest clearance data and housing shortage in cities make this tension between environmental protection and housing rights an urgent and recurring policy challenge.
