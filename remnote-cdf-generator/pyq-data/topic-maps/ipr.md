**[Q2746]** [2018]
> India enacted The Geographical Indications of Goods (Registration and Protection) Act, 1999 in order to comply with the obligations to

     A) ILO
     B) IMF
     C) UNCTAD
  ✅ D) WTO

  ⚠️ **Trap:** ILO and UNCTAD deal with trade-related matters making them plausible distractors; GI protection is a TRIPS Agreement obligation under WTO.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Geographical Indications protection is mandated by TRIPS Agreement (Trade-Related Intellectual Property Rights) — a WTO agreement. India enacted GI Act 1999 to comply with TRIPS.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - GI Act 1999 enacted to comply with TRIPS Agreement obligations under WTO
     - TRIPS: Part II, Sections 3 (trademarks) and specifically Article 22-24 on Geographical Indications
     - India's GI examples: Darjeeling tea, Mysore silk, Alphonso mangoes, Kanchipuram sarees

> With reference to the 'National Intellectual Property Rights Policy', consider the following statements:  1. It reiterates India's commitment to the Doha Development Agenda and the TRIPS Agreement. 2.      A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
  ✅ C) Both 1 and 2
     D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 mentions DIPP (now DPIIT) as nodal agency — this is correct. Statement 1 about Doha Development Agenda commitment may be doubted by candidates unsure about India's TRIPS stance.
  💡 **Eliminate:** India's National IPR Policy (2016) reiterates commitment to Doha Development Agenda and TRIPS (S1 correct). DIPP (now DPIIT) is the nodal agency (S2 correct). Answer: C (Both).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - India's National IPR Policy 2016: 'Creative India; Innovative India' — balanced innovation with public interest
     - DPIIT (formerly DIPP) is the nodal department for India's IPR regime

**[2024]** Q5. What is the present world scenario of intellectual property rights with respect to life materials? Although, India is second in the world to file patents, still only a few have been commercialized. Explain the reasons behind this less commercialization.  (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: IPR policy, Innovation ecosystem, Technology commercialization

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Despite impressive patent filing numbers, India's innovation ecosystem struggles to translate intellectual property into commercial products, limiting the economic returns on R&D investment.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • World scenario: TRIPS mandates IP protection for life materials including microorganisms, plant varieties, genetic sequences
      • Convention on Biological Diversity vs TRIPS tensions around bio-prospecting
      • India's gap: low commercialization due to — absence of technology transfer offices, risk capital scarcity, industry-academia disconnect, regulatory barriers for biotech products
      • Weak linkage between CSIR/ICAR patents and industrial production
      • Solutions: National IPR Policy 2016 implementation, BIRAC for biotech commercialization, startups in deeptech
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India must build a complete innovation ecosystem from ideation to market, with dedicated technology transfer infrastructure, risk capital, and regulatory facilitation.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Biotechnology, R&D investment, Startup ecosystem
  🌐 Contemporary: National IPR Policy 2016 aims to strengthen commercialization; Startup India has improved but gaps remain.

**[2019]** Q15. How is the Government of India protecting traditional knowledge of medicine from patenting by pharmaceutical companies? (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: IPR and traditional knowledge, Bio-piracy, India's knowledge protection strategy

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's rich tradition of medicinal knowledge has been vulnerable to bio-piracy by pharmaceutical companies, prompting the government to develop innovative legal and technological safeguards.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Traditional Knowledge Digital Library (TKDL): digitized 3.6 lakh formulations from Ayurveda, Unani, Siddha, Yoga in five languages; shared with patent offices worldwide to prevent bio-piracy
      • Biological Diversity Act 2002: requires prior approval and benefit-sharing for use of biological resources and associated knowledge
      • TRIPS flexibilities: India uses TRIPS Article 27.3(b) exclusions to deny patents on traditional knowledge
      • Successful revocations: Turmeric (1997), Neem (2000), Basmati (partially) — landmark cases of preventing/revoking wrongful patents
      • Geographical Indications Act: protects products like Darjeeling tea, Basmati rice as GI tags
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's multi-pronged approach combining TKDL, Biodiversity Act, GI registration, and TRIPS-compliant patent law amendments has significantly strengthened protection of traditional knowledge against pharmaceutical bio-piracy.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 — IPR policy, GS3 — Science and technology, bio-piracy, GS2 — Bilateral trade disputes (TRIPS)
  🌐 Contemporary: Bio-piracy attempts continue; India's TKDL model is being emulated globally by developing countries.

**[2015]** Q14. India's Traditional Knowledge Digital Library (TKDL), which has a database containing formatted information on more than 2 million medicinal formulations is proving a powerful weapon in the country's fight against erroneous patents. Discuss the pros and cons of making this database publicly available under open-source licensing. (200 words) 12.5
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: VS | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: IPR and traditional knowledge, Biopiracy prevention, Open knowledge vs protection

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's TKDL represents a pioneering defensive publication strategy to prevent biopiracy, but making it publicly available involves complex trade-offs.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Pros of open-source: accelerates global research on traditional medicine, promotes innovation
      • Cons: risks commercialization by foreign entities without benefit-sharing; undermines Nagoya Protocol goals
      • Current restricted access: only shared with patent offices under MoU (USPTO, EPO, etc.)
      • Defensive use: prevents erroneous patents by serving as prior art documentation
      • Open-source concerns: AYUSH practitioners could lose monopoly; foreign patents on formulations
      • Alternative: Tiered access with ABS (Access and Benefit Sharing) framework under Nagoya Protocol
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** TKDL's power lies in its restricted use as defensive prior art; open-sourcing must be tied to robust ABS mechanisms to prevent exploitation.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Science & Technology, GS2 – International Relations, GS3 – Environment
  🌐 Contemporary: India's Biological Diversity Amendment Act 2023 and Nagoya Protocol ratification are directly relevant.

**[2014]** Q12. In a globalized world, Intellectual Property Rights assume significance and are a source of litigation. Broadly distinguish between the terms Copyrights, Patents and Trade Secrets.  (200 words) 12.5
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Distinguishing IPR types, Importance of IPR in globalized economy, Legal framework and challenges

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Intellectual Property Rights (IPR) protect different types of intangible assets through distinct legal mechanisms, and in a globalized knowledge economy, understanding these distinctions is critical.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Copyright: protects original literary, artistic, musical works; automatic protection; no registration required; 60 years post-author's life in India; examples: books, software, films
      • Patents: protect novel, non-obvious inventions/processes; requires registration with patent office; 20-year protection; mandates public disclosure — creates knowledge commons after expiry; examples: pharma compounds, engineering devices
      • Trade Secrets: confidential business information providing competitive advantage; no registration; unlimited duration; protection ends if secrecy is lost; examples: Coca-Cola formula, Google's algorithm
      • Key differences: Copyright (no disclosure needed), Patent (disclosure mandatory), Trade Secret (no disclosure ever)
      • Litigation hotspots: pharmaceutical patents (TRIPS vs access to medicines), tech patents (patent trolls), trade secret theft (corporate espionage)
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Each IPR type serves distinct innovation and economic purposes — robust IPR enforcement combined with fair use provisions balances innovation incentives with public access.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 — Science & Technology, IPR, GS2 — International Organizations, TRIPS Agreement, GS2 — Governance, policy framework
  🌐 Contemporary: India's patent laws (Section 3(d)) remain contentious in pharma; AI-generated content challenges copyright frameworks.

**[2013]** Q13. Bringing out the circumstances in 2005 which forced amendment to the section 3(d) in Indian Patent Law, 1970, discuss how it has been utilized by the Supreme Court in its judgement in rejecting Novratis' patent application for 'Glivec'. Discuss briefly the pros and cons of the decision. [200 words] 10
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: IP rights vs access to affordable medicines, India's pharmaceutical patent regime

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's 2005 amendment to Section 3(d) of the Patents Act, driven by TRIPS compliance pressures, became a landmark tool protecting generic medicine access, as demonstrated in the Novartis Glivec judgment.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • 2005 context: TRIPS compliance required India to recognize product patents; Section 3(d) was inserted as safeguard against evergreening
      • Section 3(d): bars patents for new forms of known substances unless significantly enhanced therapeutic efficacy is shown
      • SC judgment 2013: rejected Novartis patent for beta-crystalline form of imatinib — lacked enhanced efficacy over known compound
      • Pros of decision: generic Glivec access, public health protection, pharma pricing discipline
      • Cons: concern about reduced R&D investment incentives, innovation disincentive argument from MNCs
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The Section 3(d) provision and its judicial interpretation represent India's commitment to balancing IP protection with public health imperatives.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: Health policy, IR: TRIPS and WTO, Economy: Pharma sector
  🌐 Contemporary: COVID-19 highlighted the global importance of IP flexibilities; India's patent regime is a model for developing nations.
