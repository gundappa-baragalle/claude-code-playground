**[Q3476]** [2024]
> Which of the following statements are correct about the Constitution of India ?  1. Powers of the Municipalities are given in Part IX A of the Constitution. 2. Emergency provisions are given in Part XVIII of the Constitution. 3. Provisions related to the amendment of the Constitution are given in Part XX of the Constitution.  Select the answer using the code given below :

     A) 1 and 2 only
     B) 2 and 3 only
     C) 1 and 3 only
  ✅ D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may doubt any of the three; all three are correct constitutional facts: Part IXA (Municipalities), Part XVIII (Emergency provisions), Part XX (Amendment). All three are accurate.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Part IXA = Municipalities (74th Amendment); Part XVIII = Emergency Provisions (Articles 352-360); Part XX = Amendment of the Constitution (Article 368). All three correct. Answer: D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Part IX A (Articles 243P-243ZG): Municipalities — inserted by 74th Constitutional Amendment Act 1992
     - Part XVIII (Articles 352-360): Emergency Provisions — National, State, Financial emergency
     - Part XX (Article 368): Amendment of the Constitution

**[Q3147]** [2021]
> Which one of the following best defines the term 'State'?

  ✅ A) A community of persons permanently occupying a definite territory independent of external control and possessing an organized government
     B) A politically organized people of a definite territory and possessing an authority to govern them, maintain law and order, protect their natural rights and safeguard their means of sustenance
     C) A number of persons who have been living in a definite territory for a very long time with their own culture, tradition and government
     D) A society permanently living in a definite territory with a central authority, an executive responsible to the central authority and an independent judiciary

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Other options include partial elements of the state definition (territory, government) but miss key components; the trap is choosing an option that sounds comprehensive but lacks 'independence from external control'.
  💡 **Eliminate:** The classical definition of 'State' (Oppenheim/Garner): permanent population + definite territory + government + sovereignty (independence from external control) — option A captures all four elements.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - State = population + territory + government + sovereignty
     - Sovereignty means independence from external control — key differentiator

**[Q3024]** [2020]
> Consider the following statements:  1. The Constitution of India defines its 'basic structure' in terms of federalism, secularism, fundamental rights and democracy. 2. The Constitution of India provides for 'judicial review' to safeguard the citizens' liberties and to preserve the ideals on which the Constitution is based.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
     C) Both 1 and 2
  ✅ D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 1 is the trap — the Constitution does NOT explicitly define its 'basic structure'; the doctrine was judicially evolved in Kesavananda Bharati case (1973), not written in the text.
  💡 **Eliminate:** The basic structure doctrine is judicial creation (not defined in Constitution text) — Statement 1 is wrong. Statement 2 about judicial review is correct as it is implicit in fundamental rights enforcement.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Basic structure doctrine was propounded by Supreme Court in Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)
     - Judicial review is implicit in Articles 13, 32, 226 of the Constitution

**[Q2935]** [2019]
> With reference to the Constitution of India, consider the following statements:  1. No High Court shall have the jurisdiction to declare any central law to be constitutionally invalid. 2. An amendment to the Constitution of India cannot be called into question by the Supreme Court of India.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
     C) Both 1 and 2
  ✅ D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 1 is wrong — High Courts can and do declare central laws unconstitutional (e.g., under Article 226/227). Statement 2 is wrong — SC can review constitutional amendments under the Basic Structure doctrine (Kesavananda Bharati).
  💡 **Eliminate:** Both statements are incorrect: High Courts have judicial review power over central laws; Supreme Court can strike down constitutional amendments violating basic structure (Kesavananda 1973). Answer is D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - High Courts have power of judicial review under Articles 226-227
     - Kesavananda Bharati (1973): SC established Basic Structure doctrine — amendments can be reviewed
     - Supreme Court can strike down constitutional amendments violating basic structure

**[Q2762]** [2018]

     B) 2 and 4 only
  ✅ C) 1, 2 and 4 only

     - A.V. Dicey's Rule of Law has three pillars: supremacy of law, equality before law, and predominance of legal spirit
     - India's Constitution embodies Rule of Law through Part III (Fundamental Rights) and Article 13

**[2019]** Q12. "Parliament's power to amend the Constitution is a limited power and it cannot be enlarged into absolute power." In the light of this statement explain whether Parliament under Article 368 of the Constitution can destroy the Basic Structure of the Constitution by expanding its amending power?  (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHAT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Limits on Parliament's amending power under Article 368, Basic Structure doctrine as judicial safeguard, Balance between constitutional supremacy and parliamentary democracy

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The power to amend the Constitution under Article 368, while vast, is inherently limited by the principle that Parliament cannot use its amending power to destroy the very Constitution it derives its authority from.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Article 368: Parliament's amending power — wide but not unlimited (Kesavananda Bharati 1973)
      • Basic Structure elements: supremacy of Constitution, republican & democratic form of govt, secular character, separation of powers, judicial review
      • Doctrine of implied limitations: amending power cannot 'abrogate or emasculate' essential features
      • If Parliament could expand its own amending power infinitely — paradox of self-reference, destroying constitutional governance
      • Minerva Mills (1980): struck down 42nd Amendment clause that made amendments non-justiciable — reaffirmed limitations
      • Democratic rationale: Constitution is supreme; Parliament is creature of Constitution, not its master
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The Basic Structure doctrine represents India's constitutional genius — ensuring the Constitution remains a living document guiding Parliament, not a text Parliament can rewrite at will, thus preserving the foundational values of democracy, liberty, and rule of law.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 Polity, Constitutional law, Judicial independence
  🌐 Contemporary: Basic Structure doctrine remains relevant in challenges to constitutional amendments affecting federalism and fundamental rights.

**[2019]** Q1. Do you think that Constitution of India does not accept principle of strict separation of powers rather it is based on the principle of 'checks and balance'? Explain. (150 words) 10
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Constitutional design, Separation of powers, Checks and balances

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's Constitution deliberately rejects the rigidity of Montesquieu's strict separation of powers in favor of a system of functional interdependence underpinned by constitutional checks and balances.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Strict separation rejected: PM and Cabinet (executive) are also members of Parliament (legislature)
      • Overlap by design: Parliament controls executive through no-confidence vote; executive controls Parliament through dissolution
      • Judicial checks: SC can strike down laws (judicial review), issue writs, review executive action
      • Legislative checks: Parliament can impeach President, remove judges through address
      • Executive-judicial overlap: executive appoints judges (with collegium), President can pardon
      • Constitutional basis: no explicit 'separation of powers' provision unlike US
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's constitutional design of checks and balances creates a more nuanced governance architecture than strict separation of powers, enabling democratic accountability while preserving constitutional supremacy.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Polity — basic structure and constitutional amendments, Polity — Parliament and executive relationship, Governance — judicial independence
  🌐 Contemporary: Debates on judicial vs executive supremacy in appointments and legislative encroachment make this a live constitutional debate.

**[2016]** Q6. What was held in the Coelho case? In this context, can you say that judicial review is of key importance amongst the basic features of the Constitution?  (200 words) 12.5
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: IMPACT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Judicial review, Basic structure, 9th Schedule and property rights

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The Coelho case represents a landmark affirmation that judicial review of legislation on basic structure grounds is itself a basic feature of the Constitution, closing the loophole of 9th Schedule immunity for post-Kesavananda laws.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Case context: Tamil Nadu laws reserving 69% seats placed in 9th Schedule to avoid judicial review
      • Coelho holding: laws placed in 9th Schedule after April 24, 1973 (Kesavananda date) subject to basic structure review
      • Judicial review is a basic feature: SC confirmed it cannot be abrogated even by constitutional amendment
      • Limits on legislative power: Parliament cannot use constitutional amendments to destroy rights protected by basic structure
      • Implications: 9th Schedule no longer a safe harbor from judicial review for post-1973 inclusions
      • Courts can review even constitutional amendments if they violate basic structure
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The Coelho case definitively established judicial review as an inviolable basic feature that Parliament cannot circumvent through constitutional devices like the 9th Schedule, ensuring the supremacy of the Constitution over majoritarian legislative will.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Polity — constitutional amendments and limits, Polity — fundamental rights vs DPSP, Governance — reservation policy and judicial oversight
  🌐 Contemporary: Ongoing debates on OBC reservation laws placed in 9th Schedule and EWS reservation validity invoke the Coelho precedent.
