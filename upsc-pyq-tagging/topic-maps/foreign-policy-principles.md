**[Q4918]** [2025]
> India is one of the founding members of the International North-South Transport Corridor (INSTC), a multimodal transportation corridor, which will connect

  ✅ A) India to Central Asia to Europe via Iran
     B) India to Central Asia via China
     C) India to South-East Asia through Bangladesh and Myanmar
     D) India to Europe through Azerbaijan

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Option D (via Azerbaijan) is a partial route of INSTC but the full corridor also goes through Iran; option B (via China) confuses INSTC with other corridors.
  💡 **Eliminate:** INSTC connects India to Central Asia and Europe via Iran (using sea, rail, road). The main route is India → Iran → Caspian Sea → Central Asia/Russia → Europe. Answer: A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - INSTC: 7,200 km multimodal corridor connecting Mumbai to Moscow via Iran
     - India, Russia, and Iran are founding members (2000)

**[Q2433]** [2011]
> With reference to "Look East Policy" of India, consider the following statements  1. India wants to establish itself as an important regional player in the East Asian affairs. 2. India wants to plug the vacuum created by the termination of Cold War. 3. India wants to restore the historical and cultural ties with its neighbours in Southeast and East Asia.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 only
  ✅ B) 1 and 3 only
     C) 3 only
     D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 claims Look East aims to fill Cold War vacuum — this overstates the Cold War framing; Look East was primarily driven by economic motivations (ASEAN's economic dynamism) and Indian domestic reform agenda, not Cold War vacuum-filling.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Statement 1 (India as East Asian player — TRUE) and Statement 3 (restore historical cultural ties — TRUE, India shares ancient cultural-trade links with SE Asia). Statement 2 about Cold War vacuum is debatable — Look East was launched in 1991 for economic integration, not geopolitical positioning. Correct: B (1 and 3).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Look East Policy launched under PM Narasimha Rao in 1991 to economically integrate with ASEAN; upgraded to Act East in 2014
     - India's links with Southeast Asia predate colonialism — shared Buddhist heritage, trade routes, Angkor Wat (Hindu influence)

**[Q353]** [1997]
> Which one of the following is NOT a principle of “Panch-sheel” ?

  ✅ A) Non-alignment
     B) Peaceful Co-existence
     C) Mutual respect for each other’s territorial integrity and sovereignty
     D) Mutual non-interference in each other’s internal affairs

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Non-alignment is closely associated with India's foreign policy and Nehru's era, making it feel like it belongs to Panchsheel — but it is a separate principle.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Panchsheel (1954) has 5 principles: mutual respect for territory, non-aggression, non-interference, equality/mutual benefit, peaceful co-existence. Non-alignment is NOT one of them.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Panchsheel signed 1954 between India and China
     - 5 principles: sovereignty, non-aggression, non-interference, equality, peaceful coexistence

> With reference to India's projects on connectivity, consider the following statements:  1. East-West Corridor under Golden Quadrilateral Project connects Dibrugarh and Surat. 2. Trilateral Highway connects Moreh in Manipur and Chiang Mai in Thailand via Myanmar. 3. Bangladesh-China-India-Myanmar Economic Corridor connects Varanasi in Uttar Pradesh with Kunming in China.  How many of the above statements are correct?

     B) Only two
     C) All three
  ✅ D) None

  💡 **Eliminate:** East-West Corridor: Porbandar (Gujarat) to Silchar (Assam). Trilateral Highway: Moreh (India) to Mae Sot (Thailand) via Myanmar — not Chiang Mai. BCIM: Kolkata to Kunming — not Varanasi. All three wrong. Answer: None.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - East-West Corridor (NHDP): Porbandar–Silchar, not Dibrugarh–Surat
     - India-Myanmar-Thailand Trilateral Highway: Moreh (Manipur) to Mae Sot (Thailand), passing through Myanmar

**[Q4569]** [2023]
> Consider the following statements :  1. Statement-I : Recently, the United States of America (USA) and the European Union (EU) have launched the Trade and Technology Council'. 2. Statement-II: The USA and the EU claim that through this they are trying to bring technological progress and physical productivity under their control.  Which one of the following is correct in respect of the above statements?

     A) Both Statement-I and Statement-II are correct and Statement-II is the correct explanation for Statement-I
     B) Both Statement-I and Statement-II are correct and Statement-II is not the correct explanation for Statement-I
  ✅ C) Statement-I is correct but Statement-II is incorrect
     D) Statement-I is incorrect but Statement-II is correct

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement II is wrong: the US-EU TTC was launched to promote interoperability of standards, fair trade, and democratic tech governance — not to bring tech under their control (which is a mischaracterization).
  💡 **Eliminate:** Statement I is correct (TTC was launched); Statement II incorrectly characterizes TTC's goals as seeking monopolistic control — the TTC aims at setting standards and countering authoritarian tech. Answer: C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - US-EU Trade and Technology Council (TTC): launched 2021 to coordinate trade/tech standards, counter China's tech influence
     - TTC's stated goals: democratic governance of technology, supply chain security, not technological monopoly

**[2025]** Q19. "Energy security constitutes the dominant kingpin of India's foreign policy, and is linked with India's overarching influence in Middle Eastern countries." How would you integrate energy security with India's foreign policy trajectories in the coming years?
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Energy security, Foreign policy, Middle East diplomacy

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Energy security is the spine of India's foreign policy, and deepening engagement with Middle Eastern countries must be integrated into a coherent long-term energy diplomacy strategy.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Current dependence: ~85% crude from Middle East/Gulf — Saudi, UAE, Iraq top suppliers
      • Diplomatic tools: Modi's Gulf outreach — defence, investment, diaspora links (9 million Indians in Gulf)
      • Integration trajectory: rupee-dirham trade settlement, IPI pipeline revival, green hydrogen corridors
      • Future: link ISA solar projects to Gulf sovereign wealth funds for energy transition investment
      • Diversification: reduced dependence through renewables while maintaining stable Gulf relationships
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India must evolve from transactional energy buyer to strategic energy partner with Gulf states, integrating renewable energy diplomacy into its foreign policy toolkit.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: IR, GS3: Energy security, Economy: External sector
  🌐 Contemporary: India-UAE CEPA, India-Saudi Vision 2030 alignment, and Gulf's hydrogen export potential are highly current.

**[2024]** Q10. Critically analyse India's evolving diplomatic, economic and strategic relations with the Central Asian Republics (CARs) highlighting their increasing significance in regional and global geopolitics.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: India-Central Asia relations, Regional geopolitics, Energy security

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Central Asia's strategic location, energy wealth, and historical connections with India make the region's five republics increasingly vital to India's geopolitical and economic interests.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Diplomatic: India-Central Asia Summit (2022) upgraded relations; India supports UNSC reform alongside CARs; SCO membership provides formal platform
      • Economic: energy imports (Kazakhstan oil), uranium (for nuclear programme from Kazakhstan), trade via INSTC and Chabahar
      • Strategic: CARs as buffer against China-Pakistan convergence; countering China's BRI narrative through alternative connectivity
      • Challenges: no direct land border with CARs (Pakistan/China blockage); INSTC incomplete; limited trade volumes
      • Recent significance: Russia-Ukraine war increased geopolitical importance of Central Asia as alternative supply chain hub
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Central Asia is no longer a strategic afterthought for India — as global geopolitics reshapes, the CARs offer India critical openings in energy, connectivity, and multipolarity.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 IR, GS3 Energy, Internal Security
  🌐 Contemporary: India-Central Asia Virtual Summit (2022) and SCO Samarkand Summit (2022) marked India's intensified engagement.

**[2023]** Q19. 'The expansion and strengthening of NATO and a stronger US-Europe strategic partnership works well for India.'  What is your opinion about this statement? Give reasons and examples to support your answer.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: IMPACT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Alliance politics, Strategic autonomy, Multipolarity

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** NATO expansion and a stronger US-Europe partnership present mixed implications for India — beneficial as a counterbalance to China, but potentially complicating India's Russia ties.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Benefits: stronger US-Europe axis constrains China, reduces China's bandwidth to challenge India
      • US-Europe stability allows US to focus on Indo-Pacific security — aligns with India's interests
      • Complications: India-Russia arms dependence vs Western pressure to distance from Russia
      • India's balancing: benefited from NATO deterrence against China but refuses anti-Russia NATO stance
      • Multipolarity preference: India prefers multiple power centers rather than NATO-dominated unipolarity
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India benefits from NATO cohesion as an indirect check on China but must navigate carefully to protect its strategic autonomy and Russia relationship.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: IR, GS3: Defence, Economy: Defence procurement
  🌐 Contemporary: Russia-Ukraine war and India's vote abstentions at UNGA on Ukraine make this analysis extremely relevant.

**[2022]** Q19. How will I2U2 (India, Israel, UAE and USA) grouping transform India's position in global politics?
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: IMPACT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: I2U2 as new minilateral grouping, India's enhanced role in West Asian geopolitics, Economic and strategic gains for India

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The I2U2 grouping — an unlikely but strategically coherent coalition of India, Israel, UAE and USA — signals India's expanding geopolitical footprint and its integration into evolving West Asian security and economic architecture.

      • Formation context: Enabled by Abraham Accords (2020) normalizing Israel-UAE relations; India already strong bilateral ties with all three
      • Agenda: Clean energy, food security, water, space, transportation, health — practical, economically focused unlike QUAD's security orientation

  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 IR, GS3 Economy, GS2 Governance
  🌐 Contemporary: I2U2's first project — Israeli food parks in India with UAE investment — is underway; IMEC announced at G20 2023 builds on its vision.

**[2019]** Q20. 'What introduces friction into the ties between India and the United States is that Washington is still unable to find for India a position in its global strategy, which would satisfy India's national self-esteem and ambitions.' Explain with suitable examples.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHY | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: India's strategic autonomy, India-US trust deficit, India's global positioning

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Despite convergent democratic values and growing strategic interests, India-US relations remain episodically fractious because Washington struggles to accommodate India's insistence on strategic autonomy within its global alliance framework.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • US expects treaty-ally-like reliability; India insists on case-by-case, interest-based alignment
      • CAATSA friction: India's S-400 purchase from Russia defied US pressure
      • Trade disputes: US GSP withdrawal, India's tariff barriers on Harley-Davidson
      • H-1B visa restrictions affecting Indian IT professionals
      • India-Pakistan issue: US historically favored Pakistan as a strategic partner (Cold War era)
      • Convergence: Quad, AUKUS adjacency, defence deals (BECA, LEMOA, COMCASA signed)
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Bridging the strategic vision gap requires the US to recognize India's value as an independent democratic partner rather than expecting full alignment, allowing the relationship to mature on the basis of shared interests and mutual respect.
  🔗 Interlinkages: IR — Quad and Indo-Pacific strategy, IR — Russia-Ukraine war and India's neutrality, Economy — US-India trade relations
  🌐 Contemporary: India-US iCET (Initiative on Critical and Emerging Technology) and defence industrial cooperation represent recent efforts to deepen the partnership.

**[2016]** Q18. Evaluate the economic and strategic dimensions of India's Look East Policy in the context of the post Cold War international scenario.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: IMPACT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Look East Policy dimensions, Economic and strategic rationale, Post-Cold War foreign policy

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's Look East Policy, initiated in 1991, represented a strategic reorientation towards Southeast Asia driven by both economic imperatives of liberalization and the post-Cold War geopolitical reconfiguration.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Economic dimension: access to ASEAN markets, FDI attraction, trade diversification away from traditional partners, connectivity projects (Kaladan, India-Myanmar-Thailand highway)
      • Strategic dimension: countering China's expanding influence in Southeast Asia and Indian Ocean; engaging major powers (US, Japan, Australia) through multilateral forums
      • Post-Cold War context: Soviet collapse ended India's non-aligned comfort zone, necessitating new partnerships; ASEAN's economic dynamism provided opportunity
      • Phase II (Act East, 2014): broader scope including Northeast India development, maritime security, cultural diplomacy
      • Outcomes: India-ASEAN FTA (2010), East Asia Summit membership, BIMSTEC revitalization
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Look East Policy transformed India's engagement with Asia from peripheral to central, establishing foundations for today's Indo-Pacific strategy and multi-alignment.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: IR and India's foreign policy, GS3: Trade policy, GS2: Northeast India development
  🌐 Contemporary: Act East Policy continues under PM Modi with the ASEAN centrality principle and Indo-Pacific framework.

**[2015]** Q19. Project 'Mausam' is considered a unique foreign policy initiative of the Indian Government to improve relationship with its neighbours. Does the project have a strategic dimension? Discuss.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: IMPACT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Cultural diplomacy, Indian Ocean strategy, India's soft power

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Project Mausam, leveraging India's historical Indian Ocean maritime heritage, serves simultaneously as a soft power instrument and a subtle strategic counter-narrative to China's Maritime Silk Road.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Foreign policy dimension: reconnects India with 39 Indian Ocean littoral states through shared monsoon-trade heritage
      • Strategic dimension: positions India as the natural Indian Ocean power with deep historical roots; counters China's MSRI narrative

  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Project Mausam's cultural framing provides strategic depth to India's Indian Ocean engagement by combining historical legitimacy with contemporary diplomatic objectives.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: International relations — India's foreign policy, GS1: History — ancient trade routes, GS3: Infrastructure — Sagarmala
  🌐 Contemporary: India's Indo-Pacific vision and QUAD membership make Indian Ocean primacy a core strategic priority.

**[2013]** Q24. What is meant by Gujral doctrine? Does it have any relevance today? Discuss.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: India's neighborhood diplomacy, Non-reciprocity principle, Regional cooperation

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The Gujral Doctrine's principle of non-reciprocal generosity toward smaller neighbors remains a philosophically relevant — though politically challenged — framework for India's neighborhood diplomacy.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Five principles: no interference, non-reciprocity, mutual respect, peaceful resolution of disputes, no use of territory against neighbors
      • Contemporary relevance: Neighbourhood First Policy under Modi echoes Gujral's spirit
      • Success: Bangladesh relationship (Teesta pending), Nepal connectivity, Sri Lanka assistance
      • Limitations: Pakistan incompatibility — doctrine explicitly excluded Pakistan for reciprocity
      • China's growing presence in India's neighborhood creates new pressures
      • India's vaccine diplomacy (2021) — operationalized doctrine's generosity principle
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While the Gujral Doctrine's philosophical core remains valid, it requires contextual adaptation to address asymmetric power dynamics, China's growing neighborhood presence and the need for balanced reciprocity.
  🔗 Interlinkages: IR — India-Pakistan relations, IR — SAARC and regional cooperation, IR — India-China competition in South Asia
  🌐 Contemporary: Neighbourhood First Policy, Vaccine Maitri and India-Maldives relations continuously test the doctrine's applicability.
