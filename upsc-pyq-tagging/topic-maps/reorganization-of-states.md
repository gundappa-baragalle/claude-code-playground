**[Q4908]** [2025]
> Consider the following pairs :  State                   Description I. Arunachal Pradesh : The capital is named after a fort, and the State has two National Parks II. Nagaland : The State came into existence on the basis of a Constitutional Amendment Act III. Tripura : Initially a Part 'C' State, it became a centrally administered territory with the reorganization of States in 1956 and later attained the status of a full-fledged State  How many of the above pairs are correctly matched?

     A) Only one
     B) Only two
  ✅ C) All the three
     D) None

  ⚠️ **Trap:** All three statements contain plausible-sounding but subtly wrong details. Tripura was initially a Part 'C' State under the Constitution; Nagaland was created via 13th Constitutional Amendment Act (not just reorganization); Arunachal Pradesh capital Itanagar has 'Ita Fort' (Ita = brick) giving its name.
  💡 **Eliminate:** I: Itanagar named after Ita Fort; AP has 2 NPs (Namdapha, Mouling) — correct. II: Nagaland created by 13th Amendment 1962 — correct. III: Tripura was Part C state then centrally administered territory — correct. All three correct. Answer: C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Itanagar (Arunachal Pradesh capital) named after Ita Fort; state has Namdapha and Mouling National Parks
     - Nagaland created by 13th Constitutional Amendment Act, 1962
     - Tripura was initially Part C State, became UT in 1956, full State in 1972

**[Q1979]** [2008]
> Which was the Capital of Andhra State when it was made a separate State in the year 1953?

     A) Guntur
  ✅ B) Kurnool
     C) Nellore
     D) Warangal

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Guntur and Nellore are both significant AP cities, and students may expect the first capital to be a coastal city or a larger city like Guntur.
  💡 **Eliminate:** When Andhra State was carved out of Madras State in 1953, Kurnool was made its first capital. Answer: B.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Andhra State formed 1953 — first state created on linguistic basis; Kurnool was capital
     - Andhra Pradesh formed 1956 (States Reorganization Act) with Hyderabad as capital

**[Q1870]** [2007]
> Which one of the following is the correct chronological order of the formation of the following as full States of the Indian Union?

     A) Sikkim-Arunachal Pradesh-Nagaland-Haryana
  ✅ B) Nagaland-Haryana-Sikkim-Arunachal Pradesh
     C) Sikkim-Haryana-Nagaland-Arunachal Pradesh
     D) Nagaland-Arunachal Pradesh-Sikkim-Haryana

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Haryana (1966) and Sikkim (1975) are often confused in relative order; Nagaland (1963) is easily placed first, but Arunachal Pradesh (1987) comes after Sikkim.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Nagaland (1963) → Haryana (1966) → Sikkim (1975) → Arunachal Pradesh (1987). Answer: B.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Nagaland — 16th state (1963)
     - Haryana — carved from Punjab (1966)
     - Sikkim — 22nd state (1975)
     - Arunachal Pradesh — full statehood in 1987

**[Q1706]** [2006]
> Which one of the following pairs is not correctly matched?

     A) States Reorganization Act : Andhra Pradesh
     B) Treaty of Yandabu : Assam
     C) State of Bilaspur : Himachal Pradesh
  ✅ D) Year 1996 : Gujarat becomes a State

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Gujarat became a state in 1960 (not 1996). Option D (Year 1996: Gujarat becomes a State) is clearly wrong — Gujarat was carved from Bombay State in 1960. Students who don't know the exact year fall for this.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Gujarat became a state on May 1, 1960 (from Bombay State), NOT 1996. Option D is incorrectly matched. Answer: D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Gujarat formed May 1, 1960 (with Maharashtra) from Bombay State
     - States Reorganization Act 1956 created Andhra Pradesh (correct)
     - Treaty of Yandabu 1826 ceded Assam to British (correct)
     - Bilaspur merged into Himachal Pradesh 1954 (correct)

  ✅ B) A-5, B-4, C-3, D-1

### GS1

**[2022]** Q11. The political and administrative reorganization of states and territories has been a continuous ongoing process since the mid-nineteenth century. Discuss with examples.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: State reorganisation, Linguistic federalism, Administrative history

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The reorganisation of India's states and territories has been a continuous process since the 19th century, reflecting the evolving interplay of language, identity, development, and administrative logic.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Colonial period: Presidencies, provinces — administrative convenience, not cultural logic
      • Post-independence pressure: linguistic statehood demand (Andhra, 1953)
      • States Reorganisation Act 1956: linguistic basis formalised
      • Later reorganisations: Jharkhand, Uttarakhand (developmental/tribal demands); Telangana (regional identity)
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** State reorganisation in India has consistently been a democratic response to aspirations for cultural recognition, administrative efficiency, and developmental equity.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 — Post-Independence India, GS2 — Polity, Federalism
  🌐 Contemporary: Demands for separate states (Vidarbha, Purvanchal, Bodoland) keep this issue alive.

**[2018]** Q12. Discuss whether formation of new states in recent times is beneficial or not for the economy of India.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: IMPACT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Economic development of new states, Administrative efficiency, Political dynamics of state formation, Federalism and regional aspirations

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The formation of new states in India raises fundamental questions about whether smaller administrative units lead to faster economic development or create additional fiscal burdens.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Benefits: Better governance and administration, focused regional development, addressing aspirations of marginalized communities (e.g., tribal regions in Jharkhand/Chhattisgarh)
      • Economic gains: Smaller states like Uttarakhand and Chhattisgarh showed faster GSDP growth post-formation due to focused industrial policies
      • Challenges: Higher per-capita administrative costs, dependence on central transfers, loss of economies of scale for parent states
      • Fiscal impact: New states often require special category status or additional grants increasing fiscal burden on Centre
      • Case study: Telangana formation (2014) — resource disputes over Krishna river water and Hyderabad revenues between AP and Telangana
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** State formation can be beneficial when driven by genuine developmental needs and regional aspirations, but must be accompanied by robust fiscal frameworks to ensure viability.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: Federalism and centre-state relations, GS3: Economic development and regional disparities
  🌐 Contemporary: Demands for new states (Vidarbha, Poorvanchal, Bodoland) continue to emerge driven by development and identity politics.
