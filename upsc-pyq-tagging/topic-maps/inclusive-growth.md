**[Q3758]** [2019]
> In a given year in India, official poverty lines are higher in some States than in others because

     A) poverty rates vary from State to State
  ✅ B) price levels vary from State to State
     C) Gross State Product varies from State to State
     D) quality of public distribution varies from State to State

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Option A (poverty rates vary) is a circular answer — poverty lines define poverty, so this doesn't explain the variation. Options C and D are plausible but not the direct cause.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Official poverty lines in India are based on the cost of a basic consumption basket. Since price levels vary across states, the same basket costs different amounts in different states. Answer: B.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - India's poverty line is state-specific, based on per capita consumption expenditure needed to meet basic needs at local prices
     - High-cost states (e.g., Kerala, Himachal) have higher poverty lines than low-cost states

**[2020]** Q1. Explain intra-generational and inter-generational issues of equity from the perspective of inclusive growth and sustainable development.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Equity and justice in development, Sustainable development, Inclusive growth

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Equitable development requires addressing both the disparities among people alive today (intra-generational equity) and the moral obligation to preserve resources and opportunities for future generations (inter-generational equity).
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Intra-generational equity: reducing inequality within the current generation — between rich and poor, urban and rural, across genders and regions; SDG 10 (reduced inequalities) directly addresses this
      • Inter-generational equity: development today should not deplete natural capital, pollute environments, or create debt burdens that compromise future generations' quality of life
      • Linkage with inclusive growth: growth must be broad-based, generating employment and reducing poverty (intra) while being environmentally sustainable (inter)
      • India's challenges: intra — SC/ST/OBC/women gaps; inter — groundwater depletion, soil degradation, unsustainable fiscal deficits
      • Policy instruments: progressive taxation, green budgeting, carbon pricing, social protection floors balance both equity dimensions
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Inclusive growth demands simultaneous attention to present-day disparities and long-term sustainability, making these two equity dimensions complementary rather than competing priorities.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3: Environment, sustainable development, GS2: Social justice, governance, Economy: fiscal policy, public debt
  🌐 Contemporary: India's commitment to Net Zero by 2070 and SDG targets embodies both intra- and inter-generational equity imperatives.

      • Investment in economics: addition to capital stock; gross investment includes depreciation replacement; net investment = actual capacity addition
      • Capital formation: creation of physical productive assets — machinery, infrastructure, buildings
      • PPP rationale: supplements public finance, brings private efficiency, risk transfer to private sector
      • Concession agreement design considerations: project risk allocation (construction, revenue, force majeure), tariff revision mechanism
      • User charge vs availability payment: revenue risk with concessionaire vs government; different incentive structures
      • Safeguards: step-in rights (lenders), termination payments, performance guarantees, dispute resolution mechanism
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Well-designed concession agreements that balance risk sharing and ensure investor confidence are critical to attracting and sustaining private investment in infrastructure.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Economy, GS2 – Governance, GS3 – Infrastructure
  🌐 Contemporary: NIP (National Infrastructure Pipeline) and PM Gati Shakti rely extensively on PPP models.
