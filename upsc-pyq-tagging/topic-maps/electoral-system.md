**[Q4750]** [2024]
> Consider the following statements regarding 'Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam' :  1. Provisions will come into effect from the 18th Lok Sabha. 2. This will be in force for 15 years after becoming an Act. 3. There are provisions for the reservation of seats for Scheduled Castes Women within the quota reserved for the Scheduled Castes.  Which of the statements given above are correct ?

     A) 1, 2 and 3
     B) 1 and 2 only
  ✅ C) 2 and 3 only
     D) 1 and 3 only

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 1 says provisions come into effect from 18th Lok Sabha — this is wrong; the act requires delimitation before implementation, making 18th Lok Sabha too early.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Statement 1 is wrong — implementation requires delimitation (post-2026 census), so 18th Lok Sabha cannot have women's reservation. Statements 2 (15 years sunset clause) and 3 (SC women's sub-quota) are correct.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam (106th Amendment) provides 33% reservation for women in Lok Sabha and state assemblies
     - Implementation requires fresh delimitation post-2026 census; has 15-year sunset clause with SC/ST women's sub-quota

**[Q4562]** [2023]
> Consider the following statements in respect of election to the President of India:  1. The members nominated to either House of the Parliament or the Legislative Assemblies of States are also eligible to be included in the Electoral College. 2. Higher the number of elective Assembly seats, higher is the value of vote of each MLA of that State. 3. The value of vote of each MLA of Madhya Pradesh is greater than that of Kerala. 4. The value of vote of each MLA of Puducherry is higher than that of Arunachal Pradesh because the ratio of total population to total number of elective seats in Puducherry is greater as compared to Arunachal Pradesh.  How many of the above statements are correct?

  ✅ A) Only one
     B) Only two
     C) Only three
     D) All four

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 ('higher number of assembly seats = higher MLA vote value') is wrong — value of MLA vote = state population / (number of assembly seats × 1000); more seats actually means LOWER per-MLA value.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Only statement 4 is correct (Puducherry MLA vote value higher than Arunachal Pradesh due to higher population-to-seat ratio); statements 1 (nominated members excluded from electoral college), 2 (more seats = higher value — wrong), 3 (MP MLA > Kerala MLA — debatable but generally wrong) are incorrect.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Nominated members of Parliament or state assemblies are NOT part of the Presidential Electoral College (Article 54)
     - Value of MLA vote = State population ÷ (Total elected assembly seats × 1000); more seats dilutes individual MLA vote value

**[Q3417]** [2017]
> For election to the Lok Sabha, a nomination paper can be filed by

     A) anyone residing in India.
     B) a resident of the constituency from which the election is to be contested.
  ✅ C) any citizen of India whose name appears in the electoral roll of a constituency.
     D) any citizen of India.

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Option D ('any citizen of India') seems logical but misses the key requirement — the candidate must be a registered voter in SOME constituency (not necessarily the one contested). Option B restricts it too narrowly to the specific constituency.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Under Section 4 of RPA 1951, a person can contest from any constituency but must be registered as a voter somewhere in India. Answer is C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Candidate must be registered voter in any constituency in India, not necessarily the one contested
     - Section 4 RPA 1951 lays down qualifications for Lok Sabha members
     - A candidate can contest from any constituency but needs voter registration

**[Q3446]** [2017]
> Right to vote and to be elected in India is a

     A) Fundamental Right
     B) Natural Right
  ✅ C) Constitutional Right
     D) Legal Right

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students often confuse 'constitutional right' with 'fundamental right'; right to vote is enshrined in constitution via Articles 326, 243D etc. but is NOT a Fundamental Right under Part III.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Right to vote is under Article 326 of the Constitution (Part XV) — it is a constitutional right, not a fundamental right. Fundamental Rights are under Articles 12–35.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Right to vote: Constitutional Right under Article 326, not Fundamental Right
     - Right to vote can be regulated by Parliament under Representation of People Act
     - It is also a legal right as governed by RPA 1951

**[Q2845]** [2014]
> Which one of the following Schedules of the Constitution of India contains provisions regarding anti-defection?

     A) Second Schedule
     B) Fifth Schedule
     C) Eighth Schedule
  ✅ D) Tenth Schedule

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students confuse Tenth Schedule (anti-defection) with Second Schedule (salaries of constitutional officials) or Eighth Schedule (languages).
  💡 **Eliminate:** Tenth Schedule was added by 52nd Amendment 1985 to prevent defection; remember: Ten = Tenth Schedule = anti-defection law — answer is D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Tenth Schedule = Anti-Defection Law (added by 52nd Amendment, 1985)
     - Speaker/Chairman decides defection cases under Tenth Schedule

**[Q64]** [1995]
> Who among the following have the right to vote in the elections to both Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha ?

     A) Elected members of the Lower House of the Parliament
     B) Elected members of the Upper House of the Parliament
     C) Elected members of the Upper House of the State Legislature
  ✅ D) Elected members of the Lower House of the State Legislature

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students confuse who votes for Rajya Sabha vs Lok Sabha. Elected members of the Lower House of STATE legislature vote for Rajya Sabha but not Lok Sabha. General public votes for Lok Sabha.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Rajya Sabha is elected by elected members of State Legislative Assemblies (Lower Houses of State Legislatures). Lok Sabha is elected by general public. Only elected MLAs vote for both indirectly through Rajya Sabha elections.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Rajya Sabha members elected by State Legislative Assembly (Vidhan Sabha) members via STV
     - Lok Sabha directly elected by voters; Rajya Sabha indirectly elected by MLAs

**[Q4740]** [2024]
> How many Delimitation Commissions have been constituted by the Government of India till December 2023 ?

     A) One
     B) Two
     C) Three
  ✅ D) Four

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may answer 'Three' (1952, 1963, 1973) missing the fourth Delimitation Commission of 2002. Four commissions have been constituted in total.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Delimitation Commissions: 1st (1952 Act), 2nd (1963 Act), 3rd (1973 Act), 4th (2002 Act) — four commissions in total up to December 2023.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Four Delimitation Commissions constituted: 1952, 1963, 1973, and 2002
     - Delimitation determines constituency boundaries; 4th Commission (2002) report implemented before 2008 elections

**[Q2545]** [2012]
> With reference to the Delimitation Commission, consider the following statements:  1. The orders of the Delimitation Commission cannot be challenged in a Court of Law. 2. When the orders of the Delimitation Commission are laid before the Lok Sabha or State Legislative Assembly, they cannot effect any modifications in the orders.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
  ✅ C) Both 1 and 2
     D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may think courts can review Delimitation Commission orders since they can review most government actions; the finality of DC orders is a key exception.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Both statements are correct: Delimitation Commission orders are final and not subject to judicial review (Article 329), and Parliament/State Assemblies cannot modify them.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Article 329 bars court challenge to Delimitation Commission orders
     - DC orders are laid before Parliament but cannot be modified

**[Q776]** [2000]
> A college student desires to get elected to the Municipal Council of his city. The validity of his nomination would depend on the important condition, among others, that

     A) he obtains permission from the Principal of his college
     B) he is a member of a political party
  ✅ C) his name figures in the Voters’ List
     D) he files a declaration owing allegiance to the Constitution of India

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may think party membership or principal's permission are required; the only legal requirement is being a registered voter (name in voters' list) in the constituency.

     - No party membership required to contest local body elections in India

     A) Bihar

  ✅ D) Madhya Pradesh

**[Q387]** [1997]
> Proportional representation is NOT necessary in a country where

     A) there are no reserved constituencies
  ✅ B) a two-party system has developed
     C) the first-past-post system prevails
     D) there is a fusion of Presidential and Parliamentary forms of governments

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Options A, C, and D describe features that exist even where PR is used — only a two-party system makes PR unnecessary as two parties adequately represent the political spectrum.
  💡 **Eliminate:** PR is designed to ensure minority representation where multiple parties exist. In a two-party system, voters' choices are adequately captured without PR. Answer: B.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Proportional representation ensures fair seat-to-vote ratio; unnecessary when only two parties represent all political views
     - USA and UK function without PR due to dominant two-party/two-bloc systems

**[Q432]** [1997]
> The Dinesh Goswami Committee recommended

     A) the constitution of state-level election commissions
     B) List System of election to the Lok Sabha
  ✅ C) governmental funding of parliamentary elections
     D) a ban on the candidature of independent candidates in the parliamentary elections

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may confuse Dinesh Goswami Committee with Indrajit Gupta Committee (which recommended state funding of elections) or with the Tarkunde Committee. Goswami Committee (1990) recommended government funding specifically for parliamentary elections.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Dinesh Goswami Committee (1990) made key recommendations on electoral reforms including: government funding of elections, anti-defection law refinements, use of state machinery. Government funding of parliamentary elections = answer C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Dinesh Goswami Committee (1990) recommended governmental funding of elections to reduce corruption
     - Indrajit Gupta Committee (1998) also recommended partial state funding of elections

> Consider the table given below providing some details of the results of the election to the Karnataka State Legislative . In terms of electoral analysis, the voter-seat distortion is to be explained as the result of the adoption of the Political Party | Percentage of popular votes obtained | Number of seats secured Janata Dal | 3.6 | 1.16 Congress | 3.1 | 3.5 BJP | 2.0.4 | 4.0

**[Q4188]** [2021]
> Consider the following statements :  1. In India, there is no law restricting the candidates from contesting in one Lok Sabha election from three constituencies. 2. In 1991 Lok Sabha Election, Shri Devi Lal contested from three Lok Sabha constituencies. 3. As per the existing rules, if a candidate contests in one Lok Sabha election from many constituencies, his/her party should bear the cost of bye- elections to the constituencies vacated by him/her in the event of him/her winning in all the constituencies.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
     C) 1 and 3
     D) 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** The law now restricts contesting from more than 2 constituencies (not 3); statement 1 is wrong; Devi Lal may have contested from two, not three; cost of bye-elections is borne by the candidate not the party.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Current law (RPA Act) allows contesting from maximum 2 constituencies; cost of bye-elections after winning from multiple seats is borne by the winning candidate. All statements need verification.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Representation of People Act limits contesting to 2 constituencies (not 3)
     - If a candidate wins both seats, must vacate one, leading to bye-election at candidate's expense

**[Q398]** [1997]
> In which one of the following countries are 75 per cent of seats in both Houses of Parliament filled oh the basis of first-past-the-post system and 25 per cent on the basis of Proportional Representation system of elections ?

  ✅ A) Germany
     B) Italy
     C) France
     D) Russia

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Germany uses a mixed electoral system (FPTP + PR) but in a 50-50 ratio for most of its history; the 75-25 split described here is often confused with Russia or Japan.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Germany uses a mixed member proportional system — approximately 50% FPTP and 50% PR (not 75-25); however, the answer key indicates Germany (A), suggesting the question refers to an older or specific feature of German Bundestag elections.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Germany's Bundestag uses mixed member proportional representation — half FPTP constituency seats, half party list PR
     - Russia also uses a mixed system but proportions differ

### GS2

**[2024]** Q1. Examine the need for electoral reforms as suggested by various committees with particular reference to "one nation - one election" principle.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Electoral reform, One Nation One Election, Governance efficiency

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Electoral reforms in India have been long debated to address the infirmities of a system that burdens governance, finances, and democratic participation — with 'One Nation One Election' emerging as its most ambitious proposal.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Need for reforms: frequent elections create governance disruption (MCC freeze), high costs (₹1 lakh crore estimated), voter fatigue
      • Key committee recommendations: Dinesh Goswami Committee (party-state funding), Vohra Committee (criminalization), Law Commission 1999 (simultaneous elections), Kovind Committee 2024 (ONOE implementation roadmap)
      • ONOE rationale: reduce policy paralysis, cut election expenditure, ensure stable governance for full term
      • Concerns about ONOE: threatens federalism (one election may entrench Centre over states), logistical challenges (constitutional amendments needed — Articles 83, 85, 172, 174, 356)
      • Other needed reforms: state funding of elections, anti-defection law reform, inner-party democracy
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Electoral reforms must balance efficiency with the democratic vitality that frequent accountability cycles — however disruptive — provide to India's federal polity.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 Polity, GS4 Ethics and Governance, Federalism
  🌐 Contemporary: Kovind Committee submitted ONOE report in March 2024; Constitution (129th Amendment) Bill introduced in December 2024.

**[2017]** Q3. "Simultaneous election to the Lok Sabha and the State Assemblies will limit the amount of time and money spent in electioneering but it will reduce the government's accountability to the people." Discuss.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: VS | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Simultaneous elections debate, Federal accountability, Electoral reform

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The proposal for simultaneous elections presents a genuine dilemma between administrative efficiency and the democratic principle of governmental accountability to the electorate.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Benefits: saves election expenditure, reduces political polarization, allows governments longer uninterrupted governance
      • Accountability concern: if a government falls mid-term, elections may not happen for years, reducing responsiveness
      • Federal complication: state governments may be forced to hold elections on national timetable, undermining state autonomy
      • Constitutional challenges: amendments to Articles 83, 85, 172, 174 required
      • International practice: South Africa and Sweden have simultaneous elections but with different constitutional frameworks
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Simultaneous elections can reduce costs but must be implemented with safeguards ensuring that democratic accountability is not sacrificed at the altar of efficiency.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 – Electoral system, Federalism, Constitutional amendments
  🌐 Contemporary: Ram Nath Kovind committee (2024) recommended simultaneous elections; political debate continues.

**[2017]** Q14. To enhance the quality of democracy in India the Election Commission of India has proposed electoral reforms in 2016. What are the suggested reforms and how far are they significant to make democracy successful?
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: IMPACT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Electoral reforms, Democracy quality, Campaign finance

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The Election Commission's 2016 reform proposals sought to address structural weaknesses in India's electoral process — from money power to political criminalization — that erode democratic quality.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • ECI reform proposals: state funding of elections, stricter disclosure of campaign finances, criminalization of paid news
      • Decriminalization: ECI recommended disqualifying candidates with serious criminal charges before conviction
      • NOTA expansion: including in Rajya Sabha/Legislative Council elections
      • Simultaneous elections: recommended to reduce governance disruption from perpetual election cycle
      • Electoral bond scheme concerns: ECI flagged transparency issues with anonymous electoral bonds
      • Significance: structural reforms needed to counter money/muscle power influence on democracy
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Electoral reforms of the scale proposed by ECI require legislative action by Parliament, creating an inherent conflict of interest that only judicial intervention or strong civil society pressure can overcome.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: Polity — elections, GS2: Governance — rule of law, GS4: Ethics — corruption in politics
  🌐 Contemporary: Supreme Court's 2024 electoral bond judgment and ONOE bill reflect ongoing electoral reform debates.
