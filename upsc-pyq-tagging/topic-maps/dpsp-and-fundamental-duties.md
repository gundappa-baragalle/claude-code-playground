**[Q4911]** [2025]
> Consider the following pairs :  Provision in the Constitution of India      Stated under I. Separation of Judiciary from the Executive in the public services of the State : The Directive Principles of the State Policy II. Valuing and preserving of the rich heritage of our composite culture : The Fundamental Duties III. Prohibition of employment of children below the age of 14 years in factories : The Fundamental Rights  How many of the above pairs are correctly matched?

     A) Only one
     B) Only two
  ✅ C) All the three
     D) None

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates often confuse which article contains child labour prohibition — it is under Fundamental Rights (Article 24), not DPSP. Separation of Judiciary (Article 50) under DPSP is the common confusion.
  💡 **Eliminate:** All three are correctly matched: separation of judiciary = DPSP (Article 50); rich heritage of composite culture = Fundamental Duties (Article 51A(f)); child labour prohibition = Fundamental Rights (Article 24). Answer: C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Article 24 = Fundamental Right prohibiting child labour in factories
     - Article 50 = DPSP — separation of judiciary from executive
     - Article 51A(f) = FD — preserve composite culture heritage

**[Q4199]** [2021]
> Under the Indian Constitution, concentration of wealth violates

     A) the Right to Equality
  ✅ B) the Directive Principles of State Policy
     C) the Right to Freedom
     D) the Concept of Welfare

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may choose 'Right to Equality' thinking wealth concentration creates unequal treatment. However, the Constitution's DPSP (Article 39) specifically directs the State to prevent concentration of wealth — making it the correct answer.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Article 39(b) and (c) of DPSP explicitly prohibit concentration of wealth and means of production to the common detriment — concentration of wealth violates DPSP, not Fundamental Rights (which are not directly about wealth distribution).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Article 39(c) DPSP: operation of economic system should not result in concentration of wealth to common detriment
     - DPSPs are non-justiciable but guide state policy on economic equality

**[Q3926]** [2020]
> In India, separation of judiciary from the executive is enjoined by

     A) the Preamble of the Constitution
  ✅ B) a Directive Principle of State Policy
     C) the Seventh Schedule
     D) the conventional practice

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may choose 'Seventh Schedule' (Union/State/Concurrent lists) or 'Preamble' (no legal mandate). The Preamble and conventional practice do not legally enjoin separation; only a specific DPSP does.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Article 50 (DPSP): State shall take steps to separate judiciary from executive in public services. This is a directive principle enjoining separation. Answer is B.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Article 50 DPSP: 'State shall take steps to separate judiciary from executive'
     - This led to separate judicial service in states distinct from executive services
     - DPSP, though non-justiciable, has been substantially implemented through Criminal Procedure Code and judicial reforms

**[Q3933]** [2020]
> Which part of the Constitution of India declares the ideal of Welfare State?

  ✅ A) Directive Principles of State Policy
     B) Fundamental Rights
     C) Preamble
     D) Seventh Schedule

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may choose Preamble as it mentions 'welfare' ideals, or Fundamental Rights as they guarantee positive entitlements. However, the Welfare State ideal in its operational sense is specifically embodied in DPSP.
  💡 **Eliminate:** DPSPs are guidelines to the State for governance — directing it toward social, economic, and political justice — this is the operational declaration of a Welfare State ideal. Answer is A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - DPSPs (Part IV) direct state toward equitable distribution, welfare provisions — Welfare State framework
     - Article 38: State to promote welfare of people; Article 39: policy principles for economic justice
     - Preamble mentions 'social justice' and 'welfare' in aspirational terms, but DPSPs operationalize them

**[Q3377]** [2017]
> With reference to the Constitution of India, the Directive Principles of State Policy constitute limitations upon  1. legislative function. 2. executive function.  Which of the above statements is/are correct?

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
     C) Both 1 and 2
  ✅ D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** DPSPs are non-justiciable guidelines — they do not create legal limitations on legislature or executive. Candidates might think DPSPs limit legislative power because Parliament should follow them.
  💡 **Eliminate:** DPSPs are not enforceable in courts; they are moral/political obligations but do not constitute legal limitations on either legislature or executive. Answer is D (neither).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - DPSPs are non-justiciable — cannot be enforced in court
     - They are directions to state, not legal constraints on legislation
     - Article 37: DPSPs are fundamental in governance but not enforceable

**[Q3396]** [2017]
> Which of the following statements is/are true of the Fundamental Duties of an Indian citizen?  1. A legislative process has been provided to enforce these duties. 2. They are correlative to legal duties.  Select the correct answer using the code given below :

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
     C) Both 1 and 2
  ✅ D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Many assume Fundamental Duties are legally enforceable like Fundamental Rights, but no specific legislative mechanism exists to enforce them; they are also moral duties, not correlative legal duties.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Both statements are false—no enforcement mechanism exists and duties are moral not legal correlatives—so 'Neither 1 nor 2' (D) is correct.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Fundamental Duties (Article 51A) have no enforcement mechanism in the Constitution
     - Fundamental Duties are moral obligations, not correlative to legal rights

### GS2

**[2015]** Q1. Discuss the possible factors that inhibit India from enacting for its citizens a uniform civil code as provided for in the Directive Principles of State Policy.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHY | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Uniform Civil Code, Secularism, Minority rights, Legal reform

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Article 44's vision of a Uniform Civil Code remains unimplemented after seven decades, reflecting deep tensions between legal uniformity, constitutional secularism, and pluralistic democratic politics.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Religious community resistance: minority communities fear UCC as majoritarian imposition threatening religious identity
      • Political sensitivity: electoral calculations around minority vote banks discourage legislative action
      • Constitutional tension: Article 25/26 (religious freedom) vs Article 44 (UCC as DPSP) — courts have held both valid
      • Definitional challenge: 'uniform' civil code — does it mean uniformity with Hindu law or a genuinely new code?
      • Goa model: Portuguese civil code applicable to all religions in Goa — but not without exemptions
      • SC observations: Shah Bano (1985), Sarla Mudgal (1995) — Supreme Court repeatedly nudged Parliament to enact UCC
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Enacting UCC requires political consensus, extensive consultation with all communities, and a genuinely new code that transcends existing personal laws rather than imposing one community's norms on others.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: Polity — DPSP and fundamental rights, GS1: Society — religion and communalism, GS2: Governance — law reform
  🌐 Contemporary: UCC's inclusion in BJP manifestos and Uttarakhand's 2024 UCC Act represent concrete contemporary developments.

### Common Trap Patterns on This Topic
- Option B (Union keeps all proceeds) reflects a common misunderstanding that the Centre is dominant in taxation; option D about surcharge sharing is partially correct but incomplete.
- Candidates may choose 'social democracy' or 'Gandhian democracy' as partial answers; DPSP's purpose is specifically to establish both social AND economic democracy.
- Article 48A (environment protection) and 43A (worker participation in management) are prominent DPSPs that candidates recall more easily, leading to confusion about which article deals with international peace.
- Article 39(d) does mention equal pay for equal work but as a DPSP (directive), not a fundamental right — Statement I says 'no provision' which is false. Statement II about backward classes is correct (Constitution uses the term but does not define it).
- Statement 1 (prohibition of traffic/forced labour) is a Fundamental Right (Article 23), NOT a DPSP — students confuse FRs with DPSPs. Only statement 2 (prohibition of intoxicating drinks) is a DPSP (Article 47).
