**[Q3810]** [2019]
> Which of the following statements is/are correct regarding the Maternity Benefit (Amendment) Act, 2017?  1. Pregnant women are entitled for three months pre-delivery and three months post-delivery paid leave. 2. Enterprises with crèches must allow the mother minimum six crèche visits daily. 3. Women with two children get reduced entitlements.  Select the correct answer using the code given below.

     A) 1 and 2 only
     B) 2 only
  ✅ C) 3 only
     D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 1 is wrong — the Act provides 26 weeks of paid maternity leave (not 3+3 months which is only 6 months, actually correct for 26 weeks ≈ 6.5 months). Statement 2 is wrong — mothers may visit crèches four times daily (not six). Statement 3 about reduced entitlements for women with two children is correct.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Maternity Benefit Amendment 2017: 26 weeks leave (not 3+3=6 months exactly); 4 crèche visits per day (not 6); women with 2+ children get 12 weeks — reduced entitlement (statement 3 correct). Answer is C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Maternity Benefit Amendment 2017: 26 weeks paid leave for first two children; 12 weeks for third child onwards
     - Crèche facility mandatory for establishments with 50+ employees; 4 visits per day (not 6)
     - Work from home option introduced for post-maternity period

**[2024]** Q8. Distinguish between gender equality, gender equity and women's empowerment. Why is it important to take gender concerns into account in programme design and implementation?
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: VS | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Gender equality, Women's empowerment, Programme design

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The concepts of gender equality, gender equity, and women's empowerment form a complementary triad — distinguishing between equal rights, fair treatment, and the transformation of power relations.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Gender equality: equal rights, responsibilities, and opportunities for all genders — formal legal equality
      • Gender equity: fairness accounting for historical discrimination — providing different support to achieve equality of outcomes
      • Women's empowerment: expansion of women's agency, capacity to make decisions, and control over resources
      • Programme design relevance: equal resources without accounting for women's double burden (work+care) won't achieve equity
      • MGNREGA example: equal wages, but gender-sensitive timing, childcare facilities needed for women's participation
      • SHG model: embodies empowerment — women gain economic independence and collective voice simultaneously
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Gender-sensitive programme design requires moving beyond formal equality to equity-based approaches that recognise and address the structural barriers women face, ultimately building their transformative agency.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Women & gender, GS2: Governance — social sector, GS3: Labour market
  🌐 Contemporary: India's Women's Reservation Act 2023 reflects equality; affirmative quotas within parties would reflect equity; building women leaders' capacity is empowerment.

**[2021]** Q10. Examine the role of 'Gig Economy' in the process of empowerment of women in India.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Gig economy, Women's empowerment, Labour market

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The gig economy presents a paradox for women's empowerment — offering unprecedented flexibility and income access while simultaneously perpetuating informality and excluding women from traditional labour protections.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Empowerment potential: women can work from home, manage caregiving alongside income generation
      • Urban women: Swiggy, Urban Company, tutoring platforms enabled supplementary income
      • Barrier reduction: gig platforms bypass traditional gatekeeping and employer discrimination
      • Limitations: women concentrated in lower-paid care, beauty, domestic gig segments vs men in delivery, tech
      • No social security: no maternity benefit, no pension, no ESIC — women disproportionately bear this cost
      • Digital literacy gap: rural and older women excluded from platform-based gig opportunities
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The gig economy can contribute to women's empowerment only if complemented by social security extension, digital skilling, and gender-sensitive platform design that address structural inequalities.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Women & gender, GS3: Labour market, GS2: Governance — social security
  🌐 Contemporary: Code on Social Security 2020's aggregator provisions for gig workers remain unimplemented, leaving women gig workers vulnerable.

**[2019]** Q9. "Empowering women is the key to control population growth." Discuss.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Women empowerment and population, Education-fertility nexus, Gender equality as development tool

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Women's empowerment through education, economic participation, and healthcare access is the most sustainable and rights-based approach to population stabilization.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Education raises the age of marriage and reduces fertility
      • Economic independence gives women agency over reproductive decisions
      • Access to contraception and maternal healthcare reduces unwanted pregnancies
      • Evidence from Kerala, Tamil Nadu — high female literacy correlates with low TFR
      • Contrast with forced sterilization — ethical and demographic failures
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Empowering women addresses the root cause of population growth while simultaneously achieving multiple development goals.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 — health and education schemes, GS1 — society and population
  🌐 Contemporary: India crossed 1.4 billion population; women empowerment remains central to population policy.

**[2025]** Q6. Women's social capital complements in advancing empowerment and gender equity. Explain.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Women's empowerment, Social capital, Gender equity

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Women's social capital — the networks of trust, reciprocity, and collective action embedded in communities — acts as a powerful complement to formal empowerment mechanisms in advancing gender equity.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Definition: women's social capital = participation in SHGs, mahila mandals, neighbourhood networks that build trust and shared norms
      • Complementing economic empowerment: SHGs channel microfinance, build credit history and entrepreneurship — Kudumbashree model in Kerala
      • Political empowerment: social networks help women contest panchayat elections, assert leadership despite social opposition
      • Advancing gender equity: community norms shift when women collectively challenge domestic violence, child marriage, and discriminatory practices
      • Education and health: peer networks promote girls' enrolment, vaccination, maternal health behaviours
      • Challenges: social capital can also reinforce caste/class hierarchies if not carefully designed
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Women's social capital bridges the gap between formal rights and lived realities — transforming policy promises into community-driven gender equity.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 Society, GS2 Governance, GS3 Economy
  🌐 Contemporary: National Rural Livelihoods Mission has mobilised 9 crore+ women into SHGs, demonstrating the scale of women's social capital.

**[2021]** Q17. "Though women in post-Independent India have excelled in various fields, the social attitude towards women and feminist movement has been patriarchal." Apart from women education and women empowerment schemes, what interventions can help change this milieu?
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Patriarchal attitudes, Women's political participation, Social change beyond schemes

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Despite significant achievements in women's education and political representation post-independence, deeply entrenched patriarchal social attitudes continue to constrain women's autonomy, mobility and decision-making power in India.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Legal framework: equal rights, anti-dowry, anti-domestic violence laws exist but implementation gaps
      • Education gains: girl enrollment parity in primary and secondary; but gender gap at higher education
      • Economic exclusion: low LFPR despite education — social norms restrict workforce participation
      • Interventions beyond schemes: legal reforms (Nirbhaya Act), media representation, engaging men and boys
      • Panchayati Raj: 33% reservation for women but 'sarpanch pati' syndrome undermines effective representation
      • Women's Reservation Act 2023: 33% seats in Parliament — transformative potential
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Transforming patriarchal attitudes requires not just schemes and legal reforms but sustained investment in gender-transformative education, media and community engagement that targets the social norms underpinning gender inequality.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Society — caste and gender intersection, Governance — women's welfare schemes, Polity — Women's Reservation Act
  🌐 Contemporary: Women's Reservation Act 2023 (Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam) and India's G20 WE-20 commitments are transformative recent developments.

**[2017]** Q15. Is the National Commission for Women able to strategize and tackle the problems that women face at both public and private spheres? Give reasons in support of your answer.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: NCW effectiveness, Women's empowerment institutions, Public vs private sphere

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The National Commission for Women, despite its statutory mandate, operates with significant limitations in tackling both public sphere discrimination and private sphere violence against women.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Mandate: review constitutional/legal safeguards, recommend remedies, take up cases — but no enforcement powers
      • Public sphere successes: policy advocacy on women's reservation, workplace harassment norms, POCSO awareness
      • Private sphere limitations: domestic violence, marital rape, child marriage — NCW can only refer to police/courts; no direct intervention
      • Institutional weaknesses: inadequate funding, political appointments, absence of regional presence, no suo motu criminal powers
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Strengthening NCW requires enforcement powers, financial autonomy, geographical reach, and independence from political appointments — to match its constitutional promise with institutional capacity.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 Governance — constitutional bodies, GS1 Society — women, GS2 Polity — statutory bodies
  🌐 Contemporary: NCW's role in responding to violence against women remains contested — Manipur violence (2023) and R.G. Kar incident (2024) tested its effectiveness.

### GS3

**[2023]** Q12. Distinguish between 'care economy' and 'monetized economy'. How can care economy be brought into monetized economy through women empowerment?
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Care economy, Women empowerment, Economic inclusion

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The care economy — encompassing unpaid domestic labor, childcare, and eldercare — represents a vast, invisible economic contribution overwhelmingly borne by women, whose integration into the monetized economy is central to genuine gender equality.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Care economy defined: unpaid activities including cooking, childcare, nursing sick, eldercare — economically productive but excluded from GDP; ILO estimates it at 20–39% of global GDP
      • Monetized economy: formal market-based activities that generate measurable income and are included in GDP
      • Distinction: care economy is non-market, socially necessary; monetized economy is market-mediated, GDP-counted
      • How to bring care economy into monetized economy through women empowerment: (i) valuation — time-use surveys, satellite accounts; (ii) redistribution — men sharing care work, creche facilities; (iii) formalization — domestic workers' rights, social security; (iv) women's economic participation — SHGs, skilling, digital literacy; (v) policy: maternity benefits, crèche subsidies, equal pay enforcement
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Integrating the care economy into the monetized framework through women's empowerment is not just a gender justice imperative — it is essential for accurate economic measurement and sustainable, inclusive growth.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Women issues, GS3: Economic growth and development, GS2: Social welfare schemes
  🌐 Contemporary: India's declining female labor force participation rate and the push for women-led development under G20 make this highly relevant.

**[2016]** Q2. Women empowerment in India needs gender budgeting. What are the requirements and status of gender budgeting in the Indian context?
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Gender budgeting, Women empowerment, Fiscal tools for gender equality

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Gender budgeting is a powerful fiscal instrument that makes governments accountable to women's needs by embedding gender analysis into the budgetary process.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Concept: assessing budget allocations for their differential impact on women and men; not a separate budget
      • India's approach: Union Budget Gender Budget Statement since 2005–06 (Part A: 100% women-specific schemes; Part B: ≥30% for women)
      • Requirements: gender-disaggregated data, institutional capacity, political will, women's participation in planning
      • Status: India ranks 127/146 in Gender Gap Index 2023 — indicates significant implementation gaps despite policy framework
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Gender budgeting must evolve from a reporting exercise to a genuine allocative reform tool, backed by robust data and accountability mechanisms.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 Economy — public finance, GS1 Society — women, GS2 Governance
  🌐 Contemporary: India's Gender Budget allocation (~5–6% of total budget) remains far below the recommended 30% for transformative impact.
