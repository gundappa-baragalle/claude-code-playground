**[Q4710]** [2024]
> The total fertility rate in an economy is defined as:

     A) the number of children born per 1000 people in the population in a year.
     B) the number of children born to a couple in their lifetime in a given population.
     C) the birth rate minus death rate.
  ✅ D) the average number of live births a woman would have by the end of her child-bearing age.

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may confuse TFR with Crude Birth Rate (births per 1000 population) or Growth Rate; TFR is specifically about a woman's average lifetime births.
  💡 **Eliminate:** TFR = average number of live births a woman would have during her reproductive period (ages 15-49) assuming current age-specific fertility rates. Answer: D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Total Fertility Rate (TFR): average live births per woman over her reproductive lifetime at current fertility rates
     - India's TFR: 2.0 (NFHS-5, 2019-21) — below replacement level of 2.1 for first time

     B) 2, 1, 3
  ✅ C) 2, 3, 1

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students sometimes confuse the order of stages 1 and 2, or insert stage 3 incorrectly. The key is knowing the classical demographic transition model sequence.

     - IMR = number of deaths of children under 1 year per 1000 live births in a given year

**[2024]** Q7. What is the concept of a 'demographic winter'? Is the world moving towards such a situation? Elaborate.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Demographic winter, Population decline, Global population trends

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** A 'demographic winter' describes a condition where sub-replacement fertility combines with population aging to create a society with shrinking working-age populations, fiscal stress, and potential civilisational decline.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Concept: coined by demographers — TFR persistently below 2.1; fewer births than deaths
      • Global situation: Europe, Japan, South Korea, China now facing demographic winter conditions
      • Causes: urbanisation, women's education and career, high child-rearing costs, delayed marriage
      • Consequences: labour shortages, pension system strain, deflation, reduced innovation
      • World trend: world population peak projected around 2080-2100 — some regions already declining
      • India: TFR at replacement level — still some demographic dividend remaining but declining
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While demographic winter is not imminent globally, its early manifestation in developed countries offers important lessons for India as its fertility declines — proactive policy for elder care, immigration, and productivity enhancement is essential.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Society — population, GS3: Economy — labour, International: demographic dividend
  🌐 Contemporary: South Korea's population crisis and Japan's ageing economy offer cautionary lessons India must heed as TFR declines.
