**[Q4525]** [2023]
> With reference to ancient South India, Korkai, Poompuhar and Muchiri were well known as

     A) capital cities
  ✅ B) ports
     C) centres of iron-and-steel making
     D) shrines of Jain Tirthankaras

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may guess capital cities since these are prominent ancient towns; Korkai, Poompuhar (Kaveripattinam) and Muchiri (Muziri) were actually famous trading ports.
  💡 **Eliminate:** All three — Korkai (Pandya), Poompuhar/Kaveripattinam (Chola), and Muchiri/Muziri (Chera) — were important seaports of Sangam Age South India engaged in Roman and Southeast Asian trade.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Poompuhar (Kaveripattinam) was the Chola port; Muchiri (Muziri) was the Chera port — both had Roman trade links
     - Korkai was the Pandya port city, known for pearls; later Sangam ports declined

**[Q4526]** [2023]
> Which one of the following explains the practice of Vattakirutal' as mentioned in Sangam poems?

     A) Kings employing women bodyguards
     B) Learned persons assembling in royal courts to discuss religious and philosophical matters
     C) Young girls keeping watch over agricultural fields and driving away birds and animals
  ✅ D) A king defeated in a battle committing ritual suicide by starving himself to death

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Vattakirutal sounds like a ceremonial practice; students unfamiliar with Sangam literature may guess options A or B which sound more ceremonial.
  💡 **Eliminate:** 'Vattakirutal' (literally 'sitting in a circle') refers to a defeated king fasting unto death (ritual suicide) — a concept mentioned in Sangam Tamil literature as an honorable end for vanquished rulers.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Vattakirutal: practice of a defeated/dishonored king committing ritual suicide by starvation, mentioned in Sangam poems
     - The practice reflects the Sangam-era warrior ethos where defeat in battle was considered deeply shameful

**[Q4388]** [2022]
> Which one of the following statements about Sangam literature in ancient South India is correct?

     A) Sangam poems are devoid of any reference to material culture.
  ✅ B) The social classification of Varna was known to Sangam poets.
     C) Sangam poems have no reference to warrior ethic.
     D) Sangam literature refers to magical forces as irrational.

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Options A and C are easily eliminated (Sangam literature is rich in material culture and warrior ethic). Option D about 'magical forces as irrational' is wrong — Sangam literature accepted magical/supernatural forces.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Sangam literature does refer to Varna — the concept of social hierarchy was known though not as rigidly applied in the South as in North India. Option B is the correct, specific factual claim.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Sangam poetry (Purananuru, Akananuru) richly describes warrior culture (puram poems) and material life (trade, towns)
     - Varna classification was known to Sangam poets though the southern social system had its own features

**[Q3569]** [2018]

     A) 1 only
  ✅ B) 1 and 2
     C) 3 only
     D) 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Thang-Ta dance is from Manipur, not Sikkim — statement 3 is wrong. Chapchar Kut (Mizoram) and Khongjom Parba (Manipur) are correctly matched.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Chapchar Kut: Mizoram spring festival (correct). Khongjom Parba: Manipuri ballad about 1891 Anglo-Manipur War (correct). Thang-Ta: Manipuri martial art/dance, NOT Sikkim (statement 3 wrong). Answer: 1 and 2.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Chapchar Kut: spring festival of Mizo people, Mizoram — celebrates jhum cultivation completion
     - Khongjom Parba: ballad tradition of Manipur commemorating Battle of Khongjom (1891)
     - Thang-Ta: Manipuri martial art dance — NOT from Sikkim

**[Q3254]** [2016]
> In the context of the history of India, consider the following pairs: Term - Description  1. Eripatti - Land, revenue from which was set apart for the main- tenance of the village tank 2. Taniyurs - Villages donated to a single Brahmin or a group of Brahmins 3. Ghatikas - Colleges generally attached to the temples  Which of the pairs given above is/are correctly matched?

     A) 1 and 2
     B) 3 only
     C) 2 and 3
  ✅ D) 1 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 (Taniyurs = Brahmin donation villages) is the trap — these were called Brahmadeya or Agrahara, not Taniyurs. Taniyurs were actually autonomous self-governing villages.

  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Eripatti: revenue reserved for village tank maintenance in Chola records
     - Ghatikas were Sanskrit colleges attached to temples in South India

**[Q11]** [1995]
> The term Yavanapriya, mentioned in ancient Sanskrit texts, denoted

     A) a fine variety of Indian muslin
     B) ivory
     C) damsels sent to the Greek court for dance performance
  ✅ D) pepper

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Yavanapriya literally means 'beloved of Yavanas (Greeks)' suggesting something luxurious; students might guess muslin or ivory, missing that pepper was the main trade commodity.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Yavanapriya = pepper, the most prized Indian spice in Roman-Greek trade; muslin was known by other terms; select D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Yavanapriya ('beloved of Greeks') = black pepper in ancient Sanskrit texts
     - Pepper was India's most exported commodity to Rome and Greece
     - Sangam texts and Sanskrit sources reference Indian Ocean spice trade

**[Q827]** [2000]
> Assertion (A) : The Aham and Puram poems of the Padinen Kilukanakku group formed a continuation of the Sangam composition. Reason (R) : They were included under the Post-Sangam works as against the Sangam works proper.

     A) Both A and R are true, and R is the correct explanation of A
  ✅ B) Both A and R are true, but R is not a correct explanation of A
     C) A is true, but R is false
     D) A is false, but R is true

  ⚠️ **Trap:** The Assertion states Padinen Kilukanakku group 'continued' Sangam composition, and the Reason says they are 'Post-Sangam' — both can be true simultaneously if the continuation is stylistic but classification is temporal.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Padinen Kilukanakku (18 minor works) were composed after Sangam period proper — A is true (they continued the composition tradition), R is true (they are classified as Post-Sangam), but R doesn't fully explain A — select B.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Padinen Kilukanakku: 18 minor works of post-Sangam period, didactic in nature
     - Sangam literature divided into: Ettuthokai (8 anthologies), Pattuppattu (10 idylls), Padinen Kilukanakku (18 minor works)

**[Q640]** [1999]
> One consistent feature found in the history of southern India was the growth of small regional kingdoms rather than large empires because of

     A) the absence of minerals like iron
     B) too many divisions in the social structure
  ✅ C) the absence of vast areas of fertile land
     D) the scarcity of manpower

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Option B (social divisions) seems plausible as caste divisions historically hampered large-scale mobilization; Option A (no iron) is easily eliminated as South India did have iron.
  💡 **Eliminate:** The absence of vast fertile plains (unlike Gangetic plain) meant southern kingdoms lacked the agricultural surplus to sustain large imperial armies and administration → Option C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - South India's geography: Deccan plateau, rocky terrain, no extensive fertile plains like North India's Doab region
     - Absence of large river-plain systems limited agricultural surplus needed for empire-building

**[Q650]** [1999]
> From the third century AD when the Hun invasion ended the Roman Empire, the Indian merchants relied more and more on the

     A) African trade
     B) West-European trade
  ✅ C) South-East Asian trade
     D) Middle-Eastern trade

  ⚠️ **Trap:** African trade seems plausible as India had long-standing African connections; Middle-Eastern trade is also historically important for Indian merchants.
  💡 **Eliminate:** After Rome's decline, Indian merchants shifted to South-East Asian trade as alternative markets developed in Indochina, Java, Sumatra — this is well-documented in trade history.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Indian Ocean trade shifted from Rome to Southeast Asia from 3rd-4th century CE
     - Indian merchants established trading colonies in Southeast Asia (Indianization of SE Asia)

  ✅ B) I-D, II-A, III-B, IV-C

     D) I-A, II-D, III-B, IV-C

**[Q299]** [1997]
> Which one of the following dances involves solo performance ?

  ✅ A) Bharatanatyam
     B) Kuchipudi
     C) Katahak
     D) Odissi

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Kathak and Odissi are also solo forms, so students may second-guess Bharatanatyam. Key: Kuchipudi ORIGINALLY was a GROUP performance by male Brahmin community — it was NOT historically solo unlike the others.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Kuchipudi = originated as group dramatic performance by male Brahmin community of Kuchipudi village (AP), NOT traditionally solo. Bharatanatyam = Devadasi solo tradition (Tamil Nadu). Answer: A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Bharatanatyam = Tamil Nadu, Devadasi tradition, always solo female
     - Kuchipudi = Andhra Pradesh, originally group male Brahmin performance
     - Kathak = North Indian courts, solo
     - Odissi = Odisha temple tradition, solo

### GS1

**[2013]** Q1. Though not very useful from the point of view of a connected political history of South India, the Sangam literature portrays the social and economic conditions of its time with remarkable vividness. Comment.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Sangam literature's limitations for political history, Vivid social and economic portrayal in Sangam poetry, Cultural richness of ancient Tamil society

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Sangam literature, while fragmentary and non-chronological as a source of political history, offers an unparalleled window into the social fabric, economic life and cultural values of ancient South India.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Political history limitations: Poems are panegyrics, occasional and context-less; no sequential narrative; rulers mentioned episodically without administrative detail
      • Social richness: Caste/class references (Vellala, Paraiya); warrior culture (puram poems); gender roles; sati (early references)
      • Economic life: Trade ports (Puhar/Kaveripattinam), merchant guilds, Roman trade references (amphorae), coins; spices and cotton exports
      • Cultural richness: Five tinai landscapes correlate to emotions; nature-human relationships; religious syncretism (Murugan, Shiva, Vishnu)
      • Archaeological corroboration: Arikamedu findings confirm Sangam references to Indo-Roman trade
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Sangam literature's true historical value lies not in political chronology but as a literary-sociological document — one of humanity's finest records of an ancient civilization's inner life.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 History, GS1 Art & Culture, World History
  🌐 Contemporary: Sangam literature is foundational to Tamil cultural identity and classical language status debates.
