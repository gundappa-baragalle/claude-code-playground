**[Q369]** [1997]
> Which one of the following was used as a chemical weapon in the First World War?

     A) Carbon monoxide
     B) Hydrogen cyanide
  ✅ C) Mustard gas
     D) Water gas

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Hydrogen cyanide was used but less famously; carbon monoxide is a poison but not a battlefield chemical weapon; candidates may confuse various toxic gases.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Mustard gas (dichlorodiethyl sulphide) was the most notorious chemical weapon of WWI, first used by Germany in 1917 at Ypres.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Mustard gas first used by Germany in 1917 at Ypres (WWI)
     - Mustard gas causes blistering — a vesicant/blister agent

### GS1

**[2024]** Q12. How far is it correct to say that the First World War was fought essentially for the preservation of balance of power?
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Causes of WWI, Balance of power in international relations, Role of alliances and militarism

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** While balance of power considerations were central to pre-WWI European diplomacy, characterizing WWI as fought 'essentially' for this purpose oversimplifies a conflict driven by multiple intersecting forces.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Balance of power rationale: Alliance systems (Triple Alliance vs Triple Entente) were designed to maintain equilibrium; Germany's rise threatened British-led balance
      • Britain entered war partly to prevent German hegemony over Europe — a classic balance of power motive
      • However, nationalism (Serbian, Pan-Slavism), imperial rivalries, arms race, and the assassination of Franz Ferdinand were equally critical triggers
      • Economic competition and colonial ambitions in Africa and Asia went beyond balance of power concerns
      • Fischer Controversy: German war aims were expansionist (September Programme), not merely balance-preserving
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** WWI was a product of multiple overlapping crises — balance of power was one crucial lens but insufficient to explain the war's total character and tragic scale.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 World History, IR — multilateralism and alliances
  🌐 Contemporary: NATO expansion debates echo WWI-era balance of power anxieties in modern geopolitics.

**[2021]** Q13. "There arose a serious challenge to the Democratic State System between the two World Wars." Evaluate the statement.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Challenge to democracy, Interwar ideological conflict, Rise of fascism and communism

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The interwar period (1919–1939) witnessed unprecedented challenges to democratic governance as fascism, Nazism, and Stalinist communism offered authoritarian alternatives amid economic collapse and social disillusionment.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Rise of Fascism: Mussolini in Italy (1922), Hitler in Germany (1933) exploited post-WWI humiliation, economic distress
      • Economic context: Great Depression (1929) discredited liberal democracies, creating fertile ground for authoritarian populism
      • Ideological challenge: Communism (Soviet model) and Fascism both rejected liberal democratic values of individual rights and parliamentary governance
      • Weakness of democracies: Appeasement policy, League of Nations failure, internal divisions weakened democratic response
      • Spain: Spanish Civil War (1936–39) symbolized the ideological battle between democracy/communism and fascism
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The interwar period demonstrated how economic crisis and political disillusionment can hollow out democratic institutions if not protected by strong civil society and international cooperation.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: World History, GS2: IR and political systems
  🌐 Contemporary: Rise of right-wing populism globally echoes interwar democratic challenges.

**[2015]** Q7. To what extent can Germany be held responsible for causing the two World Wars? Discuss critically.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: World War causation, German responsibility, International relations

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Germany's culpability for both World Wars, while substantial, must be assessed against the broader systemic failures of European diplomacy and the unintended consequences of post-WWI punitive settlements.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • WWI: Germany's blank cheque to Austria-Hungary, Schlieffen Plan, naval rivalry with Britain — significant but not sole responsibility; balance of power system equally culpable
      • Fischer Controversy: German elites deliberately sought European war for hegemony
      • Treaty of Versailles: humiliating terms created fertile ground for Nazi rise — making Allied powers co-responsible for WWII
      • WWII: Hitler's ideology, expansionism, and aggressive diplomacy — Germany's primary responsibility is clearer
      • Appeasement by Britain and France enabled German aggression
      • Soviet-German pact (Molotov-Ribbentrop) also facilitated WWII onset
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Germany bears significant but not exclusive responsibility — WWI emerged from systemic European failures while WWII was more directly attributable to Nazi Germany's deliberate aggression.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: World history, International relations, Decolonization aftermath
  🌐 Contemporary: The debate resonates in modern international relations regarding accountability for wars and the dangers of punitive peace settlements.
