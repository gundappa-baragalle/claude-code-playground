**[Q4723]** [2024]
> With reference to ancient India, Gautama Buddha was generally known by which of the following epithets?  1. Nayaputta 2. Shakyamuni 3. Tathagata  Select the correct answer using the code given below:

     A) 1 only
  ✅ B) 2 and 3 only
     C) 1, 2 and 3
     D) None of the above are epithets of Gautama Buddha

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Nayaputta (Jnatriputra) is an epithet of Mahavira (the Jain Tirthankara), NOT Gautama Buddha. Students who don't know this specific fact may include it.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Nayaputta = epithet of Mahavira (meaning 'son of the Naya/Jnatri clan'); Shakyamuni (sage of the Shakya clan) and Tathagata (the Thus-Gone One) are both epithets of Gautama Buddha. Answer: B.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Nayaputta/Nataputta: epithet of Vardhamana Mahavira (founder of Jainism)
     - Shakyamuni: 'sage of the Shakyas' — refers to Buddha's clan origin from Shakya republic
     - Tathagata: 'one who has thus gone/come' — key epithet of Buddha in Pali texts

     - Upasaka = lay follower of Buddhism (as opposed to monk/bhikkhu)
     - Parivrajaka = wandering renunciant in ancient India

  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*

  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The Pala period (8th–12th century CE) represents the final flourishing of Buddhism in its Indian homeland, distinguished by royal patronage, institutional learning, and the spread of Vajrayana traditions across Asia.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Royal patronage: Pala kings Dharmapala and Devapala built Vikramashila, Odantapuri monasteries and expanded Nalanda
      • Nalanda under Palas: international university attracting students from Tibet, China, Southeast Asia
      • Vajrayana Buddhism: Pala period saw emergence and codification of Tantric Buddhist practices
      • International diffusion: Pala monks traveled to Tibet, Java, Sumatra — spreading Buddhist art and philosophy
      • Decline: Bakhtiyar Khilji's destruction of Nalanda (1193 CE) ended Pala Buddhist tradition in India
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The Pala period was Buddhism's last great institutional phase in India — its legacy endured through the transmission of Nalanda's scholarship to Tibet, Southeast Asia, and East Asia.

**[2016]** Q1. Early Buddhist Stupa-art, while depicting folk motifs and narratives successfully expounds Buddhist ideals. Elucidate.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: HOW | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Early Buddhist art as medium of religious expression, Synthesis of folk and religious motifs, Stupa as symbol

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Early Buddhist stupa-art represents a remarkable synthesis where indigenous folk traditions became the medium for communicating profound Buddhist ideals to the common people.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Folk motifs: yakshas, yakshinis, trees, animals from pre-Buddhist traditions incorporated at Bharhut and Sanchi
      • Narrative panels: Jataka stories depicted in sequential panels educating illiterate masses
      • Aniconic tradition: Buddha represented through symbols (footprint, umbrella, wheel) conveying nirvana concept
      • Architectural symbolism: anda represents the cosmos, harmika the sacred zone, chatravali the three jewels
      • Torana gateways at Sanchi display elaborate carvings blending folk and Buddhist narratives
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** By weaving folk idioms into the stupa's sacred vocabulary, early Buddhist artists created a uniquely accessible and enduring visual theology.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 – Art and culture, Indian heritage
  🌐 Contemporary: UNESCO World Heritage status of Sanchi reflects continued relevance of this art tradition.
