**[Q4737]** [2024]
> Which of the following statements are correct in respect of a Money Bill in the Parliament ?  1. Article 109 mentions special procedure in respect of Money Bills. 2. A Money Bill shall not be introduced in the Council of States. 3. The Rajya Sabha can either approve the Bill or suggest changes but cannot reject it. 4. Amendments to a Money Bill suggested by the Rajya Sabha have to be accepted by the Lok Sabha.  Select the answer using the code given below:

     A) 1 and 2 only
     B) 2 and 3 only
  ✅ C) 1, 2 and 3
     D) 1, 3 and 4

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 4 is the trap: Rajya Sabha can only suggest amendments to Money Bills; Lok Sabha may or may not accept them — Lok Sabha is NOT bound to accept RS suggestions.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Statements 1, 2, 3 are correct. Statement 4 is wrong — Lok Sabha need not accept RS suggestions on Money Bills. Answer: C (1, 2 and 3).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Article 109: Money Bill introduced only in Lok Sabha; RS can suggest but not reject or amend
     - Lok Sabha has absolute authority on Money Bills — RS suggestions are non-binding

**[Q4754]** [2024]
> With reference to Union Budget, consider the following statements:  1. The Union Finance Minister on behalf of the Prime Minister lays the Annual Financial Statement before both the Houses of Parliament. 2. At the Union level, no demand for a grant can be made except on the recommendation of the President of India.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
  ✅ C) Both 1 and 2
     D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 1 says Finance Minister lays Annual Financial Statement 'on behalf of the Prime Minister' — the AFS is laid by Finance Minister on behalf of President (not PM) per constitutional convention.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Both statements, read carefully, are correct: Statement 1 — Finance Minister lays AFS (convention is on behalf of President via PM's cabinet); Statement 2 — demands for grants require President's recommendation (Article 113). Answer: C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Annual Financial Statement (Union Budget) is laid before Parliament under Article 112 by Finance Minister
     - Article 113: No demand for grant can be made except on recommendation of President — money Bill requirement

**[Q4763]** [2024]
> With reference to the Indian Parliament, consider the following statements:  1. A bill pending in the Lok Sabha lapses on its dissolution. 2. A bill passed by the Lok Sabha and pending in the Rajya Sabha lapses on the dissolution of the Lok Sabha. 3. A bill in regard to which the President of India notified his/her intention to summon the Houses to a joint sitting lapses on the dissolution of the Lok Sabha.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 only
  ✅ B) 1 and 2
     C) 2 and 3
     D) 3 only

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 3 (bill with joint sitting notification lapses) is the key trap — such a bill does NOT lapse on Lok Sabha dissolution; it is saved specifically to enable the joint sitting.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Statement 1 (pending Lok Sabha bill lapses on dissolution) = correct. Statement 2 (passed by LS, pending in RS lapses) = correct. Statement 3 (joint sitting notification bill lapses) = WRONG — it is saved. Answer: B (1 and 2).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Bills pending in Lok Sabha at dissolution lapse; bills in Rajya Sabha (not passed by LS) don't lapse
     - A bill notified for joint sitting is specially saved from lapsing — Art 108 protects it across dissolution

**[Q4519]** [2023]
> With reference to Finance Bill and Money Bill in the Indian Parliament, consider the following statements:  1. When the Lok Sabha transmits Finance Bill to the Rajya Sabha, it can amend or reject the Bill. 2. When the Lok Sabha transmits Money Bill to the Rajya Sabha, it cannot amend or reject the Bill, it can only make recommendations. 3. In the case of disagreement between the Lok Sabha and the Rajya Sabha, there is no joint sitting for Money Bill, but a joint sitting becomes necessary for Finance Bill.  How many of the above statements are correct?

     A) Only one
  ✅ B) Only two
     C) All three
     D) None

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 1 (Rajya Sabha can amend/reject Finance Bill) is correct but statement 3 (joint sitting for Finance Bill) is incorrect — Finance Bills (non-Money Bills) go to joint sitting but some Finance Bills certified as Money Bills do not.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Statement 1 (RS can amend/reject Finance Bill — correct) and statement 2 (RS only recommends on Money Bill — correct) are both right; Statement 3 is wrong — Finance Bill disagreements go to joint sitting but Money Bill has no joint sitting — only two are correct.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Finance Bill (not certified as Money Bill): RS can amend or reject; deadlock resolved by joint sitting
     - Money Bill: RS can only recommend; Lok Sabha may accept or reject recommendations; no joint sitting

The Constitution of India provides for three sessions of the Parliament in a year, but it is not mandatory to conduct all three sessions. 3. There is no minimum number of days that the Parliament is required to meet in a year.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
  ✅ C) 1 and 3 only
     D) 2 and 3 only

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 is wrong — the Constitution does not specify three sessions; there is no constitutional mandate for any fixed number of sessions. Statement 3 (no minimum days) is correct.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Article 85 empowers President to summon Parliament at any place (1 is correct). There is no constitutional provision mandating three sessions or any minimum sitting days (3 is correct). Statement 2 is wrong.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Article 85: President summons, prorogues Parliament; can summon at any place
     - Constitution does not fix number of sessions or minimum sitting days — only gap between sessions (6 months max)

      • Parliament-court conflict: privilege claimed vs fundamental rights of citizens
      • Reform models: New Zealand, Australia codified privileges with clarity on scope
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Codifying parliamentary privileges through legislation would bring clarity, proportionality and constitutional accountability to Parliament's internal functioning while preserving the legitimate protection of independent legislative activity.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Polity — separation of powers and judicial review, Governance — parliamentary accountability, Polity — fundamental rights and privilege conflict
  🌐 Contemporary: Ongoing debates on media freedom vs parliamentary privilege and MP expulsion controversies keep this issue alive.

**[2013]** Q1. The role of individual MPs (Members of Parliament) has diminished over the years and as a result healthy constructive debates on policy issues are not usually witnessed. How far can this be attributed to the anti-defection law which was legislated but with a different intention?
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Anti-defection law and parliamentary democracy, Role of MPs in legislation, Party discipline vs individual conscience

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The Tenth Schedule, designed to prevent political defections, has inadvertently converted Parliament into a forum of party mandates rather than individual deliberation.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Anti-defection law background: 52nd Amendment 1985 to address political instability
      • Unintended consequence: MPs compelled to vote per party whip — dissent equated with defection risk
      • Decline of private member bills, adjournment motions, and substantive policy debates
      • Reform suggestions: restrict whip to confidence motions and money bills only; free votes on other issues
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Revisiting the scope of the anti-defection law to allow MPs greater legislative independence on non-confidence matters would revitalise parliamentary debate.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 Polity — Parliamentary functioning, GS4 Ethics — individual conscience vs party loyalty
  🌐 Contemporary: Anti-defection law reform is debated by Law Commission and parliamentary reform advocates.
