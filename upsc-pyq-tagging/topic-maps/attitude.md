**[2016]** Q6. Our attitudes towards life, work, other people and society are generally shaped unconsciously by the family and the social surroundings in which we grow up. Some of these unconsciously acquired attitudes and values are often undesirable in the citizens of a modern democratic and egalitarian society.  (a) Discuss such undesirable values prevalent in today's educated Indians.  (b) How can such undesirable attitudes be changed and socio-ethical values considered necessary in public services be cultivated in the aspiring and serving civil servants ?
  *Paper: GS4 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Undesirable attitudes in educated Indians, Attitudinal change for civil servants, Democratic socialization

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The transformation of unconsciously acquired undesirable attitudes is a prerequisite for building a civil service that truly embodies constitutional values of equality and justice.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Undesirable values: casteism, gender bias, nepotism, regionalism, religious prejudice, elitism
      • Persistence despite education: structural reinforcement through family and community
      • Changing attitudes: ethics training in civil service academies, sensitization workshops, diverse team experiences, exemplary leadership
      • Cultivating desirable values: equity, empathy, constitutional morality, inclusiveness
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Attitudinal transformation requires deliberate institutional intervention alongside personal reflection; civil service training must prioritize values alongside competencies.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Society: Indian Society, Governance: Civil Services
  🌐 Contemporary: Mission Karmayogi's competency framework includes behavioral competencies directly targeting attitudinal change in civil servants.

**[2014]** Q6. What factors affect the formation of a person's attitude towards social problems? In our society, contrasting attitudes are prevalent about many social problems. What contrasting attitudes do you notice about the caste system in our society? How do you explain the existence of these contrasting attitudes?
  *Paper: GS4 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Attitude formation, Contrasting attitudes toward caste

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Attitudes toward social problems like the caste system are shaped by a complex interplay of socialization, education, and experience, resulting in starkly contrasting perspectives within Indian society.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Factors: family upbringing (primary socialization), peer groups, education, religion, media, personal experience with discrimination
      • Contrasting caste attitudes: traditional view — caste as divinely ordained social order providing stability; reformist/constitutional view — caste as discriminatory, unconstitutional oppression
      • Why contrasting attitudes persist: geographic isolation reinforces tradition; urban education promotes liberal views; economic interests of dominant castes; political mobilization
      • Cognitive dissonance: educated individuals simultaneously accepting caste in marriage while rejecting it in principle
      • Constitutional framework vs lived reality: Fundamental Rights prohibit caste discrimination but social practice continues
      • Ambedkar's solution: education + inter-caste marriage + economic equality to change caste attitudes
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Transforming attitudes toward the caste system requires sustained educational interventions, constitutional reinforcement, and socioeconomic policies that make caste-based discrimination both morally unacceptable and economically redundant.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS4 Ethics — Attitude, GS1 Society — Caste System, Polity — Fundamental Rights
  🌐 Contemporary: Continued caste discrimination, inter-caste violence, and debates over reservation policies keep caste attitudes central to India's social and political discourse.
