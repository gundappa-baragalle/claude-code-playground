**[Q3559]** [2018]
> Consider the following statements:  1. Aadhaar card can be used as a proof of citizenship or domicile. 2. Once issued, Aadhaar number cannot be deactivated or omitted by the Issuing Authority.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
     C) Both 1 and 2
  ✅ D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 1 (Aadhaar as citizenship proof) is a major trap — Aadhaar proves identity and residence, NOT citizenship or domicile. Statement 2 about permanence is also wrong — UIDAI can deactivate numbers.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Aadhaar is proof of identity and address, NOT citizenship (statement 1 wrong). UIDAI can deactivate Aadhaar under certain conditions (statement 2 wrong). Answer: Neither.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Aadhaar proves identity and address, NOT citizenship or domicile — NRIs and foreigners can have Aadhaar
     - UIDAI can deactivate/cancel Aadhaar for reasons like duplicate entries or court orders
     - Supreme Court 2018 (Puttaswamy) restricted Aadhaar use but upheld its validity for government benefits

**[2025]** Q7. e-governance projects have a built-in bias towards technology and back-end integration than user-centric designs. Examine.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: E-governance, Citizen-centric design, Digital inclusion

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's e-governance architecture has prioritized backend technological integration at the expense of user-centric design, limiting its accessibility and effectiveness for ordinary citizens.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Technology bias: GSTN, PFMS, GeM are powerful backend systems but complex interfaces
      • User exclusion: portals require digital literacy that many citizens lack
      • Language barrier: most e-gov portals English-dominant despite multilingual India
      • Accessibility gap: portals not designed for persons with disabilities or elderly
      • Way forward: human-centered design, multilingual interfaces, simplified UX, offline-online hybrid models
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Effective e-governance must shift from technology showcase to genuine service enablement by placing citizen experience at the center of design and deployment.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: Governance, GS3: IT, Society: Digital divide
  🌐 Contemporary: Jeevan Pramaan digital life certificate rollout challenges and DigiLocker accessibility debates exemplify this tension.

**[2022]** Q8. Reforming the government delivery system through the Direct Benefit Transfer Scheme is a progressive step, but it has its limitations too. Comment.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: DBT as welfare delivery reform, Inclusion and exclusion errors in DBT, Technology-enabled governance and its limits

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The Direct Benefit Transfer scheme represents a paradigm shift in government benefit delivery — leveraging the JAM trinity to reduce leakage, yet its progressive character must be assessed alongside its documented limitations.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Progressive aspects: Eliminated ghost beneficiaries (savings of ₹2.7 lakh crore claimed by govt); reduced middlemen; faster delivery of LPG subsidies, scholarship, MGNREGS wages
      • Limitations — exclusion errors: lack of bank accounts/Aadhaar among marginalised communities; elderly and differently-abled struggle with biometric authentication
      • Last-mile connectivity: Banking Correspondent network weak in remote areas; cash-out failures in tribal districts
      • Conditionality issues: DBT conditionality (e.g., school attendance, immunization) can exclude vulnerable beneficiaries during emergencies
      • Recommendations: Grievance redressal strengthening, expanding BC network, alternative authentication methods for special categories
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** DBT is a progressive step in accountable welfare delivery, but its equity promise will remain unfulfilled without addressing exclusion errors and last-mile infrastructure gaps.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 Governance, GS3 Economy, GS1 Society
  🌐 Contemporary: India's DBT program has become a model referenced globally; its COVID-19 relief transfer was a major test of its efficacy.
