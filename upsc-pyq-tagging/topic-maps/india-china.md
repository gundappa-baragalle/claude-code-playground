### GS2

**[2024]** Q9. 'The West is fostering India as an alternative to reduce dependence on China's supply chain and as a strategic ally to counter China's political and economic dominance.' Explain this statement with examples.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: India as alternative to China in global supply chains, Western strategic alignment with India, Geopolitics and economic partnerships

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The post-COVID realignment of global supply chains and growing concerns about China's political assertiveness have positioned India as both a strategic partner and an economic alternative for Western nations.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Supply chain context: COVID-19 exposed over-dependence on China; US-China trade war accelerated China+1 strategy
      • India as manufacturing alternative: PLI schemes in semiconductors, electronics, pharmaceuticals; Apple, Samsung moving production to India
      • Strategic dimension: Quad (India-US-Japan-Australia) as Indo-Pacific security architecture to balance Chinese influence
      • Technology partnerships: US CHIPS Act, India Semiconductor Mission; critical mineral supply chain agreements
      • Trade agreements: India-EU FTA negotiations, India-US trade framework signal deepening economic ties
      • Limitations: India's FDI environment, infrastructure gaps, labor laws still challenge it as full alternative to China
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's rise as an alternative to China's supply chain dominance requires sustained domestic reforms alongside strategic international positioning to capitalize on the ongoing global realignment.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 — India's foreign policy, GS3 — Economic growth and industrial policy
  🌐 Contemporary: Apple's shift of significant iPhone production to India (2023-24) exemplifies this trend.

**[2017]** Q9. "China is using its economic relations and positive trade surplus as tools to develop potential military power status in Asia." In the light of this statement, discuss its impact on India as her neighbour.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: China's rise and India's strategic response, Economic interdependence vs security dilemma, India's neighbourhood policy

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** China's deliberate use of economic relationships and trade surpluses to build strategic influence fundamentally alters India's security calculus in Asia.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • China's tools: BRI investment ($1 trillion+), trade dependency creation, debt-trap diplomacy in India's neighbourhood
      • India-China trade: India's deficit of $80+ billion makes India economically vulnerable to Chinese leverage
      • Military-economic nexus: economic surplus funds PLA modernization, Beidou GPS, naval expansion
      • Impact on India: encirclement via String of Pearls (Gwadar, Hambantota, Chittagong), border infrastructure buildup at LAC
      • India's response: Atmanirbhar Bharat to reduce Chinese imports, ban on 300+ Chinese apps, FDI restrictions from border nations
      • Quad, IPEF, Indo-Pacific strategy as multilateral counterbalance to Chinese economic-military integration
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India must simultaneously diversify supply chains away from China, build countervailing partnerships, and invest in border infrastructure to effectively manage the security implications of China's economic-military strategy.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3: Internal security, GS3: Economy, GS2: India's foreign policy
  🌐 Contemporary: Highly relevant given Galwan clashes aftermath and ongoing LAC tensions.

**[2014]** Q16. With respect to the South China sea, maritime territorial disputes and rising tension affirm the need for safeguarding maritime security to ensure freedom of navigation and over flight throughout the region. In this context, discuss the bilateral issues between India and China.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHAT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Maritime security, India-China bilateral, Indo-Pacific order

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The South China Sea tensions are a prism through which India's evolving concerns about China's assertiveness in the maritime domain must be understood.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • South China Sea: China's Nine-Dash Line claim violates UNCLOS; disputes with Vietnam, Philippines, Malaysia
      • India's interest: Sea lanes of communication (SLOCs) — 55% of India's trade passes through South China Sea
      • India-China bilateral: LAC standoffs (Galwan 2020), debt-trap concerns in Indian Ocean (String of Pearls), trade deficit of $85 billion
      • India's approach: supports freedom of navigation, backs ASEAN centrality, deepens Quad without joining military alliances
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India must balance economic engagement with China against strategic vigilance, anchoring its maritime security in multilateral frameworks like UNCLOS.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 IR — India-China, GS3 — Maritime Security, IR — ASEAN relations
  🌐 Contemporary: Post-Galwan (2020) India-China relations remain strained with partial disengagement at LAC as of 2024.

**[2013]** Q19. What do you understand by 'The String of Pearls'? How does it impact India? Briefly outline the steps taken by India to counter this.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Maritime security, China's Indian Ocean strategy, India's strategic response

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** China's 'String of Pearls' strategy — developing naval-capable infrastructure across the Indian Ocean — represents a systematic effort to project maritime power into India's strategic backyard.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • String of Pearls: Chinese ports/military access in Pakistan (Gwadar), Sri Lanka (Hambantota), Bangladesh, Myanmar, Maldives, Djibouti
      • Strategic implications: potential naval encirclement, SLOCs (Sea Lines of Communication) vulnerability, IOR dominance
      • Economic dimension: BRI investments creating debt-trap dependencies giving China strategic leverage
      • Impact on India: strategic encirclement, threat to SLOCs, lost influence in neighborhood
      • India's countermeasures: SAGAR (Security and Growth for All in the Region), island territories development, Act East Policy
      • Diplomatic: engaging Sri Lanka (INS Airavat), Maldives (infrastructure), Bangladesh (connectivity)
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's response to the String of Pearls must combine maritime capacity building, active neighborhood diplomacy and multilateral partnerships through Quad and IORA to preserve its strategic primacy in the Indian Ocean Region.
  🔗 Interlinkages: IR — India-China relations, Internal Security — maritime security, IR — India's SAARC and Indian Ocean diplomacy
  🌐 Contemporary: Hambantota port lease, Maldives political changes and China's IOR presence make this perennially relevant.

**[2013]** Q20. Economic ties between India and Japan while growing in the recent years are still far below their potential. Elucidate the policy constraints which are inhibiting this growth.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: India-Japan economic ties, Investment barriers, Indo-Pacific economic cooperation

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Despite a robust strategic partnership, India-Japan economic ties remain far below their potential, constrained by structural and policy barriers on both sides.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Trade potential: complementary economies — Japan's capital/technology + India's market/labor — yet bilateral trade stagnates
      • India-side constraints: complex regulatory environment, land acquisition difficulties, labor laws, infrastructure gaps
      • Japan-side constraints: risk-aversion of Japanese firms, preference for ASEAN destinations, language/cultural barriers
      • Way forward: fast-track Japanese Industrial Townships, simplify FDI norms, leverage India-Japan Industrial Competitiveness Partnership
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Realizing the India-Japan economic partnership requires structural reforms in India alongside Japanese firms' willingness to accept higher risk for long-term gains.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 IR — India-Japan, GS3 Economy — FDI, GS3 — Infrastructure
  🌐 Contemporary: India-Japan cooperation on semiconductors, 5G, and green energy has gained strategic momentum in the post-2020 geopolitical realignment.

### GS3

**[2018]** Q9. The China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC) is viewed as a cardinal subset of China's larger 'One Belt One Road' initiative. Give a brief description of CPEC and enumerate the reasons why India has distanced itself from the same.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: China's BRI strategy, India's strategic concerns, Regional connectivity

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** CPEC, as the flagship BRI project through Pakistan-occupied Kashmir, directly challenges India's territorial integrity and strategic interests.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • CPEC description: ~$62 billion infrastructure corridor (roads, railways, pipelines, SEZs) from Gwadar to Kashgar
      • India's distance: passes through PoK (India's sovereign territory); sovereignty violation
      • Strategic concerns: Chinese naval presence at Gwadar threatens India's Indian Ocean interests; military implications
      • Economic concerns: debt-trap potential for Pakistan; China-Pakistan strategic axis strengthening
      • Geopolitical: encirclement narrative — Chinese ports in Sri Lanka, Bangladesh, Myanmar alongside Gwadar
      • India's alternatives: INSTC, IMEC (India-Middle East-Europe Corridor), Chabahar port development
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's firm stance against CPEC reflects core sovereignty concerns, necessitating proactive alternative connectivity strategies to counter BRI's regional influence.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 – International Relations, GS3 – Internal Security, GS1 – Geography
  🌐 Contemporary: IMEC launch at G20 2023 as India's counter-narrative to BRI makes this actively relevant.
