> Which one of the following States of India has passed a legislation (in 1996) making the maintenance of one’s parents mandatory?

     A) Kerala
     B) West Bengal
     C) Maharashtra
  ✅ D) Himachal Pradesh

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Kerala (known for progressive legislation) and Maharashtra are common distractors. Himachal Pradesh passed the Himachal Pradesh Parents Maintenance Act in 1996 — a niche state-specific fact.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Himachal Pradesh was the first state to pass a mandatory parental maintenance law (1996). The Maintenance of Parents Act at the national level came much later (2007). Eliminate more populous/prominent states.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Himachal Pradesh passed the HP Parents Maintenance Act, 1996 — first state-level mandatory parental maintenance law
     - National Maintenance of Parents and Senior Citizens Act came in 2007

### GS1

**[2023]** Q8. Do you think marriage as a sacrament is loosing its value in Modern India?
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Marriage and modernity, Social change, Family values

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The question of whether marriage as a sacrament is losing value reflects broader sociological tensions between traditional institutions and the individualising forces of modernity in contemporary India.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Traditional view: marriage as sanskara — sacred, irrevocable bond; religious ceremony central
      • Evidence of change: rising divorce rates (though still low), delayed marriages, live-in relationships
      • Urban educated youth: marriage increasingly seen as partnership of equals — contract elements growing
      • Supreme Court: live-in relationships given legal recognition — erosion of formal marriage primacy
      • Counter-evidence: marriage rates remain very high in India; arranged marriage still dominant
      • Regional variation: urban metropolitan India shows faster change; rural India relatively traditional
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While marriage's sacramental character is under pressure from individualisation and urbanisation, it remains a deeply entrenched institution in India — transformation rather than dissolution best describes current trends.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Society — family and kinship, Women's issues, Social change
  🌐 Contemporary: Supreme Court debates on right to cohabit and live-in relationship rights reflect evolving legal attitudes toward marriage.

**[2022]** Q8. Explore and evaluate the impact of 'Work From Home' on family relationships.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Changing family structures, Gender equity, Work-life balance, Digital inequality

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The COVID-19-induced shift to Work From Home has profoundly altered family relationships, creating both opportunities for deeper bonding and new sources of stress.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Positive impacts: increased time with family, parents more involved in children's education, reduced commute stress
      • Negative impacts: blurring of work-life boundaries causing burnout, increased domestic conflicts over space and noise
      • Gender dimension: WFH increased women's double burden — professional work plus disproportionate domestic labour
      • Children: disrupted routines, screen time increase, reduced peer socialisation
      • Elderly: mixed — more family contact but also exposure to anxiety/stress in household
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** WFH's long-term impact on families depends on enabling equitable division of domestic labour and creating digital infrastructure for balanced hybrid work.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 (society, gender), GS2 (health, mental health), GS3 (digital economy)
  🌐 Contemporary: Hybrid work models are now permanent in many sectors; India's IT sector WFH debate has direct family welfare implications.

**[2014]** Q13. The life cycle of a joint family depends on economic factors rather than social values. Discuss.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Changing family structures, Modernization and tradition, Sociology of kinship

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The debate over whether economic pressures or social values primarily determine the fate of the joint family system reflects the broader tension between tradition and modernity in Indian society.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Economic argument: urban migration, separate employment, high real estate costs drive nuclear families
      • M.N. Srinivas noted 'structural' joint family may break into 'functional' nuclear units
      • Social values: religious duty, filial piety, care for elderly still sustain joint families in rural areas
      • Economic benefits of joint family: shared childcare, pooled income, economies of scale
      • Evidence: IT professionals forming 'modified' joint families in cities — economic logic
      • Conclusion: both factors interact — economic stress fractures families that social values alone would hold together
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The joint family's trajectory is best explained by the interaction of economic compulsions and evolving social values rather than either factor alone.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Indian society, Urbanization, Women's issues
  🌐 Contemporary: COVID-19 pandemic led to a revival of joint family arrangements as economic safety nets.
