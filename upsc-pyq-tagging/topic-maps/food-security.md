**[Q3984]** [2020]
> Which of the following factors/policies were affecting the price of rice in India in the recent past?  1. Minimum Support Price 2. Government's trading 3. Government's stockpiling 4. Consumer subsidies  Select the correct answer using the code given below:

     A) 1, 2 and 4 only
     B) 1, 3 and 4 only
     C) 2 and 3 only
  ✅ D) 1, 2, 3 and 4

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may doubt that all four factors affect rice prices — but MSP (floor price), government trading (procurement/release), stockpiling (buffer stock), and consumer subsidies (PDS) all directly influence market rice prices.
  💡 **Eliminate:** All four — MSP (sets price floor), government trading (procurement and open market sales), stockpiling (affects supply), consumer subsidies (PDS reduces effective consumer price) — affect rice price. Answer is D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - MSP for paddy sets minimum price farmers receive — signals market floor
     - Government procurement and open market sales (OMSS) directly affect market supply
     - PDS subsidized distribution reduces effective consumer price below market

**[Q3572]** [2018]
> With reference to the provisions made under the National Food Security Act, 2013, consider the following statements :  1. The families coming under the category of 'below poverty line (BPL)' only are eligible to receive subsidised food grains. 2. The eldest woman in a household, of age 18 years or above, shall be the head of the household for the purpose of issuance of a ration card. 3. Pregnant women and lactating mothers are entitled to a 'take-home ration' of 1600 calories per day during pregnancy and for six months thereafter.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 and 2
  ✅ B) 2 only
     C) 1 and 3
     D) 3 only

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 1 is a major trap — NFSA 2013 is NOT only for BPL families; it covers Priority Households and Antyodaya Anna Yojana (AAY) — including some APL households. Statement 3 about 1600 calories is also wrong.
  💡 **Eliminate:** NFSA 2013: covers priority households (not just BPL) — statement 1 wrong. Eldest woman (18+) as household head for ration card — statement 2 correct. Pregnant women: 600 calories/day (not 1600) — statement 3 wrong. Answer: 2 only.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - NFSA 2013: covers 75% rural and 50% urban population — not just BPL
     - Eldest woman (18+) is head of household for ration card purposes under NFSA
     - Pregnant/lactating women: entitled to free meal or 600 calories/day (not 1600 calories)

**[Q3176]** [2016]
> Which of the following is/are the indicator/indicators used by IFPRI to compute the Global Hunger Index Report?  1. Undernourishment 2. Child stunting 3. Child mortality  Select the correct answer using the code given below.

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 and 3 only
  ✅ C) 1, 2 and 3
     D) 1 and 3 only

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Child wasting is often confused with child stunting. GHI now uses child wasting (not stunting alone) but earlier versions varied; candidates must recall current methodology.
  💡 **Eliminate:** GHI 2021 onwards uses: undernourishment + child stunting + child wasting + child mortality (4 indicators). But in 2016 context: undernourishment, child stunting, child wasting, child mortality. The question lists stunting not wasting — historically all three (1, 2, 3) were included. Answer: C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - GHI is calculated by IFPRI using undernourishment, child stunting, child wasting, and child mortality
     - India consistently ranks poorly on GHI due to high child malnutrition rates

**[Q3813]** [2019]
> The economic cost of food grains to the Food Corporation of India is Minimum Support Price and bonus (if any) paid to the farmers plus

     A) transportation cost only
     B) interest cost only
  ✅ C) procurement incidentals and distribution cost
     D) procurement incidentals and charges for godowns

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Options A and B are partial — transportation alone or interest alone do not fully capture FCI's economic cost. Option D (godowns charges) is included within procurement incidentals, making C the comprehensive answer.
  💡 **Eliminate:** FCI's economic cost = MSP + bonus + procurement incidentals (handling, storage, gunny bags etc.) + distribution cost. Answer is C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - FCI economic cost = MSP/procurement price + procurement incidentals + distribution cost
     - Economic cost is different from issue price (Central Issue Price) — gap is food subsidy
     - Economic cost determines the food subsidy burden on government

Government of India announces minimum support prices only for cereals. 3. For distribution under Targeted Public Distribution System (TPDS), wheat and rice are issued by the Government of India at uniform Central issue prices to the States/ Union Territories. Which of the statements given above is/ are correct?

     A) 1 and 2
     B) 2 only
  ✅ C) 1 and 3

     - India follows open-ended procurement, not target-based

     - Central Issue Price for TPDS is uniform across states

### GS2

**[2019]** Q7. There is a growing divergence in the relationship between poverty and hunger in India. The shrinking of social expenditure by the government is forcing the poor to spend more on non-food essential items squeezing their food-budget. — Elucidate.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Food security, Poverty-hunger nexus, Social expenditure

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The widening gap between poverty reduction and hunger elimination in India reflects a structural squeeze where rising non-food essential costs force the poor to compromise their nutritional intake.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Divergence mechanism: as healthcare costs, education fees, transport costs rise, poor households reduce food expenditure
      • Social expenditure shrinkage: reduction in state health/education subsidies increases out-of-pocket spending on these non-food essentials
      • Impact: caloric intake may remain adequate but nutrition quality (protein, micronutrients) declines
      • Data evidence: GHI rank, NFHS stunting data show nutritional poverty persisting despite income poverty reduction
      • Policy response needed: strengthen universal PDS with nutritional diversity, expand POSHAN Abhiyan, restore social sector spending
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Addressing the poverty-hunger divergence requires not just food transfers but comprehensive social protection that prevents non-food essential costs from crowding out nutritional spending.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: Governance — food security schemes, GS3: Economy — social sector spending, GS1: Society — poverty
  🌐 Contemporary: India's 111th rank in Global Hunger Index (contested but significant) and PM Poshan Shakti Nirman scheme are directly relevant.

**[2018]** Q17. How far do you agree with the view that the focus on lack of availability of food as the main cause of hunger takes the attention away from ineffective human development policies in India?
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Food security beyond availability, Human development and hunger, PDS and nutrition policy

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's paradox of food surplus amid pervasive hunger demonstrates that food availability is a necessary but insufficient condition for food security — the critical issue is ineffective human development policies that prevent the poor from converting food availability into nutritional outcomes.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Sen's entitlement theory: hunger from exchange entitlement failure — low wages, unemployment, discrimination
      • India's food surplus: >100 MT in FCI but >18% children severely malnourished (NFHS-5)
      • PDS limitations: targeting errors, exclusion errors, corruption, caloric focus (ignoring micronutrients)
      • Human development failures: poor WASH leading to malnutrition despite food intake (sanitation-nutrition link)
      • Women's nutritional status: LFPR, anemia in women affecting child nutrition
      • POSHAN Abhiyan: multi-sectoral convergence targeting stunting, wasting, malnutrition
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Ending hunger in food-surplus India requires moving beyond supply-side food distribution to demand-side human development — improving women's status, sanitation, healthcare access and livelihood security that enable poor households to translate food availability into nutritional security.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Society — women's health and nutrition, Governance — POSHAN Abhiyan, Economy — poverty and entitlement
  🌐 Contemporary: POSHAN 2.0, National Food Security Act and GHI ranking debates make this a live policy challenge.

### GS3

**[2024]** Q4. Explain the role of millets for ensuring health and nutritional security in India.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Millets for nutrition security, Climate-resilient agriculture

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** As India battles the dual burden of malnutrition and climate change, millets — the ancient 'Shree Anna' — offer a nutritionally dense, environmentally sustainable solution.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Health benefits: rich in iron, calcium, fibre, and micronutrients; low glycaemic index (good for diabetics)
      • Nutritional security: addressing hidden hunger among vulnerable populations through PDS integration
      • Climate resilience: drought-tolerant, require 30-40% less water than rice, hardy in marginal soils
      • Government push: POSHAN 2.0 integration, including millets in Mid-Day Meal/PM Poshan scheme
      • Promotion: Nutri-cereal rebranding as 'Shree Anna', international advocacy via G20 and UNFCCC
      • Economic opportunity: premium export market for organic millets
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Mainstreaming millets in PDS, school nutrition programmes, and export policy can simultaneously address nutritional security, farmer income, and climate adaptation.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 Economy — Agriculture, GS2 Governance — Health & Education, Environment — Climate Change
  🌐 Contemporary: 2023 International Year of Millets and POSHAN 2.0 have placed millets at the centre of India's food and nutrition security strategy.

**[2024]** Q14. Elucidate the importance of buffer stocks for stabilizing agricultural prices in India. What are the challenges associated with the storage of buffer stock? Discuss.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHY | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Buffer stocks and price stability, Agricultural storage challenges

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Buffer stocks serve as a critical instrument for stabilizing agricultural prices and ensuring food security, but their management poses significant logistical and fiscal challenges.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Importance: cushion against supply shocks, price stabilization during shortfall, ensure PDS supply, food sovereignty
      • Price stabilization: OMSS releases grains in open market to cool inflation; buffer norms set quarterly
      • FCI's role: procurement at MSP, storage, distribution
      • Storage challenges: inadequate cold storage (especially for perishables), high storage losses (5-10% for grains), rodent/pest damage
      • Fiscal burden: carrying cost of buffer stocks is significant (interest + storage + handling)
      • Reforms needed: Warehouse Receipts System, private sector participation in storage, silo construction
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Buffer stocks are indispensable for food security but require modernized storage infrastructure, reduced fiscal burden through targeted stockholding, and integration with futures markets.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 Economy — Agriculture, GS2 Governance — Food Security, Poverty & Hunger
  🌐 Contemporary: The 2022-23 wheat export ban and OMSS releases during inflationary periods demonstrate the active role of buffer stocks in India's food price management.

**[2022]** Q3. What are the major challenges of Public Distribution System (PDS) in India ? How can it be made effective and transparent?
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: PDS challenges and reforms, Food security and last-mile delivery, Technology in welfare schemes

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Despite being one of the world's largest food distribution networks, India's Public Distribution System faces systemic challenges that undermine its food security objectives.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Major challenges: Exclusion errors (poor households not identified); inclusion errors (ineligible beneficiaries); leakages and diversion estimated at 40%+; poor grain quality at FPS; inadequate beneficiary awareness
      • Geographical and logistical challenges: Remote area supply, inadequate cold storage, road connectivity issues affecting timely delivery
      • Making PDS effective: End-to-end IT (e-PoS machines, digitised beneficiary database, Aadhaar seeding); One Nation One Ration Card for migrant workers; doorstep delivery pilots
      • Transparency: Social audits under Right to Food campaign; online grievance redressal; Jan Poshan Kendras; SMS alerts to beneficiaries
      • Reform alternatives: Nutrition-sensitive PDS (fortified foods); direct benefit transfer debate — cash vs kind; millet integration under Atmanirbhar Krishi
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Transforming PDS into a transparent, tech-enabled system with convergence across nutrition and social protection schemes is essential for realising India's food security commitments.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: NFSA 2013 and right to food, GS3: Food security and FCI, GS2: Governance — DBT and Aadhaar
  🌐 Contemporary: PMGKAY (free grain scheme extended multiple times) has expanded PDS reach significantly post-COVID.

**[2021]** Q13. What are the salient features of the National Food Security Act, 2013? How has the Food Security Bill helped in eliminating hunger and malnutrition in India?
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Food security, Right to food, Welfare state

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The National Food Security Act 2013 represents a landmark shift from welfare to rights-based approach to food security, enshrining entitlements for over two-thirds of India's population.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Salient features: legal entitlement to subsidized foodgrains, 5 kg/person/month at ₹1-3/kg
      • Coverage: 81.35 crore people, Antyodaya families get 35 kg/month
      • Maternity benefit of ₹6000, meal to pregnant/lactating mothers
      • Mid-day meal and ICDS integrated
      • Grievance redressal mechanism and Vigilance Committees at PDS level
      • Limitations: doesn't address nutritional security, pulses/oil not covered, implementation gaps
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While NFSA has significantly expanded food entitlements, transitioning from caloric security to comprehensive nutritional security requires expanding the Act's scope and improving implementation.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Nutrition (stunting, wasting), MGNREGS, Women's empowerment
  🌐 Contemporary: PMGKAY extended free grain scheme beyond NFSA entitlements till December 2023.

**[2019]** Q13. What are the reformative steps taken by the Government to make food grain distribution system more effective?
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Food security, PDS reforms, Governance efficiency

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Reforming India's food grain distribution system has been a persistent governance challenge, with recent technology-driven and portability-based reforms showing promise.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • End-to-end computerization of PDS: digitization of ration cards, supply chain monitoring
      • One Nation One Ration Card (ONORC): enables migrants to access PDS anywhere
      • Aadhaar-based biometric authentication to reduce ghost beneficiaries
      • Private participation in warehousing under WDRA
      • Decentralized procurement for local grains; increasing MSP support
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Technological modernization and portability reforms have significantly improved PDS efficiency, though last-mile delivery challenges in remote areas persist.
  🔗 Interlinkages: NFSA, Aadhaar, Migration and labour
  🌐 Contemporary: Free food grain scheme under PMGKAY during COVID demonstrated system resilience.

**[2013]** Q4. Food Security Bill is expected to eliminate hunger and malnutrition in India. Critically discuss various apprehensions in its effective implementation along with the concerns it has generated in WTO.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Food security legislation, WTO-India tensions, Implementation challenges

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The National Food Security Act 2013 is a landmark legislation aiming to eliminate hunger, but its implementation faces systemic and international challenges.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Apprehensions in implementation: PDS leakages, targeting errors, FCI storage capacity, fiscal burden
      • Supply side: dependence on MSP-based procurement, limited crop diversification
      • WTO concerns: food subsidies may exceed Aggregate Measure of Support limits under AoA
      • Peace Clause: interim protection but subject to transparency and consultation obligations
      • Malnutrition angle: NFSA addresses caloric security but not nutritional diversity (protein, micronutrients)
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** True food security requires not just entitlement legislation but structural reforms in PDS, crop diversification, and nutritional supplementation.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Economy, GS2 – Governance, GS2 – International Relations
  🌐 Contemporary: Ongoing WTO negotiations on public stockholding for food security remain unresolved, with India leading developing country bloc.
