**[Q4196]** [2021]
> With reference to Indian judiciary, consider the following statements :  1. Any retired judge of the Supreme Court of India can be called back to sit and act as a Supreme Court judge by the Chief Justice of India with prior permission of the President of India. 2. A High Court in India has the power to review its own judgement as the Supreme Court does.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

  ✅ A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
     C) Both 1 and 2
     D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 is wrong: unlike the Supreme Court, High Courts do NOT have inherent review power under Article 137 (that power belongs only to SC); students often assume both courts have review powers.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Article 128 allows retired SC judges to be recalled by CJI with President's permission — statement 1 is correct; High Courts lack power to review their own judgments (Article 137 is SC-specific) — statement 2 is wrong.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Article 128: CJI can recall retired SC judges with President's permission
     - Article 137: Review power available only to Supreme Court, not High Courts

**[Q2867]** [2014]
> The power of the Supreme Court of India to decide disputes between the Centre and the States falls under its

     A) advisory jurisdiction
     B) appellate jurisdiction
  ✅ C) original jurisdiction
     D) writ jurisdiction

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may confuse original jurisdiction (Centre-State disputes) with appellate jurisdiction (appeals from High Courts); Centre-State disputes are original jurisdiction as SC is the first forum.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Original jurisdiction (Article 131) = SC has first and exclusive authority to decide Centre-State and inter-State disputes; appellate = appeals from lower courts — answer is C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Article 131: Supreme Court's original jurisdiction for Centre-State and inter-State disputes
     - Original jurisdiction means SC is the FIRST court to hear the case, not an appeal

**[2025]** Q13. Discuss the evolution of collegium system in India. Critically examine the advantages and disadvantages of the system of appointment of the Judges of the Supreme Court of India and that of the USA.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Judicial appointments, Collegium system, Comparative constitutional law

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's collegium system represents an evolutionary judicial response to protect independence from executive influence, but its opacity and accountability gaps invite comparison with more transparent yet politically exposed appointment models like the US.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Evolution: SP Gupta (1981) — executive primacy; Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record (1993) — collegium primacy; Third Judges Case (1998) — five-senior-judge collegium
      • Advantages: insulated from political patronage, protects judicial independence
      • Disadvantages: opaque, no accountability, nepotism allegations, diversity deficit
      • NJAC (2014): Article 124A — joint judicial-executive body; struck down in 2015 as violating basic structure
      • US model: President nominates, Senate confirms — transparent but politically polarized (Kavanaugh, Barrett controversies)
      • Reforms proposed: MoP (Memorandum of Procedure) reform, independent secretariat for collegium
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's ideal judicial appointment system should preserve independence while incorporating transparency and diversity requirements — a reformed collegium with structured criteria, documented reasoning and diversity targets offers the best balance.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Polity — basic structure doctrine, Governance — judicial accountability, IR — comparative constitutional systems
  🌐 Contemporary: Ongoing MoP negotiations and SC's diversity deficit debates keep judicial appointments in policy spotlight.

**[2024]** Q2. Explain and distinguish between Lok Adalats and Arbitration Tribunals. Whether they entertain civil as well as criminal cases?
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: VS | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Alternative Dispute Resolution mechanisms, Access to justice, Judicial reforms

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Lok Adalats and Arbitration Tribunals are both Alternative Dispute Resolution mechanisms aimed at reducing judicial burden, but they differ fundamentally in their legal basis, procedure, and jurisdiction.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Lok Adalats: Created under Legal Services Authorities Act 1987; based on conciliation and compromise; award is final decree — not appealable in any court
      • Arbitration: Governed by Arbitration and Conciliation Act 1996; adjudicative in nature; award can be challenged under Section 34 on limited grounds
      • Civil jurisdiction: Both can handle civil disputes; Lok Adalats are popular for motor accident claims, matrimonial disputes, compoundable offences
      • Criminal jurisdiction: Lok Adalats can handle compoundable criminal offences only; Arbitration Tribunals generally handle civil/commercial disputes, not criminal cases
      • Distinction: Lok Adalat relies on mutual consent; arbitration is binding adjudication
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While both mechanisms reduce court burden, Lok Adalats are more accessible for common citizens, whereas arbitration is better suited for commercial and contractual disputes.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 — Judiciary and dispute resolution, GS2 — Access to justice
  🌐 Contemporary: India's push for commercial arbitration hub status and the Mediation Act 2023 make ADR increasingly relevant.

**[2024]** Q14. Explain the reasons for the growth of public interest litigation in India. As a result of it, has the Indian Supreme Court emerged as the world's most powerful judiciary?
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHY | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Judicial activism, PIL and access to justice, Supreme Court's global standing

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The extraordinary growth of Public Interest Litigation in India transformed the Supreme Court from a constitutional interpreter into an active participant in governance, raising both the promise of justice and concerns about judicial overreach.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • PIL growth reasons: poverty barriers to justice, illiterate litigants, executive unresponsiveness, activist judiciary
      • PIL innovations: epistolary jurisdiction (letter as petition), amicus curiae, suo motu cognizance
      • Landmark PILs: Hussainara Khatoon (undertrial prisoners), Vishaka (sexual harassment), MC Mehta (environment)
      • PIL expanded SC's role into governance: monitoring scheme implementation, directing executive action
      • Critics: judicial overreach, policy-making through PIL, pendency crisis worsened by frivolous PILs
      • World's most powerful?: unique combination of PIL jurisdiction, basic structure, contempt power makes SC distinctive
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While PIL has made the Supreme Court uniquely responsive to citizen needs and arguably the world's most accessible apex court, avoiding mission creep into executive governance requires calibrated judicial self-restraint.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Polity — fundamental rights enforcement, Governance — judicial oversight of government schemes, Environment — MC Mehta PILs
  🌐 Contemporary: Continuing SC intervention in electoral bonds, sedition law, Delhi air quality management keep PIL at the governance frontier.

**[2023]** Q1. "Constitutionally guaranteed judicial independence is a prerequisite of democracy." Comment.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Judicial independence, Democracy and rule of law, Constitutional safeguards

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Judicial independence is not merely a constitutional formality but the cornerstone of democratic governance, ensuring the rule of law over the rule of men.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Constitutional guarantees: security of tenure (impeachment only by Parliament), non-reducible salary, separation from executive (Art. 50)
      • Democracy link: courts as counter-majoritarian check — protect minority rights against legislative overreach
      • Indian experience: NJAC struck down (2015) — SC reaffirmed collegium to prevent executive capture
      • Challenges: executive influence through appointment delays, contempt powers limiting criticism, pendency undermining accessibility
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Genuine judicial independence requires not only constitutional provisions but institutional culture, transparency in appointments, and accountability without subordination.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 Polity — judiciary, GS2 Governance, GS4 Ethics — institutional integrity
  🌐 Contemporary: Debates over collegium transparency, judicial appointments delays, and high court vacancies remain live governance issues.

**[2022]** Q1. "The most significant achievement of modern law in India is the constitutionalization of environmental problems by the Supreme Court." Discuss this statement with the help of relevant case laws.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Constitutionalization of environmental rights via Article 21, Key environmental case laws by Supreme Court, Limits of judicial intervention in environmental governance

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's Supreme Court, through creative interpretation of Article 21, has transformed environmental protection from a statutory right into a constitutional imperative — a development widely recognized as a defining achievement of modern Indian law.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Subhash Kumar v. Bihar (1991): SC held right to live in pollution-free environment is part of Art 21
      • MC Mehta cases: Ganga pollution (1988), Delhi vehicular pollution, Taj Mahal heritage protection — all used PIL to impose environmental obligations
      • Vellore Citizens Forum (1996): SC adopted precautionary principle and polluter pays as part of Indian law
      • Absolute liability (Oleum Gas Leak, 1987): overruled Rylands v. Fletcher; imposed strict no-fault liability on hazardous industries
      • Limitations: SC lacks technical expertise; orders sometimes conflict with democratic policymaking; implementation deficit
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The constitutionalization of environmental law by the Supreme Court is a significant achievement, but durable environmental protection ultimately requires legislative action, institutional capacity and executive enforcement.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 Environment, GS2 Polity, Governance
  🌐 Contemporary: NGT's role and SC's environmental activism remain central to India's environmental governance framework.

**[2018]** Q12. How far do you agree with the view that tribunals curtail the jurisdiction of ordinary courts? In view of the above, discuss the constitutional validity and competency of the tribunals in India.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Tribunal system, Judicial independence, Access to justice

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The constitutional validity of tribunals in India rests on a delicate balance between enabling specialized adjudication and preserving the Supreme Court's position as the ultimate constitutional arbiter through judicial review.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Tribunals created under Articles 323A and 323B to reduce burden on regular courts
      • Argument that they curtail courts: tribunals have exclusive jurisdiction in service, tax, environment matters
      • L. Chandra Kumar (1997): SC ruled tribunals cannot exclude High Court judicial review under Article 226/227
      • SC upheld constitutional validity but with safeguards: judicial member requirement, independence of members
      • Problems: short tenures, executive influence on appointments, inadequate infrastructure
      • Reforms needed: stronger independence, NJAC-like body for tribunal appointments
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Tribunals serve a vital function in specialized dispute resolution but must be structurally insulated from executive influence and their decisions must remain subject to High Court judicial review to preserve constitutional integrity.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Polity — separation of powers, Governance — administrative law, Environment — NGT and environmental adjudication
  🌐 Contemporary: Tribunals Reforms Act 2021 being challenged in Supreme Court makes this an actively evolving legal area.

**[2017]** Q2. Critically examine the Supreme Court's judgement on 'National Judicial Appointments Commission Act, 2014' with reference to appointment of judges of higher judiciary in India.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Judicial independence and accountability, Appointment of judges and basic structure, Executive-judiciary relations in India

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The Supreme Court's 2015 judgment striking down the NJAC Act affirmed judicial independence as a non-negotiable component of the basic structure, while reigniting debate about accountability in judicial appointments.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • NJAC composition: CJI + 2 senior-most SC judges + Law Minister + 2 eminent persons — gave executive a role in appointments
      • SC's reasoning: judicial independence (part of basic structure) compromised by executive participation; veto-power of eminent persons problematic
      • Justice Chelameswar's dissent: Collegium lacks transparency; NJAC should be reformed, not struck down
      • Post-judgment: SC sought reform of Collegium through revised MoP; MoP negotiations between executive and judiciary remain unresolved
      • Criticism of Collegium: opacity, lack of diversity, nepotism allegations, absence of written criteria
      • Way forward: balanced system with transparency, written criteria, diversity targets while preserving judicial primacy
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While the NJAC judgment correctly safeguarded judicial independence, the Collegium system's lack of transparency demands structural reform that ensures both independence and accountable appointment processes.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: Polity — judiciary, Constitutional framework — basic structure, Governance — judicial accountability
  🌐 Contemporary: NJAC vs Collegium debate continues with government-judiciary tensions over appointments.

### Common Trap Patterns on This Topic
- Constitutional jurisdiction (Option D) sounds most appropriate for Supreme Court's role in interpreting the Constitution. But 'constitutional jurisdiction' is not a standard legal category; original jurisdiction is the correct term.
- The assertion and reason are both individually true, tempting students to pick option A; the reason does not directly explain the assertion — contempt powers are not the source of judicial activism.
- Students often assume the Attorney General must be a Parliament member (as in UK), but the Indian constitution only requires AG to have SC judge qualifications, not parliamentary membership.
- Students may think the Supreme Court can give advisory opinions on its own initiative (like constitutional bodies in some countries); option C (only on Fundamental Rights) is a common distractor.
- Students confuse the President with Parliament for expanding Supreme Court's jurisdiction; the President appoints judges but Parliament has legislative power to expand jurisdiction.
