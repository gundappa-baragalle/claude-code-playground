### GS3

**[2024]** Q17. What is disaster resilience? How is it determined? Describe various elements of a resilience framework. Also mention the global targets of Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction (2015-2030).
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Disaster governance, Climate resilience, International DRR frameworks

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Disaster resilience — the capacity of communities and systems to absorb shocks, adapt and recover — has emerged as the central paradigm of modern disaster risk reduction, operationalized through the Sendai Framework.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Definition: Resilience = absorptive capacity + adaptive capacity + transformative capacity
      • Determinants: institutional capacity, early warning systems, infrastructure quality, community awareness, financial reserves
      • Resilience framework elements: risk assessment, governance, preparedness, response, recovery, Build Back Better
      • Sendai targets: reduce mortality, reduce affected people, reduce economic losses, reduce critical infrastructure damage, increase strategies by 2020, enhance international cooperation, increase multi-hazard early warning access
      • India's steps: NDMA, SDMA, State DM plans, NDRF, linking DRR with SDGs
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Building genuine disaster resilience requires mainstreaming DRR into development planning and governance, not treating it as a post-disaster response mechanism.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3: Disaster management, GS2: Governance and institutional mechanisms, Environment: Climate change adaptation
  🌐 Contemporary: Increasing frequency of extreme weather events makes disaster resilience a top policy priority for India.

**[2020]** Q18. Discuss the initiatives mentioned in disaster management by the Government of India for the development of disaster resilient infrastructure.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Disaster resilient infrastructure, Government initiatives, Sendai Framework implementation

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India has moved from a reactive disaster response approach to proactive disaster risk reduction, with resilient infrastructure at the centre of its strategy.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Coalition for Disaster Resilient Infrastructure (CDRI): India launched CDRI at UNGA 2019 — a global partnership to promote resilient infrastructure in developing nations
      • National Disaster Management Authority (NDMA): Formulates guidelines for disaster resilient construction — earthquake-resistant buildings, flood-proof roads, cyclone shelters
      • PMGSY and rural roads: Standards upgraded for resilience against floods and landslides
      • Cyclone resilient infrastructure: Gujarat and Odisha models — reinforced pucca houses, early warning systems reduced cyclone deaths dramatically
      • Sendai Framework alignment: India's National DRR Plan 2019-2030 aligns with Sendai's priorities: understand risk, strengthen governance, invest in resilience
      • Smart Cities Mission: Incorporates climate resilience and disaster risk reduction in urban infrastructure planning
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's leadership in CDRI, combined with domestic policy reforms, positions it as a global champion for disaster resilient infrastructure, though implementation gaps at sub-national level remain.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3: Disaster management, environment, GS2: Government schemes, IR: India's global climate commitments
  🌐 Contemporary: CDRI's importance grew after COVID-19 highlighted need for resilient health infrastructure; India also chairs CDRI.

**[2019]** Q18. Disaster preparedness is the first step in any disaster management process. Explain how hazard zonation mapping will help disaster mitigation in the case of landslides.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Disaster preparedness, Landslide mitigation, Land use planning

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Disaster preparedness through scientific hazard assessment, particularly hazard zonation mapping, is more cost-effective than post-disaster response in landslide-prone regions.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Hazard zonation: classifies areas by susceptibility based on slope, geology, rainfall, vegetation
      • Uses: land use planning, construction regulations, evacuation route planning
      • Landslide early warning systems integrated with real-time rainfall data
      • NDMA guidelines for landslide management: do's and don'ts
      • Role of GSI in mapping; satellite-based monitoring of slope movements
      • Community awareness and capacity building in vulnerable areas
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Systematic hazard zonation mapping, integrated with urban planning and early warning systems, can dramatically reduce landslide fatalities and economic losses.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Climate change (intensifying rainfall), Urban planning, Infrastructure development
  🌐 Contemporary: Increasing extreme rainfall events due to climate change are triggering more frequent landslides.

**[2018]** Q18. Describe various measures taken in India for Disaster Risk Reduction (DRR) before and after signing 'Sendai Framework for DRR (2015-2030)'. How is this framework different from 'Hyogo Framework for Action, 2005'?
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: VS | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: International DRR frameworks, India's disaster management evolution, Risk reduction policy

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's disaster risk reduction journey has been shaped by international frameworks, culminating in the adoption of the Sendai Framework as the guiding vision.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Sendai vs Hyogo: Sendai has 4 priorities vs Hyogo's 5; Sendai explicitly measures risk reduction outcomes; Hyogo focused on capacities
      • Sendai priorities: risk understanding, risk governance, risk reduction investment, disaster preparedness for effective response
      • Pre-Sendai measures: NDMA 2005, DM Act, NDRF, National Cyclone Risk Mitigation Project
      • Post-Sendai: National DRR Policy 2019, NDMA-aligned state plans, Sendai monitoring integration
      • India's specific measures: cyclone resilient infrastructure (ODISHA model), earthquake hazard mapping, flood forecasting enhancement
      • Innovation: Coalition for Disaster Resilient Infrastructure (CDRI) launched by India at UNGA 2019
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's DRR framework has matured significantly, with Sendai providing the international benchmark while CDRI demonstrates India's global leadership in disaster resilience.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Disaster Management, GS2 – Governance, GS2 – International Relations
  🌐 Contemporary: CDRI and Sendai mid-term review (2023) highlight India's evolving global DRR leadership.

**[2017]** Q18. On December 2004, tsunami brought havoc on fourteen countries including India. Discuss the factors responsible for occurrence of tsunami and its effects on life and economy. In the light of guidelines of NDMA (2010) describe the mechanisms for preparedness to reduce the risk during such events.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Tsunami preparedness, NDMA guidelines, Disaster risk reduction

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The 2004 tsunami was a watershed event that prompted India to establish robust disaster management institutions and preparedness frameworks.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Factors: Indian Ocean subduction zone earthquake (M 9.1 off Sumatra); vertical seafloor displacement creates waves
      • Effects: coastal devastation, fishing community deaths, infrastructure loss, long-term PTSD
      • NDMA mechanisms (2010): Community awareness and mock drills; evacuation routes; coastal warning towers; Indian Tsunami Early Warning System (INCOIS)
      • ITEWS: operational since 2007; can issue warning within 20 minutes of earthquake
      • Preparedness: mock tsunamis, cyclone shelter repurposing, coastal zoning enforcement
      • Building code: CRZ regulations restrict construction in inundation zones
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While India has significantly improved tsunami preparedness since 2004, continued investment in early warning technology and community drills remains essential.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Disaster Management, GS1 – Oceanography, GS2 – Governance
  🌐 Contemporary: Any major Andaman-Nicobar seismic event could trigger tsunamis; India's INCOIS readiness is being continuously upgraded.

**[2016]** Q15. The frequency of urban floods due to high intensity rainfall is increasing over the years. Discussing the reasons for urban floods, highlight the mechanisms for preparedness to reduce the risk during such events.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: HOW | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Urban disaster risk, Climate adaptation, Urban planning reform

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The increasing frequency of urban floods reflects both intensifying rainfall patterns and systemic failures in urban planning and drainage infrastructure.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Reasons: concretization of natural drainage channels, encroachment on floodplains, inadequate stormwater drains, urban heat islands increasing rainfall intensity
      • Examples: Mumbai 2005, Chennai 2015, Bengaluru 2022 — each revealed planning gaps
      • Preparedness mechanisms: integrated urban drainage masterplans, retention ponds, green infrastructure
      • Sponge city concept: permeable surfaces, green roofs, urban wetlands for water absorption
      • NDMA guidelines: early warning systems, evacuation protocols, community preparedness
      • AMRUT: dedicated fund for urban infrastructure including storm drains
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Addressing urban floods requires integrating climate risk into urban master plans, enforcing building codes, and investing in nature-based solutions.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Disaster Management, GS1 – Climatology, GS2 – Governance
  🌐 Contemporary: Bengaluru 2022 floods and increasing extreme rainfall events make this a recurring contemporary issue.

**[2015]** Q16. The frequency of earthquakes appears to have increased in the Indian subcontinent. However, India's preparedness for mitigating their impact has significant gaps. Discuss various aspects.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: IMPACT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Disaster preparedness, Seismic risk, Urban vulnerability

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's position at the collision zone of the Indian and Eurasian plates makes it inherently seismically active, yet preparedness for mitigating human and economic losses remains critically inadequate.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Increased frequency: Himalayan seismicity driven by ongoing plate collision; peninsular India showing increased intraplate seismicity
      • Gaps in preparedness: non-compliance with earthquake-resistant building codes, poor enforcement especially in informal housing
      • Early warning: no functional nationwide earthquake early warning system unlike Japan
      • Institutional gaps: NDMA earthquake management guidelines exist but implementation at local body level weak
      • Lessons from Gujarat 2001 (26,000 deaths) and Sikkim 2011: need for retrofitting and community awareness
      • Way forward: mandatory BIS code compliance, microzonation mapping, community-level preparedness drills
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Bridging India's seismic preparedness gap requires mandatory earthquake-resistant construction enforcement, functional early warning systems, and community-centric disaster risk reduction.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Geomorphology — plate tectonics, GS3: Disaster management, GS2: Urban governance
  🌐 Contemporary: Turkey 2023 earthquake and Delhi's seismic vulnerability keep urban earthquake preparedness in focus.

**[2014]** Q14. Drought has been recognized as a disaster in view of its spatial expanse, temporal duration, slow onset and lasting effects on vulnerable sections. With a focus on the September 2010 guidelines from the National Disaster Management Authority (NDMA), discuss the mechanisms for preparedness to deal with likely El Niño and La Niña fallouts in India.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: HOW | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Climate variability, Drought management, Disaster preparedness

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's vulnerability to El Niño-induced droughts and La Niña-induced floods requires a differentiated preparedness approach guided by NDMA's 2010 drought guidelines.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • NDMA 2010 drought guidelines: drought declaration criteria, relief mechanisms, inter-ministerial coordination
      • El Niño preparedness: early crop advisory (alternate drought-resistant varieties), contingency agriculture plans
      • La Niña preparedness: flood warning systems, embankment strengthening, evacuation protocols
      • IMD seasonal forecasts: critical input for NDMA/state DMA preparedness triggers
      • Mechanisms: National Crisis Management Committee, State Relief Commissioners, SDRF/NDRF activation
      • Integration with Krishi Vigyan Kendras for localized agro-advisory
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's disaster preparedness for ENSO-related extremes must be anchored in real-time climate monitoring, pre-positioned relief, and localized agricultural contingency planning.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3: Disaster management, GS1: Climatology, GS3: Agriculture
  🌐 Contemporary: 2023 El Niño's impact on Indian monsoon and subsequent La Niña watch (2024) make this analysis timely.

**[2013]** Q18. How important are vulnerability and risk assessment for pre-disaster management ? As an administrator, what are key areas that you would focus on in a Disaster Management System.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Disaster risk reduction, Pre-disaster preparedness, Governance of disasters

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Vulnerability and risk assessment are the foundation of effective pre-disaster management, enabling targeted interventions before catastrophe strikes.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Importance: identifies high-risk populations, critical infrastructure vulnerabilities, and geographic hotspots
      • Risk assessment components: hazard assessment, exposure mapping, vulnerability profiling
      • Administrative focus areas: early warning systems (IMD, INCOIS), mock drills, community preparedness
      • NDMA's role: guidelines, capacity building, SDMA coordination
      • Technology: satellite-based monitoring, GIS mapping, flood/drought monitoring systems

  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Effective disaster management demands shifting from reactive response to proactive vulnerability reduction, with robust risk assessments driving targeted preparedness investments.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3: Disaster management, GS1: Geography (climate hazards), Governance: NDMA
  🌐 Contemporary: Uttarakhand floods, Odisha cyclone preparedness, and NDRF's expanding role exemplify this framework.
