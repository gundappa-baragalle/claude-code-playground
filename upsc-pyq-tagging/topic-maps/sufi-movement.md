**[Q2560]** [2012]
> With reference to the religious history of medieval India, the Sufi mystics were known to pursue which of the following practices?  1. Meditation and control of breath 2. Severe ascetic exercises in a lonely place 3. Recitation of holy songs to arouse a state of ecstasy in their audience.  Select the correct answer using the codes given below;

     A) 1 and 2 only
     B) 2 and 3 only
     C) 3 only
  ✅ D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may hesitate on statement 2 (severe asceticism) thinking Sufis were more moderate than Hindu ascetics; but some Sufi orders did practice austerities.
  💡 **Eliminate:** All three practices — breath control/meditation (1), seclusion/ascetic exercises (2), and Sama/Qawwali recitation for ecstasy (3) — are part of Sufi practices. Answer: D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Sama (Qawwali) is central to Sufi practice for inducing spiritual ecstasy (wajd)
     - Chilla (40-day seclusion) is a Sufi ascetic practice

**[Q318]** [1997]
> Assertion (A) : The sponsor and the most prominent figure of the Chisti order of Sufis in India is Khwaja Moinuddin Chisti. Reason (R) : The Chisti order takes its name from a village Chisti in Ajmer. In the context of the above two statements, which one of the following is correct ?

     A) Both A and R are true and R is the correct explanation of A
     B) Both A and R are true, but R is not a correct explanation of A
  ✅ C) A is true, but R is false
     D) A is false, but R is true

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement A says Moinuddin Chisti is the sponsor/most prominent figure — this is true. But R says Chisti order is named after Ajmer — this is incorrect; it is named after Chisht village in Afghanistan.
  💡 **Eliminate:** A is true — Moinuddin Chisti is indeed the foremost figure of the Chisti order in India. R is false — the order is named after Chisht near Herat (Afghanistan), not Ajmer. Select C (A true, R false).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Chisti order named after Chisht village near Herat, Afghanistan
     - Khwaja Moinuddin Chisti brought Chishtiya to India (settled in Ajmer)

**[Q243]** [1996]
> The sufi saint who maintained that devotional music was one way of coming close to God was

  ✅ A) Muin-ud-din Chishti
     B) Baba Farid
     C) Saiyid Muhammad Gesudaraz
     D) Shah Alam Bukhari

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Baba Farid and Saiyid Muhammad Gesudaraz are also famous Chishti saints associated with devotion; the founder of the Chishti order in India (Muin-ud-din Chishti) specifically popularized devotional music (Sama/Qawwali).
  💡 **Eliminate:** Khwaja Muin-ud-din Chishti of Ajmer is the founder of the Chishti Sufi order in India and specifically championed devotional music (Sama) as a path to God. Answer: A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Muin-ud-din Chishti founded Chishti order in India; popularized Qawwali/Sama as devotional music
     - Chishti order is known for its emphasis on music (Sama) unlike the Naqshbandi order which opposed it

**[Q2887]** [2014]
> A community of people called Manganiyars is well-known for their

     A) martial arts in North-East India
  ✅ B) musical tradition in North-West India
     C) classical vocal music in South India
     D) pietra dura tradition in Central India

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Manganiyars are often confused with classical musicians — they are hereditary folk musicians from the Thar Desert region of Rajasthan (northwest India), known for their traditional music.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Manganiyars are Muslim hereditary folk musicians from Rajasthan (northwest India) — not martial artists, not classical vocalists, not pietra dura craftsmen. Answer is B.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Manganiyars are hereditary folk musicians from Barmer-Jaisalmer region of Rajasthan
     - They perform for Rajput and other patron communities
     - Known for their distinctive sarangi and khartal playing

**[Q1162]** [2002]
> With reference to Sufism in Indian history, consider the following statements: 1. Shaikh Ahmad Sarhandi was a contemporary of Ibrahim Lodi 2. Shiakh Nasiruddin Chirag-i-Dehlavi was a disciple of Shaikh Nizamuddin Auliya 3. Aurangzeb was contemporary of Shaikh Salim Chisti 4. The Qadiri order of Sufis was first introduced in India by Shaikh Niamtullah and Makhdum Muhammad Jilani Which of these statements are correct?

     A) 1 and 2
     B) 1 and 3
     C) 2 and 3
  ✅ D) 2 and 4

  ⚠️ **Trap:** The four Sufi orders, silsilas, and their chronology are complex; statements mixing contemporaries (Aurangzeb and Salim Chisti) or Sarhindi with wrong rulers create traps.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Shaikh Nasiruddin Chirag-i-Dehlavi was indeed a disciple of Nizamuddin Auliya (correct); Qadiri order was introduced by Shaikh Niamtullah and Makhdum Jilani (correct) — answer D (2 and 4).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Shaikh Nasiruddin Chirag-i-Dehlavi was the chief disciple of Nizamuddin Auliya of Chishti order
     - Qadiri order was brought to India by Shaikh Muhammad Ghauth and was patronized in the south

### GS1

**[2014]** Q5. Sufis and medieval mystic saints failed to modify either the religious ideas and practices or the outward structure of Hindu/Muslim societies to any appreciable extent. Comment.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Religious reform and social change, Limits of medieval reformism, Syncretic traditions

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** While Sufi and medieval mystic saints contributed profoundly to spiritual syncretism and cultural exchange, their impact on reforming the rigid social and religious structures remained limited.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Spiritual impact: promoted love, devotion, and inter-religious tolerance — contributed to composite culture
      • Limitation: saints like Kabir preached equality but caste hierarchy persisted in social practice
      • Sufi khanqahs attracted followers across faiths but did not dismantle religious orthodoxy or caste-based discrimination
      • Bhakti saints challenged priesthood but their followers often reverted to caste practices
      • Structural constraint: state power, economic interests of upper castes, and orthodox clergy resisted fundamental social change
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Medieval mystics planted seeds of social consciousness but lacked institutional mechanisms to translate spiritual egalitarianism into structural social transformation.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 – Medieval India, GS1 – Indian culture, GS1 – Indian Society
  🌐 Contemporary: The debate on religion and social reform continues in contexts of caste discrimination and communal harmony.
