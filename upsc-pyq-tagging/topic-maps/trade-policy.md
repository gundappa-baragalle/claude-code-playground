**[2018]** Q12. How would the recent phenomena of protectionism and currency manipulations in world trade affect macroeconomic stability of India?
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: IMPACT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Global trade tensions and India, Macroeconomic stability, Protectionism and export competitiveness

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Rising global protectionism and deliberate currency manipulation by major economies pose significant threats to India's macroeconomic stability through trade channel disruptions.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Protectionism: Higher tariffs/NTBs in US, EU reduce demand for Indian exports (IT, textiles, pharma) — compresses export earnings, widens current account deficit
      • Currency manipulation: When trading partners artificially depress their currencies, Indian exports become uncompetitive; Indian firms face cheaper imports undermining domestic industry
      • Capital flows: Trade uncertainty reduces FDI and FPI inflows; currency volatility makes rupee vulnerable
      • Macroeconomic consequences: widening CAD, inflationary pressure through import cost rise, RBI forced to intervene depleting forex reserves
      • India's response levers: WTO dispute resolution, bilateral agreements, promoting domestic manufacturing (Make in India), diversifying export destinations
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India must build export resilience through product and market diversification while engaging multilaterally to preserve rule-based trade order.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: International organisations (WTO), GS3: Indian economy and external sector, IR: India-US/China trade relations
  🌐 Contemporary: US tariffs under Trump 2.0 era and China's currency management directly threaten India's macroeconomic stability.

**[2013]** Q7. Examine the impact of liberalization on companies owned by Indians. Are they competing with the MNCs satisfactorily ? Discuss.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Impact of liberalization on Indian companies, Competitive response to MNCs, Sectoral success and failure

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The 1991 liberalization opened India's economy to global competition, fundamentally transforming Indian companies from protected domestic players to aspiring global competitors.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Initial impact: Indian companies faced pricing pressure, quality competition, and market share loss to MNCs in consumer goods, FMCG, auto sectors
      • Success stories: IT sector (TCS, Infosys, Wipro — world leaders), pharma (Sun Pharma, Cipla — generic drug exports), auto (Tata Motors — Jaguar Land Rover acquisition)
      • Areas of challenge: manufacturing still trails MNCs in technology intensity; retail, aviation sectors struggle vs global competition
      • Adaptation strategies: joint ventures, technology tie-ups, acquisitions abroad (Tata's Corus, Mittal Steel), quality upgradation
      • Overall assessment: Indian companies compete satisfactorily in services and selected manufacturing; hardware, semiconductors, defence manufacturing still lag
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Liberalization has broadly strengthened Indian companies through competitive pressure, but closing the gap with MNCs in technology-intensive manufacturing requires sustained policy support.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 — Economy, external sector, GS2 — Governance, industrial policy
  🌐 Contemporary: China-plus-one strategy and PLI scheme offer new opportunities for Indian manufacturers to compete globally.
