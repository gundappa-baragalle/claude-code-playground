**[2013]** Q17. (b) What do you understand by Run-of-river hydroelectricity project ? How is it different from any other hydroelectricity project ?
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 5m | ~100w*
  🏷️ Themes: Run-of-river hydroelectricity concept, Differences from conventional hydropower, Environmental advantages

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~100 words: intro 30w / body 80w / conclusion 25w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** A run-of-river (RoR) hydroelectricity project generates power from the natural flow of a river without creating large storage reservoirs, offering a lower-impact alternative to conventional dam-based hydropower.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • What is RoR: diverts a portion of river flow through a channel/tunnel to a powerhouse; uses kinetic energy of flowing water; minimal storage (only pondage for diurnal variation)
      • Difference from conventional hydropower: no large dam; no significant storage reservoir; power output varies with river flow (seasonal variation); less land submergence and displacement
      • Environmental advantages: lower ecological footprint, less methane from submerged vegetation, minimal displacement, maintains river ecology downstream
      • Limitations: no flood control benefit; power generation dependent on rainfall/snow melt (intermittent); limited peaking capacity
      • Examples in India: Tehri (storage) vs Nathpa Jhakri (partly RoR) vs Kishanganga (RoR, contested with Pakistan)
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Run-of-river projects offer a cleaner hydropower option but require careful hydrological assessment to ensure reliable power output given their dependence on natural river flow.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 — Energy, environment, GS1 — Physical geography, rivers
  🌐 Contemporary: Himachal Pradesh and Uttarakhand's hydropower expansion relies heavily on RoR projects; Kishanganga case (India-Pakistan) remains significant.
