**[Q4685]** [2024]
> One of the following regions has the world's largest tropical peatland, which holds about three years worth of global carbon emissions from fossil fuels; and the possible destruction of which can exert detrimental effect on the global climate. Which one of the following denotes that region ?

     A) Amazon Basin
  ✅ B) Congo Basin
     C) Kikori Basin
     D) Rio de la Plata Basin

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Amazon Basin is the most famous tropical region and students instinctively choose it; the Congo Basin's peatlands were only discovered/measured in 2017.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Congo Basin in Central Africa houses the world's largest tropical peatland (Cuvette Centrale) discovered in 2017 — stores ~30 billion tonnes of carbon (about 3 years of global fossil fuel emissions).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Congo Basin's Cuvette Centrale peatland: ~145,500 km², stores ~30 billion tonnes carbon — world's largest tropical peatland
     - Discovered in 2017 by scientists from University of Leeds — previously unmapped

**[Q4338]** [2022]
> "If rainforests and tropical forests are the lungs of the Earth, then surely wetlands function as its kidneys." Which one of the following functions of wetlands best reflects the above statement?

     A) The water cycle in wetlands involves surface runoff, subsoil percolation and evaporation.
     B) Algae form the nutrient base upon which fish, crustaceans, molluscs, birds, reptiles and mammals thrive.
     C) Wetlands play a vital role in maintaining sedimentation balance and soil stabilization.
  ✅ D) Aquatic plants absorb heavy metals and excess nutrients.

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Options A and C describe water cycle and sedimentation — useful wetland functions but not the 'filtration/purification' that mirrors kidney function.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Kidneys filter toxins and excess substances from blood. Wetlands filter heavy metals and excess nutrients from water — Option D matches this purification analogy perfectly.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Wetlands are called 'kidneys of Earth' for their water purification function — absorbing pollutants
     - Aquatic plants like water hyacinth absorb heavy metals and excess nutrients (phytoremediation)

**[Q4168]** [2021]
> “Leaf litter decomposes faster than in any other biome and as a result the soil surface is often almost bare. Apart from trees, the vegetation is largely composed of plant forms that reach up into the canopy vicariously, by climbing the trees or growing as epiphytes, rooted on the upper branches of trees." This is the most likely description of

     A) coniferous forest
     B) dry deciduous forest
     C) mangrove forest
  ✅ D) tropical rain forest

  ⚠️ **Trap:** The description of rapid leaf litter decomposition, bare soil, epiphytes, and climbing plants perfectly describes tropical rainforest — candidates may confuse with mangrove due to 'trees' mention.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Fastest litter decomposition + epiphytes + canopy climbers + sparse ground cover = tropical rainforest — answer is D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Tropical rainforest: highest biodiversity, fastest nutrient cycling, poor soil (nutrients in biomass)
     - Epiphytes (orchids, bromeliads) are characteristic of tropical rainforests

**[Q3634]** [2018]
> Consider the following statements :  1. Most of the world's coral reefs are in tropical waters. 2. More than one-third of the world's coral reefs are located in the territories of Australia, Indonesia and Philippines. 3. Coral reefs host far more number of animal phyla than those hosted by tropical rainforests.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 and 2 only
     B) 3 only
     C) 1 and 3 only
  ✅ D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2's claim about more than one-third in just three countries is a specific statistic students may doubt; statement 3 about coral reefs hosting more phyla than rainforests is counterintuitive.
  💡 **Eliminate:** All three are correct: most coral reefs in tropical waters (1); Australia, Indonesia, Philippines hold >1/3 of global reefs (2); coral reefs host more animal phyla than tropical rainforests (3). Answer: D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Coral reefs called 'rainforests of the sea' — host more animal phyla than tropical forests
     - Australia's Great Barrier Reef is world's largest coral reef system

**[Q2837]** [2014]
> Which of the following have coral reefs?  1. Andaman and Nicobar Islands 2. Gulf of Kachchh 3. Gulf of Mannar 4. Sunderbans  Select the correct answer using the code given below.

  ✅ A) 1, 2 and 3 only
     B) 2 and 4 only
     C) 1 and 3 only
     D) 1, 2, 3 and 4

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Sundarbans does not have coral reefs — it is a mangrove delta; students sometimes include it thinking all coastal areas have coral reefs.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Sundarbans = mangrove, not coral reef; Andaman & Nicobar (1), Gulf of Kachchh (2), Gulf of Mannar (3) all have coral reefs — answer is A (1, 2 and 3 only).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - India's four coral reef areas: Andaman & Nicobar, Gulf of Kachchh, Gulf of Mannar, Lakshadweep
     - Sundarbans = mangroves, NOT coral reefs

**[Q2614]** [2013]
> Which one of the following is the correct sequence of ecosystems in the order of decreasing productivity?

     A) Oceans, lakes, grasslands, mangroves
     B) Mangroves, oceans, grasslands, lakes
  ✅ C) Mangroves, grasslands, lakes, oceans
     D) Oceans, mangroves, lakes, grasslands

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Oceans are tempting as the most productive due to their size, but per-unit-area productivity is low. Mangroves have highest productivity per unit area, followed by grasslands, then lakes, then open oceans.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Think in terms of net primary productivity per unit area: Mangroves > Grasslands > Lakes > Oceans (open ocean is least productive per unit area).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Mangroves have highest net primary productivity among ecosystems
     - Open ocean has lowest NPP per unit area despite its size
     - Wetlands and estuaries are highly productive ecosystems

**[Q2414]** [2011]
> If a tropical rain forest is removed, it does not regenerate quickly as compared to a tropical deciduous forest. This is because

  ✅ A) the soil of rain forest is deficient in nutrients
     B) propagules of the trees in a rain forest have poor viability
     C) the rain forest species are slow-growing
     D) exotic species invade the fertile soil of rain forest

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may choose slow-growing species (option C) as the reason; the correct answer is nutrient-poor soil (option A) — tropical rainforest soils are leached and almost all nutrients are in the biomass, not soil.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Tropical rainforest: nutrients stored in biomass, NOT in soil; when trees are removed, nutrient cycling breaks, leaving barren, leached soil with no base for regeneration.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Tropical rainforests have nutrient-poor, laterite soil — nutrients locked in living biomass
     - Slash-and-burn farming exhausts nutrients within 2-3 years due to leaching

**[Q524]** [1998]
> Estuaries possess distinct blooms of excessive growth of a pigmented dinoflagellates. These blooms are called

  ✅ A) red tides
     B) sea tides
     C) black tides
     D) sea flowers

  ⚠️ **Trap:** The colour red suggests 'red tides' but students might think of oil spills as 'black tides' or conflate the term. The word 'bloom' might suggest 'sea flowers'.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Red tides are caused by dinoflagellate blooms that produce red/brownish pigmentation; the term is directly associated with estuaries and harmful algal blooms.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Red tides are caused by dinoflagellate blooms
     - They can be toxic to marine life and humans
     - Occur in estuaries and coastal areas

**[Q237]** [1996]
> Assertion (A) : Mangroves are very specialised forest ecosystems of tropical and subtropical regions bordering certain sea coasts. Reason (R) : They stabilise the shoreline and act as bulwark against encroachments by sea. In the context of the above two statements, which one of the following is correct ?

     A) Both A and R are true and R is the correct explanation of A
  ✅ B) Both A and R are true but R is not a correct explanation of A
     C) A is true but R is false
     D) A is false but R is true

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Both A and R are individually true, but R (shoreline stabilization) is the result of mangrove characteristics, not the reason why they are specialized ecosystems of tropical/subtropical coasts — the two are related but R doesn't explain why mangroves exist where they do.
  💡 **Eliminate:** A is true: mangroves are indeed specialized ecosystems of tropical/subtropical coastal regions. R is also true: they do stabilize shorelines. But R does not explain why they are specialized coastal ecosystems — the reason is their adaptation to saline, tidal, anaerobic conditions. Answer: B.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Mangroves grow in tropical/subtropical intertidal zones with saline-tolerant adaptations (pneumatophores, prop roots)
     - They stabilize shorelines but this is an ecological service, not the reason for their coastal specialization

**[Q1085]** [2002]
> Consider the following ecosystems: 1. Taiga 2. Tropical evergreen 3. Tropical deciduous 4. Tundra The correct sequence in decreasing order of the albedo values of these ecosystems is

     A) 1, 4, 3, 2
     B) 4, 1, 2, 3
  ✅ C) 4, 1, 3, 2
     D) 1, 4, 2, 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Albedo is the reflectivity of a surface; candidates may confuse the order assuming snow-covered tundra has highest albedo — but tundra snow cover makes it highest, with taiga second due to dark conifer trees absorbing light.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Albedo order (decreasing): Tundra (snow-covered, high reflectivity) > Taiga (some snow + dark conifers) > Tropical deciduous (seasonal leaf cover) > Tropical evergreen (dense dark canopy, lowest albedo). Answer: C (4,1,3,2).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Tundra has highest albedo due to snow and ice cover
     - Tropical evergreen forests have lowest albedo due to dense dark canopy absorbing sunlight

**[Q2537]** [2012]
> With reference to the wetlands of India, consider the following statements:  1. The country's total geographical area under the category of wetlands is recorded more in. Gujarat as compared to other States. 2. In India, the total geographical area of coastal wetlands is larger than that of inland wetlands.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

  ✅ A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
     C) Both 1 and 2
     D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students expect Gujarat to lead in coastal wetlands (long coastline) and thus may wrongly affirm statement 2 about coastal > inland wetlands nationally. Both common assumptions are wrong.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Gujarat does have the highest wetland area (Rann of Kutch is a major wetland). However, inland wetlands exceed coastal wetlands in total area nationally — so only statement 1 is correct.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Gujarat has largest wetland area in India (including Rann of Kutch)
     - India's inland wetlands exceed coastal wetlands in total area

### GS1

**[2019]** Q5. Discuss the causes of depletion of mangroves and explain their importance in maintaining coastal ecology.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Coastal ecology, Environmental conservation, Climate resilience

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Mangroves, the unique intertidal forest ecosystems, are under severe anthropogenic pressure even as they serve as irreplaceable buffers for coastal communities and biodiversity.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Causes of depletion: aquaculture expansion (shrimp farming), urban encroachment, pollution, sea-level rise, and tourism development
      • Importance: coastal protection against storms and erosion, nursery habitat for marine species, carbon sequestration (blue carbon)
      • Role in biodiversity: habitat for migratory birds, fish breeding grounds, and endemic species
      • Socio-economic relevance: livelihoods of coastal communities depend on mangrove ecosystem services
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Protecting mangroves requires integrated coastal zone management, community participation, and legally binding conservation frameworks.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 Environment, GS2 Disaster Management, International Conservation Law
  🌐 Contemporary: India's mangrove cover has marginally increased per recent FSI reports, but threats from climate change and development persist.

**[2019]** Q15. How can the mountain ecosystem be restored from the negative impact of development initiatives and tourism?
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Mountain ecosystem restoration, Sustainable development vs conservation

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Mountain ecosystems, characterised by exceptional biodiversity and fragile ecology, face accelerating degradation from poorly planned development and unregulated tourism, necessitating urgent restoration measures.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Impacts of development: road construction (landslides, habitat fragmentation), hydropower projects (river ecology disruption), mining
      • Tourism impacts: waste accumulation (Himalayan pilgrimage routes), trail erosion, campfire deforestation, wildlife disturbance
      • Restoration strategies: afforestation with native species, banning single-use plastics in mountain areas, carrying capacity norms for tourist sites
      • Eco-tourism model: Sikkim's organic/eco-tourism approach as model; community-based tourism for local livelihood
      • Policy tools: Eco-Sensitive Zone notifications, Himalayan Glaciology Mission, Snow Leopard conservation
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Restoring mountain ecosystems requires integrating ecological carrying capacity into development planning, empowering local communities, and enforcing sustainable tourism practices.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 Geography — Himalayan ecology, GS3 Environment — biodiversity conservation, disaster management
  🌐 Contemporary: Joshimath land subsidence (2023) highlighted risks of unplanned development in mountain areas.
