**[Q4727]** [2024]
> Consider the following statements:  1. There are no parables in Upanishads. 2. Upanishads were composed earlier than the Puranas.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 only
  ✅ B) 2 only
     C) Both 1 and 2
     D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 1 is wrong: Upanishads DO contain parables — famous ones include Nachiketa (Katha Upanishad), Satyakama (Chandogya Upanishad), Shvetaketu (Chandogya Upanishad). Only Statement 2 is correct.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Statement 1 wrong (Upanishads contain many famous parables and dialogues); Statement 2 correct (Upanishads are part of Vedic literature composed before the Puranas). Answer: B.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Upanishads contain famous parables: Nachiketa-Yama dialogue (Katha Upanishad); Sveatasvetara; Shvetaketu-Uddalaka dialogue (Chandogya)
     - Upanishads (composed ~800-200 BCE) predate Puranas (1st-10th century CE)

**[Q2843]** [2014]
> Which one of the following pairs does not form part of the six systems of Indian Philosophy?

     B) Nyaya and Vaisheshika

**[2024]** Q1. Underline the changes in the field of society and economy from the Rig Vedic to the later Vedic period.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Vedic social change, Ancient Indian economy, Caste origins

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The transition from the Rig Vedic to the Later Vedic period represents one of ancient India's most consequential social and economic transformations, as a mobile pastoral society settled into a stratified agrarian order.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Society: Rig Vedic — tribe (jana) based, varna flexible; Later Vedic — fixed varna hierarchy, brahmin ascendancy
      • Ritual elaboration: complex yajnas (Ashvamedha, Rajasuya) in Later Vedic period — brahmin power increased
      • Economy: Rig Vedic — cattle-centric pastoral economy; Later Vedic — agriculture dominant, iron ploughs
      • Iron technology: Painted Grey Ware associated with iron-using Later Vedic people in Gangetic plains
      • Trade: Later Vedic texts mention shrenis (guilds), long-distance trade — commercial economy emerging
      • Urbanisation: Later Vedic texts mention larger settlements; archaeology shows Hastinapur, Kausambi
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The Rig Vedic to Later Vedic transition marks India's shift from an egalitarian pastoral society to a hierarchical agrarian civilisation, laying the structural foundations for the caste system and state formation.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Ancient India, Sociology: caste origins, Economic history
  🌐 Contemporary: Historical understanding of varna origins informs contemporary debates about the antiquity and malleability of caste.

**[2022]** Q13. Discuss the significance of the lion and bull figures in Indian mythology, art and architecture.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHAT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Cultural continuity, Iconography in Indian art, Mythology and statecraft

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The lion and bull are among the most enduring symbols in Indian civilization, weaving through mythology, religious iconography, and political imagery across millennia.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Lion in mythology: Narasimha avatar of Vishnu; vahana of Durga (Simhavahini)
      • Lion in statecraft: Lion Capital of Ashoka at Sarnath — adopted as India's national emblem
      • Lion in architecture: guardian figures at temple entrances (dwarpala tradition)
      • Bull in mythology: Nandi as Shiva's vahana and gatekeeper; symbol of dharma and fertility
      • Bull in Indus Valley: prominent on seals possibly representing a deity or totem
      • Bull in art: depicted in Sanchi stupa railings, Chola bronzes, and Pallava rock-cut temples
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The lion and bull in Indian tradition transcend mere artistic motifs — they encode deep mythological, spiritual, and political meanings that persist in India's national identity.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 Art and Culture, Indian History, National symbols
  🌐 Contemporary: India's national emblem (Lion Capital) is derived from Ashokan art, making this directly relevant to constitutional and cultural identity.

**[2014]** Q3. Taxila university was one of the oldest universities of the world with which were associated a number of renowned learned personalities of different disciplines. Its strategic location caused its fame to flourish, but unlike Nalanda, it is not considered as a university in the modern sense. Discuss.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Ancient Indian education, Intellectual heritage of India

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Taxila, located at the crossroads of Central Asian trade routes, was one of antiquity's greatest centres of learning, yet lacked the formal institutional structure that defines a modern university.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Taxila's strategic location on the Uttarapatha trade route attracted scholars from across Asia
      • Kautilya, Panini, Charaka, Jivaka associated with Taxila — interdisciplinary in nature
      • No fixed curriculum, no residential arrangement, no formal degrees — open system of learning
      • Nalanda had dormitories, structured courses, admission tests, and specialised faculties
      • Taxila functioned more as an intellectual marketplace than an organised institution
      • Destruction by Huns (5th century CE) ended its role as a learning centre
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While Taxila's intellectual contributions rival any ancient institution, its decentralised and informal structure distinguishes it fundamentally from the modern concept of a university as exemplified by Nalanda.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Ancient India culture, Art & Architecture, Buddhist heritage
  🌐 Contemporary: Nalanda University has been revived as an international institution, reflecting India's pride in its ancient educational legacy.
