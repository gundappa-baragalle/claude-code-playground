**[Q4745]** [2024]
> Under which of the following Articles of the Constitution of India, has the Supreme Court of India placed the Right to Privacy ?

     A) Article 15
     B) Article 16
     C) Article 19
  ✅ D) Article 21

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may guess Article 19 (freedom of speech/expression) as privacy relates to personal liberty and free speech, but the Supreme Court placed privacy under Article 21.
  💡 **Eliminate:** K.S. Puttaswamy judgment (2017) unanimously declared Right to Privacy as a fundamental right under Article 21 (Right to Life and Personal Liberty).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017): 9-judge bench held Right to Privacy is intrinsic to Article 21
     - Privacy is protected as part of 'personal liberty' under Article 21 — not a separate article

**[Q4752]** [2024]
> A Writ of Prohibition is an order issued by the Supreme Court or High Courts to:

     A) a government officer prohibiting him from taking a particular action.
     B) the Parliament/Legislative Assembly to pass a law on Prohibition.
  ✅ C) the lower court prohibiting continuation of proceedings in a case.
     D) the Government prohibiting it from following an unconstitutional policy.

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may confuse Prohibition with Certiorari (which quashes orders of lower courts) or with Mandamus. Prohibition specifically stops ongoing proceedings.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Writ of Prohibition is issued to an inferior court/tribunal to stop proceedings that are beyond its jurisdiction — it prevents, rather than quashes. Answer is C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Prohibition writ prevents inferior courts from exceeding jurisdiction — issued BEFORE judgment
     - Certiorari quashes the order AFTER it is passed; Prohibition stops proceedings midway

**[Q4753]** [2024]
> Consider the following statements:  1. It is the Governor of the State who recognizes and declares any community of that State as a Scheduled Tribe. 2. A community declared as a Scheduled Tribe in a State need not be so in another State.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 only
  ✅ B) 2 only
     C) Both 1 and 2
     D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 1 says Governor declares Scheduled Tribes — this is wrong; it is the President who specifies STs by public notification under Article 342, on advice of concerned State Governor.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Only Statement 2 is correct: a community declared ST in one state need not be ST in another (state-specific lists). Statement 1 is wrong — President specifies STs under Article 342, not the Governor.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Article 342 empowers the President (not Governor) to specify Scheduled Tribes by public notification
     - ST lists are state-specific — a tribe may be ST in one state but not another (e.g., Gond is ST in some states)

**[Q4516]** [2023]
> In India, which one of the following Constitutional Amendments was widely believed to be enacted to overcome the judicial interpretations of the Fundamental Rights?

     A) 1st Amendment
     B) 42nd Amendment
     C) 44th Amendment
     D) 86th Amendment

  ⚠️ **Trap:** The 42nd Amendment (1976) made sweeping changes during Emergency, but the 1st Amendment (1951) was the first specific attempt to overcome judicial interpretation of Fundamental Rights (especially property rights and free speech).
  💡 **Eliminate:** The 1st Constitutional Amendment (1951) was specifically enacted to overcome SC rulings on free speech (Romesh Thappar case) and property rights — it added the Ninth Schedule to immunize laws from FR challenge; answer is 1st Amendment.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - 1st Constitutional Amendment (1951) added Ninth Schedule and introduced 'reasonable restrictions' to free speech — response to Romesh Thappar and Brij Bhushan cases
     - It also inserted Articles 31A and 31B to protect land reform laws from property right challenges

**[Q4193]** [2021]
> A legislation which confers on the executive or administrative authority an unguided and uncontrolled discretionary power in the matter of application of law violates which one of the following Articles of the Constitution of India?

  ✅ A) Article 14
     B) Article 28
     C) Article 32
     D) Article 44

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may pick Article 32 (right to constitutional remedies) thinking it's about enforcement. However, the question is about a law giving unguided discretion, which violates Article 14 (right to equality/equal protection of law).

     - The 'equality clause' has been interpreted to include the right against arbitrariness (E.P. Royappa case)

**[2013]** Q2. Discuss Section 66A of IT Act, with reference to its alleged violation of Article 19 of the Constitution.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Free speech in digital age, Judicial review of legislation, Constitutional validity of internet laws

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Section 66A of the IT Act represented a legislative attempt to regulate online speech that was ultimately found to impose an unconstitutional chill on free expression, highlighting the tension between cybersecurity and fundamental rights.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Section 66A criminalized 'grossly offensive' online messages — vague, subjective language
      • Used to arrest activists, journalists and citizens for social media posts criticizing government
      • Violated Article 19(1)(a) — freedom of speech, expression and press
      • Article 19(2) allows restrictions only for specified grounds: sovereignty, security, public order etc.
      • SC in Shreya Singhal (2015): Section 66A was not saved by Art 19(2) — too broad and chilling effect
      • SC distinguished between advocacy and incitement — only incitement to imminent lawless action can be restricted
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The Shreya Singhal judgment established a vital precedent that digital speech enjoys the same constitutional protection as offline speech, requiring any restriction to satisfy the test of proportionality and nexus to Article 19(2) grounds.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Governance — IT Act reforms, S&T — cyber law and digital governance, Internal Security — hate speech and national security
  🌐 Contemporary: Debates on IT Rules 2021 and proposed Digital India Act keep Section 66A's legacy alive as a benchmark for free speech protection online.
