**[Q2542]** [2012]
> In India, other than ensuring that public funds are used efficiently and for intended purpose, what is the importance of the office of the Comptroller and Auditor General (CAG)?  1. CAG exercises exchequer control on behalf of the Parliament when the President of India declares national emergency/financial emergency. 2. CAG reports on the execution of projects or programmes by the ministries are discussed by the Public Accounts Committee. 3. Information from CAG reports can be used by investigating agencies to press charges. against those who have violated the law while managing public finances. 4. While dealing with the audit and accounting of government companies, CAG has certain judicial.powers for prosecuting those who violate the law.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1, 3 and 4 only
     B) 2 only
  ✅ C) 2 and 3 only
     D) 1, 2, 3 and 4

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statements 1 and 4 contain inaccuracies: CAG has NO exchequer control role during emergency, and CAG has NO judicial powers for prosecution — these make Statements 1 and 4 wrong.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Statements 2 (CAG reports discussed in PAC) and 3 (CAG reports usable by investigating agencies) are correct. Statements 1 and 4 wrongly attribute judicial/exchequer powers to CAG. Answer: C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - CAG reports are examined by Public Accounts Committee (PAC) of Parliament
     - CAG has no judicial powers — cannot prosecute; that role belongs to CBI/ED

**[Q909]** [2001]
> Which one of the following duties is NOT performed by the Comptroller and Auditor General of India ?

     A) To audit and report on all expenditure from the Consolidated Fund of India
     B) To audit and report on all expenditure from the Contingency Funds and Public Accounts
     C) To audit and report on all trading, manufacturing, profit and loss accounts
  ✅ D) To control the receipt and issue of public money, and to ensure that the public revenue is lodged in the exchequer

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students often think CAG 'controls' public money (like a treasurer), but it only audits — it does not control receipt/issue of public money. That is the function of the Controller General of Accounts, not CAG.
  💡 **Eliminate:** CAG (Article 148) audits expenditure but does NOT control public money receipts/issue — that role belongs to the Ministry of Finance/CGA. The question asks for what CAG does NOT do. Answer: D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - CAG audits Consolidated Fund, Contingency Funds, and Public Accounts
     - CAG does NOT control the flow of public money — that is the executive's role
     - CAG reports go to PAC (Public Accounts Committee)

### GS2

**[2024]** Q4. "The duty of the Comptroller and Auditor General is not merely to ensure the legality of expenditure but also its propriety." Comment.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Financial accountability, CAG's expanded mandate, Good governance

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The CAG's mandate transcends the narrow verification of rules — it extends to the higher standard of propriety, asking not merely whether money was spent legally, but wisely and in public interest.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Legality vs propriety: legality = compliance with rules and procedures; propriety = whether expenditure was prudent, necessary, and in public interest
      • Constitutional basis: Article 149 empowers Parliament to define CAG's duties; Comptroller and Auditor General's (Duties, Powers, and Conditions of Service) Act 1971
      • Propriety audit in practice: CAG flagged 2G spectrum allocation as loss of notional revenue (propriety, not just legality); CWG expenditure scrutinized for waste
      • Checks on propriety audit: CAG cannot substitute its judgment for executive's in policy matters — only scrutinizes implementation propriety
      • Significance: propriety audit catches 'smart corruption' — technically legal but purposefully wasteful schemes
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** By extending audit to propriety, the CAG ensures that public money serves its intended purpose — transforming audit from a procedural exercise into a pillar of democratic accountability.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 Polity, GS3 Economy, Transparency and Accountability
  🌐 Contemporary: CAG's performance audits of PM-KISAN, MGNREGS and PPE procurement during COVID demonstrate propriety audit in action.

**[2018]** Q5. "The Comptroller and Auditor General (CAG) has a very vital role to play." Explain how this is reflected in the method and terms of his appointment as well as the range of powers he can exercise.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Accountability, CAG role, Financial oversight

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The CAG of India stands as the guardian of the public purse — its appointment safeguards and sweeping audit powers reflect its indispensability in upholding financial accountability in a democracy.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Appointment: by President of India (Article 148); security of tenure — cannot be removed except by impeachment process like SC judge; salary charged to Consolidated Fund — making it independent of vote of Parliament
      • Range of powers: audits accounts of Union and States; audits PSUs (Section 619 Companies Act); receipts and expenditure audit; performance/efficiency audit; propriety audit (checks waste, not just legality)
      • Significance: reports expose scams (2G, CWG — CAG reports triggered major accountability debates); strengthens Parliamentary control through PAC
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The CAG's constitutional insulation and comprehensive audit mandate make it the most powerful watchdog of government finances, provided Parliament acts on its reports.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 Polity, GS3 Economy, Transparency and Accountability
  🌐 Contemporary: CAG reports on MGNREGS, PM-KISAN and COVID funds expenditure have been central to accountability debates.

**[2016]** Q4. Exercise of CAG's powers in relation to the accounts of the Union and the States is derived from Article 149 of the Indian Constitution. Discuss whether audit of the Government's policy implementation could amount to overstepping its own (CAG) jurisdiction.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: CAG's audit mandate, Policy audit vs financial audit, Executive accountability

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The question of whether CAG's policy implementation audit constitutes jurisdictional overreach rests on the contested boundary between performance audit of policy execution and policy formulation itself — a boundary the CAG's landmark reports have repeatedly tested.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • CAG's mandate: audit expenditure of Union and States, autonomous bodies receiving grants
      • Types of audit: regularity, performance, propriety — performance audit covers 'value for money'
      • Policy audit controversy: CAG's 2G audit assessed policy of first-come-first-served spectrum allocation
      • Defence: CAG audits implementation, not policy wisdom — assessing whether spending achieved policy goals is legitimate
      • Criticism: CAG exceeded mandate by questioning policy choice (zero-price spectrum) not just implementation
      • Constitutional basis: Article 149 + Comptroller's duties extend to propriety audit
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** CAG's performance audits of policy implementation fall within its constitutional mandate as long as it evaluates effectiveness of implementation rather than second-guessing policy choices — a distinction requiring institutional clarity and restraint.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Polity — Parliamentary oversight, Governance — accountability of public expenditure, Polity — PAC and CAG reports
  🌐 Contemporary: CAG reports on COVID vaccine procurement, PPE kits and PMAY implementation remain subjects of parliamentary and public debate.
