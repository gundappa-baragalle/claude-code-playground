  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's secularism is a civilizational synthesis rooted in tolerance and pluralism, distinct from the Western model of strict church-state separation.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Tolerance: Long history of accommodation — Ashoka's Dhamma, Akbar's Din-i-Ilahi, Sufi-Bhakti movements show tolerance as core Indian value
      • Assimilation: India absorbed multiple waves of migrants/religions (Parsis, Jews, early Christians, Islamic traditions) creating syncretic culture
      • Pluralism: Recognition of multiple truths; 'Sarva Dharma Sama Bhava' principle; Articles 25-28 protect all religious practices
      • Constitutional secularism: State neither favors nor discriminates any religion; but can intervene for social reform (e.g., abolishing untouchability, regulating temples)

  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's secular model, built on tolerance and pluralism rather than religious exclusion, remains both its strength and a subject of ongoing democratic debate.

**[2017]** Q18. The women's questions arose in modern India as a part of the 19th century social reform movement. What were the major issues and debates concerning women in that period?
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHAT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Social reform and women's emancipation, 19th century India, Colonial modernity

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The 19th century social reform movement in India was fundamentally intertwined with the 'women's question' — a debate about the place of women in a modernizing yet tradition-bound society.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Major issues: Sati (abolished 1829), child marriage, purdah system, widow remarriage prohibition, female infanticide, lack of female education
      • Sati abolition (1829): Raja Ram Mohan Roy's campaign → Regulation XVII by William Bentinck
      • Widow Remarriage: Ishwar Chandra Vidyasagar's advocacy → Hindu Widow Remarriage Act 1856
      • Female education: Jyotiba Phule and Savitribai Phule opened first school for girls (1848); Pandita Ramabai championed women's education

      • Women's agency: Pandita Ramabai, Tarabai Shinde wrote against patriarchal norms — early feminist voices
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The 19th century women's question was not merely about legal reform but reflected a deeper struggle between colonial modernity and indigenous patriarchy, laying the foundation for modern Indian feminism.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: Women empowerment schemes, GS1: Art and Culture — changing status of women
  🌐 Contemporary: Continuity of issues like gender equality and women's safety makes this historically grounded topic perennially relevant.
