## Prelims Questions

### 🟢 LOW Priority — Full Options

**[Q376]** [1997]
> Assertion (A) : The emergence of economic globalism does not imply the decline of socialist ideology. Reason (R) : The ideology of Socialism believes in universalism and globalism. In the context of the above two statements, which one of the following is correct ?

  ✅ A) Both A and R are true and R is the correct explanation of A
     B) Both A and R are true, but R is not a correct explanation of A
     C) A is true, but R is false
     D) A is false, but R is true

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may pick B (both true, R not explaining A) without carefully analyzing whether socialism's universalism actually supports the assertion about globalism not declining socialism.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Socialism is inherently internationalist/universalist; economic globalism doesn't automatically destroy socialist thought since socialism also advocates global solidarity — both A and R are true and R explains A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Socialist ideology is universalist — advocates international worker solidarity
     - Economic globalism and socialist globalism are distinct but not always contradictory

### GS1

**[2025]** Q10. Do you think that globalization results in only an aggressive consumer culture? Justify your answer.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Globalization and culture, Consumer society, Homogenization vs heterogenization

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** While globalization has undoubtedly intensified consumerism, reducing it solely to an aggressive consumer culture ignores its complex and contradictory cultural, economic and social impacts.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Globalization promotes consumerism through MNCs, advertising and aspirational lifestyles
      • Cultural homogenization: spread of western brands, fast food, pop culture
      • Counter-argument: cultural revival and glocalization (yoga global, local festivals preserved)
      • Economic benefits: jobs, technology transfer, poverty reduction through integration
      • Digital economy enables local artisans to reach global markets (vocal for local)
      • Environmental cost of consumerism: e-waste, carbon footprint
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Globalization is a multi-dimensional process that simultaneously promotes consumerism and cultural vitality; the challenge is to harness its economic benefits while resisting cultural homogenization and ecological costs.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Economy — FDI and trade, Society — cultural change and identity, Environment — consumption-driven ecological footprint
  🌐 Contemporary: India's 'Aatmanirbhar Bharat' and 'Vocal for Local' campaigns represent efforts to channel globalization toward domestic empowerment.

**[2022]** Q20. Elucidate the relationship between globalization and new technology in a world of scarce resources, with special reference to India.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Technology and globalisation, India's digital economy, Resource efficiency

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The convergence of globalisation and new technology presents India with a unique opportunity to leapfrog resource-intensive development pathways, though it also introduces new forms of resource competition and digital inequality.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Technology enables resource efficiency: AI-optimised agriculture reduces water and fertiliser use
      • Globalisation of rare earth supply chains creates new resource dependencies for tech hardware
      • India's digital stack (Aadhaar, UPI, ONDC) as globally applicable public goods
      • Gig economy: globalisation of labour markets via technology — creates flexibility but reduces security
      • Digital divide: globalisation's technology benefits unevenly distributed — urban vs rural India
      • Critical minerals: technology's demand for lithium, cobalt creates new resource scarcity dynamics
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India must strategically leverage its digital infrastructure strengths while addressing resource dependencies and digital inequalities that new technology and globalisation simultaneously create.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3: Technology, GS3: Environment, GS2: Social sector
  🌐 Contemporary: India's G20 presidency focused on digital public infrastructure as a model for global technology-enabled development.

**[2021]** Q19. What is Cryptocurrency? How does it affect global society? Has it been affecting Indian society also?
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHAT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Cryptocurrency, Financial globalisation, Digital economy

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Cryptocurrency represents the most radical experiment in decentralised digital finance, challenging state monetary sovereignty while creating new opportunities and risks for both global and Indian society.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Global society: crypto enables cross-border transactions bypassing traditional banking — financial inclusion for unbanked
      • Speculation and volatility: Bitcoin's wild price swings create financial instability and investor harm
      • Dark web and illicit finance: crypto's anonymity enables money laundering, ransomware, drug trade
      • India: young urban professionals invested heavily; 30% tax + 1% TDS regime drove crypto volume offshore
      • Financial sovereignty: RBI concern that crypto threatens monetary policy transmission
      • CBDC (Digital Rupee): India's state response — blockchain benefits with RBI control
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Cryptocurrency's transformative potential in financial inclusion must be balanced against its systemic risks, necessitating a calibrated regulatory framework rather than outright prohibition or unregulated adoption.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3: Money and banking, GS2: Governance — regulation, IR: Global financial governance
  🌐 Contemporary: FTX collapse (2022) and global crypto regulation discussions have made India's crypto tax and CBDC policy increasingly relevant.

**[2020]** Q18. Is diversity and pluralism in India under threat due to globalisation? Justify your answer.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: IMPACT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Globalization and cultural identity, Pluralism under threat, India's multicultural ethos

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's civilizational diversity and pluralism face both challenges and opportunities from the forces of globalization in the contemporary era.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Threats: cultural homogenization, dominance of Western media, commodification of culture, erosion of local languages and crafts
      • Counterargument: globalization also promotes cultural exchange, digital revival of folk arts, and platform for indigenous voices globally
      • Constitutional protection: Articles 29, 30 safeguard minority cultures; 8th Schedule protects 22 languages
      • Empirical evidence: regional cultures thriving (K-pop analogy with regional music), but tribal languages and oral traditions under pressure
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While globalization poses real threats to India's diversity, a calibrated engagement that harnesses technology for cultural preservation can protect pluralism.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Indian Society, GS2: Minority rights, IR: Cultural diplomacy
  🌐 Contemporary: UNESCO's report on cultural diversity and global media dominance makes this topic consistently relevant.

**[2019]** Q20. Are we losing our local identity for the global identity? Discuss.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Local vs global identity tension, Cultural imperialism, Preservation of indigenous identity

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Globalization has accelerated the spread of a universal consumer culture that increasingly challenges and dilutes local identities, raising the question of whether we are losing our distinctiveness to a homogenized global self.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Evidence of loss: global brands (McDonald's, Netflix) replacing local food, entertainment; English displacing regional languages in urban spaces; Western fashion supplanting traditional dress
      • Identity erosion mechanisms: media imperialism (Hollywood, K-pop), social media algorithms promoting global trends, migration and diaspora blending cultures
      • Counter-arguments (identity retained): rise of regional content (OTT platforms), revival of craft industries, yoga and Ayurveda global popularity (Indian identity going global)
      • Glocalization: global formats adapted to local taste — Bollywood-Hollywood hybrid, regional language OTT content
      • Youth and identity: younger Indians navigate dual identity — global consumers AND local cultural participants
      • Policy response: UNESCO Convention on Cultural Diversity (2005) — recognizes right to maintain local cultural expression
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Rather than a binary loss, a nuanced negotiation between local and global identity is underway — the challenge is to ensure globalization enriches rather than erases cultural heritage.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 — Indian society, globalization, GS4 — Ethics, cultural values
  🌐 Contemporary: Social media's role in both spreading and celebrating local cultures complicates the homogenization narrative.

**[2018]** Q19. 'Globalization is generally said to promote cultural homogenization but due to this cultural specificities appear to be strengthened in the Indian Society.' Elucidate.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: VS | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Globalization and culture, Identity politics, Indian society

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The paradox of globalisation in India is that far from erasing cultural specificities, it has often intensified local cultural identities as communities assert their distinctiveness in response to homogenising pressures.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Homogenisation thesis: Western media, fast food, fashion creating global consumer culture
      • Indian reality: global exposure has triggered revival of classical arts, yoga, organic farming, regional languages
      • Urban middle class: simultaneously consuming global products and funding local temple festivals
      • Glocalization: McDonald's Maharaja Mac, Spotify's Carnatic music playlists — global form, local content
      • Caste assertion: globalisation created prosperity that funded caste-based political and cultural mobilisation (Jat, Patel agitations)
      • Digital technology: WhatsApp groups for Puja committees, regional language social media boom
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's experience refutes simple cultural homogenisation — globalisation has been a catalyst for both cultural exchange and cultural assertiveness, creating a more complex hybrid identity landscape.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Society — Globalization, GS1: Regionalism, IR: Global culture
  🌐 Contemporary: OTT platforms' regional language content boom exemplifies cultural specificity strengthening under global digital infrastructure.

**[2016]** Q8. To what extent globalization has influenced the core of cultural diversity in India? Explain.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: IMPACT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Globalization and culture, Identity politics, Cultural diversity

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** While globalization has penetrated India's cultural landscape, the core of India's civilizational diversity has demonstrated remarkable resilience and adaptability.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Impact on core: Western consumer culture (fashion, food, entertainment) has influenced urban India significantly
      • Resilience: languages (22 scheduled + regional), religious practices, festivals, classical arts remain vibrant
      • Hybridization: Bollywood integrates Western music; Indian cuisine globalized; yoga becomes global — two-way cultural exchange
      • Threat areas: tribal cultures, local dialects, traditional crafts facing erosion under market pressures
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's cultural diversity is neither unchanged by globalization nor overwhelmed by it — rather it is transforming through a process of selective synthesis and adaptive resilience.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 Society, GS2 Governance — cultural policy, IR — India's soft power

**[2013]** Q17. Critically examine the effect of globalization on the aged population in India.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Globalization and social fabric, Elderly welfare, Demographic dividend and liability

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Globalization, while accelerating economic growth, has significantly altered family structures and social support systems, particularly affecting India's ageing population.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Migration of young working adults to cities/abroad has weakened joint family support for the elderly
      • Rising cost of healthcare and living strains elderly with fixed incomes amid market-driven globalization
      • Positive impact: exposure to global healthcare innovations, pension products, technology-assisted elderly care
      • Cultural shifts have led to breakdown of traditional care norms; old-age homes and geriatric care sector emerging
      • Policy gaps: India lacks comprehensive social security for the elderly; National Policy for Senior Citizens needs strengthening
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Globalization's impact on the elderly demands a policy response that rebuilds social safety nets while leveraging the opportunities of a connected world.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 – Indian Society, GS2 – Social Justice, GS3 – Economy (demographic transition)
  🌐 Contemporary: India's ageing demographic and the erosion of joint families make elderly welfare a pressing policy challenge.
