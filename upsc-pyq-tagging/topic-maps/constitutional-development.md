**[Q4731]** [2024]
> With reference to the Government of India Act, 1935, consider the following statements:  1. It provided for the establishment of an All India Federation based on the union of the British Indian Provinces and Princely States. 2. Defence and Foreign Affairs were kept under the control of the federal legislature.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

  ✅ A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
     C) Both 1 and 2
     D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 is the trap — Defence and Foreign Affairs were kept under the Viceroy/Governor-General (reserved subjects), NOT the federal legislature. Students confuse reserved vs federal subjects.
  💡 **Eliminate:** GoI Act 1935 did provide for an All India Federation (Statement 1 correct). But Defence and External Affairs were reserved subjects of the Governor-General, not the federal legislature (Statement 2 wrong).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - GoI Act 1935 envisaged All India Federation of British provinces and Princely States — never fully implemented
     - Defence and Foreign Affairs remained with Viceroy under reserved subjects — not transferred to legislature

     A) The Regulating Act
     B) The Pitt's India Act
     C) The Charter Act of 1793
  ✅ D) The Charter Act of 1833

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Pitt's India Act created the Board of Control but the Governor General was still Governor General of Bengal; Charter Act of 1793 renewed Company charter but didn't change the title.

  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Charter Act of 1833: Governor General of Bengal became Governor General of India; William Bentinck was the first
     - Same Act centralized legislative power at the Governor General level, stripping Presidencies of legislative power

### GS1

**[2015]** Q5. It would have been difficult for the Constituent Assembly to complete its historic task of drafting the Constitution for Independent India in just three years but for the experience gained with the Government of India Act, 1935. Discuss.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: HOW | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Constitutional history, Colonial legacy, Institutional continuity

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The Government of India Act 1935, despite being a colonial instrument, served as the administrative and constitutional scaffolding upon which the framers of independent India's Constitution built their democratic edifice.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Federal structure with Centre-State division of powers borrowed from 1935 Act
      • Emergency provisions in Articles 352-360 trace their origins to 1935 Act's provisions
      • Bicameral legislature, Public Service Commissions, Auditor General — all from 1935 Act
      • Constituent Assembly members had experience legislating under 1935 Act framework
      • Drafting Committee used 1935 Act's language and structure as starting draft
      • Ambedkar acknowledged: 'a large part of the provisions of this Constitution have been taken from the Government of India Act, 1935'
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Far from being a liability, the familiarity with the 1935 Act's framework proved indispensable in enabling the Constituent Assembly to draft one of the world's longest constitutions in record time.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: Polity — Constitutional Framework, Modern Indian history, Colonial administration
  🌐 Contemporary: Understanding this continuity helps explain certain 'colonial' features critics identify in Indian governance structures.

### GS2

**[2016]** Q7. Did the Government of India Act, 1935 lay down a federal constitution ? Discuss.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHAT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Constitutional evolution, Federalism in colonial India, GOI Act 1935

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The Government of India Act 1935 attempted a federal structure for India but fell short of genuine federalism due to authoritarian safeguards and the dominance of appointed executives.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Federal features: three legislative lists, bicameral legislature, provincial autonomy, Federal Court
      • Non-federal features: residuary powers with Governor-General; safeguards for minorities/civil services weakened provincial sovereignty
      • Governor's special powers: could override provincial legislatures — undermining the federal bargain
      • Assessment: quasi-federation at best; never fully operative as princes did not join the All-India Federation
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The 1935 Act created a federal scaffold but packed it with unitary features — a 'federation with a unitary bias' whose limitations informed the framers of the 1950 Constitution.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 Polity — constitutional background, GS1 History — modern, GS2 — Federalism
