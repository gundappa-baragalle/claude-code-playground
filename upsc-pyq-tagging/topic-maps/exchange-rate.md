**[Q4720]** [2024]
> Consider the following statements:  1. Statement-I: If the United States of America (USA) were to default on its debt, holders of US Treasury Bonds will not be able to exercise their claims to receive payment. 2. Statement-II: The USA Government debt is not backed by any hard assets, but only by the faith of the Government.  Which one of the following is correct in respect of the above statements?

  ✅ A) Both Statement-I and Statement-II are correct and Statement-II explains Statement-I
     B) Both Statement-I and Statement-II are correct, but Statement-II does not explain Statement-I
     C) Statement-I is correct, but Statement-II is incorrect
     D) Statement-I is incorrect, but Statement-II is correct

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may think sovereign debt defaults mean bondholders can legally claim assets — but for sovereign debt, there is no international court to enforce claims; creditors cannot seize assets easily. Statement II correctly explains this.
  💡 **Eliminate:** If USA defaults: Treasury bondholders cannot exercise legal claims easily because US government debt is backed by 'full faith and credit' (fiat currency trust), not hard assets — Statement II explains why claims cannot be enforced. Answer: A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - US Treasury bonds are backed by 'full faith and credit' of US government — fiat money, not hard assets like gold
     - In sovereign debt default, creditors have no recourse to a bankruptcy court — no international enforcement mechanism

**[Q4297]** [2022]
> With reference to the Indian economy, consider the following statements:  1. An increase in Nominal Effective Exchange Rate (NEER) indicates the appreciation of rupee. 2. An increase in the Real Effective Exchange Rate (REER) indicates an improvement in trade competitiveness. 3. An increasing trend in domestic inflation relative to inflation in other countries is likely to cause an increasing divergence between NEER and REER.  Which of the above statements are correct?

     A) 1 and 2 only
     B) 2 and 3 only
  ✅ C) 1 and 3 only
     D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 is the trap: increase in REER indicates appreciation of domestic currency in real terms → this REDUCES export competitiveness, not improves it.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Statement 1 (NEER increase = rupee appreciation) is correct. Statement 2 is wrong — rising REER means domestic goods are relatively expensive, hurting competitiveness. Statement 3 (domestic inflation widens NEER-REER gap) is correct.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - NEER = nominal effective exchange rate; increase means domestic currency appreciates against basket
     - REER = NEER adjusted for inflation differentials; rising REER means loss of price competitiveness

**[Q4114]** [2021]
> Indian Government Bond Yields are influenced by which of the following?  1. Actions of the United States Federal Reserve 2. Actions of the Reserve Bank of India 3. Inflation and short-term interest rates  Select the correct answer using the code given below.

     A) 1 and 2 only
     B) 2 only
     C) 3 only
  ✅ D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may think only RBI influences bond yields, overlooking US Fed actions and domestic inflation — all three factors are correct.
  💡 **Eliminate:** All three factors influence Indian Government Bond Yields: US Fed rate actions (global capital flows), RBI actions (domestic monetary policy), and inflation/short-term rates — select option D (1, 2 and 3).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Indian G-sec yields rise when US Fed hikes rates (capital outflow from emerging markets)
     - RBI's repo rate changes directly impact government bond yields via liquidity and benchmark effects

**[Q4116]** [2021]
> Consider the following statements : The effect of devaluation of a currency is that it necessarily  1. improves the competitiveness of the domestic exports in the foreign markets 2. increases the foreign value of domestic currency 3. improves the trade balance  Which of the above statements is/are correct?

  ✅ A) 1 only
     B) 1 and 2
     C) 3 only
     D) 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 3 (devaluation necessarily improves trade balance) is false — it depends on the Marshall-Lerner condition and J-curve effect; devaluation often worsens trade balance initially.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Devaluation necessarily makes domestic exports cheaper in foreign markets (statement 1 correct); it reduces, not increases, the foreign value of domestic currency (statement 2 false); statement 3 is not automatic — only 1 is necessarily correct.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Currency devaluation makes exports cheaper for foreign buyers by reducing their cost in foreign currency
     - J-curve effect: trade balance may worsen initially after devaluation before improving

**[Q3799]** [2019]
> In the context of India, which of the following factors is/are contributor/ contributors to reducing the risk of a currency crisis?  1. The foreign currency earnings of India's IT sector 2. Increasing the government expenditure 3. Remittances from Indians abroad  Select the correct answer using the code given below.

     A) 1 only
  ✅ B) 1 and 3 only
     C) 2 only
     D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Increasing government expenditure (statement 2) actually increases fiscal deficit, widens current account deficit, and can worsen currency crisis — it is not a mitigant. Candidates may think government spending helps.
  💡 **Eliminate:** IT sector forex earnings (statement 1) and remittances (statement 3) both bring in foreign currency, reducing current account deficit and building reserves — both reduce currency crisis risk. Government expenditure increases risk. Answer is B.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - India's IT sector earns ~$200+ billion in forex annually — major CAD buffer
     - Remittances to India are among the world's largest — ~$100+ billion/year
     - Higher govt expenditure widens fiscal deficit and can worsen rupee depreciation pressure

**[Q3820]** [2019]
> Which one of the following is not the most likely measure the Government/ RBI takes to stop the slide of Indian rupee?

     A) Curbing imports of non-essential goods and promoting exports
     B) Encouraging Indian borrowers to issue rupee denominated Masala Bonds
     C) Easing conditions relating to external commercial borrowing
  ✅ D) Following an expansionary monetary policy

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Expansionary monetary policy (lower interest rates) increases money supply and actually weakens the rupee by reducing interest rate differential — it is counterproductive to stop rupee slide. This is the NOT correct measure.
  💡 **Eliminate:** To stop rupee depreciation: curb imports/boost exports (reduces CAD), encourage ECBs (brings forex), issue Masala Bonds (forex inflow). Expansionary monetary policy weakens rupee — the WRONG measure. Answer is D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Expansionary monetary policy (rate cuts) reduces interest rate differential, causing capital outflow, weakening rupee
     - Masala Bonds = rupee-denominated bonds issued abroad; bring forex to India
     - ECBs bring foreign currency loans; curbing imports reduces forex demand

**[Q3188]** [2016]
> Recently, which one of the following currencies has been proposed to be added to the basket of IMF's SDR?

     A) Rouble
     B) Rand
     C) Indian Rupee
  ✅ D) Renminbi

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Indian Rupee was widely speculated to be added to SDR basket; students may pick Rupee over China's Renminbi.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Chinese Renminbi (Yuan) was added to IMF's SDR basket in 2016, making it the 5th currency — answer is D (Renminbi).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - IMF added Chinese Renminbi to SDR basket in October 2016 — the 5th currency after USD, EUR, GBP, JPY
     - SDR = Special Drawing Rights, an international reserve asset created by IMF

**[Q3022]** [2015]
> Convertibility of rupee implies

     A) being able to convert rupee notes into gold
     B) allowing the value of rupee to be fixed by market forces
  ✅ C) freely permitting the conversion of rupee to other currencies and vice versa
     D) developing an international market for currencies in India

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Option A (convertibility = exchange for gold) is a classic trap referencing the gold standard which no longer applies; option B (market-determined exchange rate) is about floating rate, not convertibility per se.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Convertibility means freely converting one currency to another without restrictions; it has two forms — current account (for trade) and capital account (for investments).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Rupee convertibility means free conversion to/from other currencies
     - India has current account convertibility but restricted capital account convertibility
     - Full capital account convertibility remains a policy debate

**[Q2286]** [2010]
> Which of the following is/are treated as artificial currency?

     A) ADR
     B) GDR
  ✅ C) SDR
     D) Both ADR and SDR

  ⚠️ **Trap:** GDR (Global Depositary Receipt) is confused with artificial currency; ADR and GDR are real instruments representing real shares, not artificial currencies.
  💡 **Eliminate:** SDR (Special Drawing Rights) is an international reserve asset created by IMF — an artificial/supplementary currency. ADR and GDR are depositary receipts, not currencies. Answer: C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - SDR (Special Drawing Rights) = artificial reserve currency created by IMF in 1969
     - ADR/GDR = depositary receipts for shares, NOT currencies

**[Q1159]** [2002]
> Consider the following statements: Full convertibility of the rupee may mean 1. its free float with other international currencies. 2. its direct exchange with any other international currency at any prescribed place inside and outside the country. 3. it acts just like any other international currency. Which of these statements are correct?

     A) 1 and 2
     B) 1 and 3
     C) 2 and 3
  ✅ D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may hesitate on statement 3 thinking 'international currency' implies reserve currency status. All three statements collectively define full convertibility correctly.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Full convertibility means rupee can float freely, be exchanged anywhere without restrictions, and functions as any international currency — all three statements are correct.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Full convertibility = free float + unrestricted exchange + international currency status
     - India has current account convertibility but limited capital account convertibility

**[Q821]** [2000]
> Consider the following statements : The Indian rupee is fully convertible I. in respect of Current Account of Balance of Payment. II. in respect of Capital Account of Balance of Payment. III. into gold. Which of these statements is/are correct ?

  ✅ A) I alone
     B) III alone
     C) I and II
     D) I, II and III

  ⚠️ **Trap:** India has current account convertibility (I is TRUE) but students often mistake capital account as also being fully convertible — it is NOT; capital controls remain in place.
  💡 **Eliminate:** India has full current account convertibility since 1994 under IMF Article VIII; capital account is partially convertible (FDI allowed, some capital flows controlled); not convertible to gold.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - India accepted IMF Article VIII in 1994 making rupee fully convertible on current account
     - Capital account convertibility: India has partial convertibility — FDI allowed but full CAC not yet achieved

**[Q621]** [1999]
> Assertion (A) : Devaluation of a currency may promote export. Reason (R) : Price of the country’s products in the international market may fall due to devaluation.

  ✅ A) Both A and R are true, and R is the correct explanation of A
     B) Both A and R are true, but R is not a correct explanation of A
     C) A is true, but R is false
     D) A is false, but R is true

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may doubt whether devaluation actually promotes exports (it does) or may not understand how devaluation reduces export prices in foreign currency terms.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Both A and R are true — devaluation makes domestic currency cheaper, so foreign buyers pay less in their currency → Indian exports become more competitive → exports rise. R correctly explains A → Option A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Devaluation: reducing official exchange rate of domestic currency against foreign currencies
     - Post-devaluation: export prices fall in foreign currency terms → export competitiveness rises

**[Q458]** [1998]
> Consider the following statements : The price of any currency in international market is decided by the I. World Bank. II. demand for goods/services provided by the country concerned. III. stability of the government of the concerned country. IV. economic potential of the country in question. Of these statements :

     A) I, II, III and IV are correct
  ✅ B) II and III are correct
     C) III and IV are correct
     D) I and IV are correct

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may include World Bank as a currency price-setter or include IV (economic potential) as an obvious factor — but exchange rates in free markets are driven by demand-supply dynamics.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Exchange rate in international markets is set by demand for goods/services and political stability — World Bank does NOT set exchange rates; economic potential is indirect, not direct.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Exchange rate determined by: trade demand, political stability, speculation, interest rates
     - World Bank/IMF do not fix exchange rates (unlike Bretton Woods era)

**[Q197]** [1996]
> One of the important goals of the economic liberalisation policy is to achieve full convertibility of the Indian rupee. This is being advocated because

  ✅ A) convertibility of the rupee will stabilize its exchange value against major currencies of the world
     B) it will attract more foreign capital inflow in India
     C) it will help promote exports
     D) it will help India secure loans from the world financial markets at attractive terms

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Options B, C, D are all benefits of convertibility but are secondary effects. Option A (stabilize exchange value) is actually questionable — full convertibility often leads to exchange rate volatility, not stability. Yet it is the stated correct answer per the question.
  💡 **Eliminate:** The stated reason for advocating full convertibility in this context (mid-1990s debate) was that market-determined rates would reflect true value and thus stabilize long-term. Correct per answer key: A. However, note that B (attract foreign capital) is arguably a stronger practical reason.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Capital Account Convertibility (CAC) recommended by Tarapore Committee (1997)
     - Full convertibility allows free exchange of rupee for foreign currency at market rates

**[Q3970]** [2020]
> If another global financial crisis happens in the near future, which of the following actions/policies are most likely to give some immunity to India?  1. Not depending on short-term foreign borrowings 2. Opening up to more foreign banks 3. Maintaining full capital account convertibility  Select the correct answer using the code given below:

  ✅ A) 1 only
     B) 1 and 2 only
     C) 3 only
     D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Opening to more foreign banks (2) sounds counterproductive to immunity but could diversify sources; full capital account convertibility (3) actually increases vulnerability to capital flight during crises.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Full capital account convertibility increases exposure to volatile capital flows during crises. Not depending on short-term foreign borrowings (1) is the clearest immunity measure. Foreign bank entry (2) doesn't directly provide crisis immunity.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Short-term foreign debt creates rollover risk during global financial crises
     - Full capital account convertibility can amplify crisis contagion through sudden capital outflows

**[Q3816]** [2019]
> Consider the following statements :  1. Purchasing Power Parity (PPP) exchange rates are calculated by comparing the prices of the same basket of goods and services in different countries. 2. In terms of PPP dollars, India is the sixth largest economy in the world.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

  ✅ A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
     C) Both 1 and 2
     D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 is date-sensitive — by 2019 India had overtaken Japan to become third largest economy in PPP terms (after China and USA); the question was set in 2019 when India was third or fourth, not sixth.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Statement 1 (PPP calculated by comparing same basket of goods — Big Mac Index principle) is correct; statement 2 (India sixth largest in PPP — was incorrect even in 2019 as India was 3rd) is wrong — answer is statement 1 only.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - PPP exchange rates use 'basket of goods' comparison to equalize purchasing power across nations
     - India is typically the 3rd largest economy by PPP (after USA and China) — not 6th
