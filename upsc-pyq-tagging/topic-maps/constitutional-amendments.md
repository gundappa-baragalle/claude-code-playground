**[Q4907]** [2025]
> With reference to the Indian polity, consider the following statements :  I. An Ordinance can amend any Central Act. Il. An Ordinance can abridge a Fundamental Right. III. An Ordinance can come into effect from a back date.  Which of the statements given above are correct?

     A) I and II only
     B) II and III only
  ✅ C) I and III only
     D) I, II and III

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement II (Ordinance can abridge Fundamental Rights) is the trap — an Ordinance cannot override or abridge Fundamental Rights, as it must be consistent with the Constitution.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Ordinances CAN amend Central Acts (I correct) and CAN have retrospective effect/back date (III correct). Ordinances CANNOT abridge Fundamental Rights (II wrong). Answer: C (I and III).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Ordinances can amend any Central Act (including constitutional ones with limitations)
     - Ordinances can be retrospective but cannot violate Fundamental Rights

**[Q4914]** [2025]
> Consider the following subjects under the Constitution of India :  I. List I-Union List, in the Seventh Schedule II. Extent of the executive power of a State III. Conditions of the Governor's office  For a constitutional amendment with respect to which of the above, ratification by the Legislatures of not less than one-half of the States is required before presenting the bill to the President of India for assent?

  ✅ A) I and II only
     B) II and III only
     C) I and III only
     D) I, II and III

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Article 368 specifies which amendments require state ratification: Union List (Schedule VII), extent of executive power of Union/States, representation in Parliament, SC/HC provisions. Conditions of Governor's office (Article 155/158) does NOT require state ratification.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Ratification by at least half of state legislatures required for: Union List (Schedule VII), extent of executive power of States — yes. Governor's conditions of office — NOT in the ratification list (Article 368(2) proviso). Answer: A (I and II only).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Article 368(2) proviso: ratification by ≥50% state legislatures needed for amendments affecting Union/State executive power, Seventh Schedule, Supreme Court, etc.
     - Governor's office conditions (Articles 155-158) NOT in the special ratification requirement list

**[Q4735]** [2024]
> As per Article 368 of the Constitution of India, the Parliament may amend any provision of the Constitution by way of:  1. Addition 2. Variation 3. Repeal  Select the correct answer using the code given below:

     A) 1 and 2 only
     B) 2 and 3 only
     C) 1 and 3 only
  ✅ D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may think 'Repeal' is not permitted under Article 368 as it seems to conflict with Basic Structure doctrine — but Article 368 text explicitly includes repeal as a method.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Article 368 verbatim states Parliament may amend by way of 'addition, variation or repeal' — all three are correct. Answer is D. Note: Repeal of Basic Structure features is still constitutionally impermissible despite the text.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Article 368 uses the words 'addition, variation or repeal' as methods of constitutional amendment
     - Kesavananda Bharati case (1973) held that Parliament cannot use Article 368 to repeal/abrogate Basic Structure

**[2025]** Q12. Indian Constitution has conferred the amending power on the ordinary legislative institutions with a few procedural hurdles. In view of this statement, examine the procedural and substantive limitations on the amending power of the Parliament to change the Constitution.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Constitutional amendment, Judicial review, Parliamentary sovereignty

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Parliament's amending power under Article 368, while broad, is constrained by both procedural safeguards and substantive limits flowing from the basic structure doctrine.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Procedural: special majority (2/3 present and voting + majority of total membership) for most amendments; state ratification for federal provisions
      • Substantive: basic structure — SC can invalidate amendments destroying democratic governance, fundamental rights, judicial review, federalism, separation of powers
      • Development: Golaknath (1967) — fundamental rights unamendable; overruled by Kesavananda (1973) — amendable but not to destroy basic structure
      • Minerva Mills (1980): limited Parliament's amending power explicitly
      • Current position: Parliament's amending power is 'wide but not unlimited' (Kesavananda)
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The combination of procedural hurdles and the basic structure doctrine ensures that constitutional change remains deliberate and rights-protective, balancing legislative flexibility with constitutional integrity.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: Polity, Constitutional Framework, Fundamental Rights
  🌐 Contemporary: Debates on amending DPSP-FR hierarchy and EWS reservation's constitutional validity invoke basic structure arguments.

**[2018]** Q3. Under what circumstances can the Financial Emergency be proclaimed by the President of India? What consequences follow when such a declaration remains in force?
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Emergency provisions, Presidential powers, Constitutional safeguards

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Article 360's Financial Emergency provision represents the Constitution's response to existential fiscal crises, establishing a rare but powerful mechanism that has never been invoked in India's post-independence history.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Proclamation conditions: President must be satisfied that India's financial stability/credit is threatened
      • Cabinet advice required for proclamation — safeguard against arbitrary use
      • Consequences: Centre can direct States on financial matters; reduce salaries of all civil servants, HC/SC judges
      • Money Bills of states can be reserved for President's consideration
      • Duration: indefinite unless revoked; Parliament must approve within 2 months (majority of each house)
      • Revocation: by President anytime — no need for Parliamentary resolution
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The Financial Emergency provision's non-use reflects both India's resilience in managing fiscal crises through ordinary mechanisms and the high threshold appropriately set for such a drastic constitutional intervention.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Polity — emergency provisions, Economy — fiscal crisis management, Polity — federalism and emergency
  🌐 Contemporary: COVID economic impact and GST revenue shortfalls triggered debates (academic) about whether Financial Emergency threshold was approaching.

**[2016]** Q2. To what extent is Article 370 of the Indian Constitution, bearing marginal note "Temporary provision with respect to the State of Jammu and Kashmir", temporary? Discuss the future prospects of this provision in the context of Indian polity.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: IMPACT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Constitutional special provisions, J&K's status, Centre-State relations in J&K

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Article 370's constitutional characterization as 'temporary' belied its entrenched political reality for seven decades, creating a fundamental tension between the constitutional text and the political permanence it acquired, resolved decisively by the 2019 abrogation.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Article 370: special status for J&K, limiting Parliament's legislative power, separate constitution
      • Temporary in text: Article 370(3) provided for presidential proclamation to cease its application
      • But practically permanent: the J&K Constituent Assembly dissolved in 1956 without recommending cessation — appeared perpetual
      • Debate: whether 'temporary' meant transitional or time-limited — conflicting interpretations for 70 years
      • 2019 abrogation: Presidential Order replaced J&K Constituent Assembly concurrence with Parliament (as J&K was under President's Rule)
      • SC 2023: upheld abrogation, reaffirmed it was always temporary — historical and textual interpretation accepted
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The 2019 abrogation and SC's 2023 validation settled the constitutional debate on Article 370's temporariness, though the political integration of J&K and restoration of statehood remain important ongoing processes.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Internal Security — J&K militancy and security, Polity — Centre-State relations, IR — India-Pakistan and Kashmir issue
  🌐 Contemporary: J&K statehood restoration promise and first post-370 elections (2024) make this an actively evolving political situation.

**[2015]** Q6. What are the major changes brought in the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996 through the recent Ordinance promulgated by the President? How far will it improve India's dispute resolution mechanism? Discuss.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: IMPACT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Dispute resolution mechanism, Investment climate, Legal reform

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The 2015 arbitration amendment sought to address India's reputation for slow and expensive commercial dispute resolution that deterred foreign investment and increased transaction costs.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Key changes: 12-month time limit for arbitration awards (extendable by 6 months), court permission required for stay of award
      • Neutrality: grounds for challenge of arbitrators tightened on bias; disclosure requirements enhanced
      • Fast-track arbitration: 6-month timeline for disputes below specified value
      • Court intervention minimized: courts to refer to arbitration if arbitration agreement exists
      • Limitations: India still lacks strong institutional arbitration bodies; many international parties prefer Singapore/London
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** While the 2015 amendment was a meaningful step toward investor-friendly dispute resolution, making India a global arbitration hub requires establishing world-class arbitral institutions and an arbitration-friendly judicial culture.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3: Indian economy — investment climate, GS2: Governance — rule of law, IR: India as investment destination
  🌐 Contemporary: India's aspiration to become a global arbitration hub underpins subsequent 2019 and 2021 arbitration amendments.
