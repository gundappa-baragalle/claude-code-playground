**[Q4947]** [2025]
> Consider the following statements :  I. Panchayats at the intermediate level exist in all States. II. To be eligible to be a Member of a Panchayat at the intermediate level, a person should attain the age of thirty years. III. The Chief Minister of a State constitutes a commission to review the financial position of Panchayats at the intermediate levels and to make recommendations regarding the distribution of net proceeds of taxes and duties, leviable by the State, between the State and Panchayats at the intermediate level.  Which of the statements given above are not correct?

     A) I and II only
     B) II and III only
     C) I and III only
  ✅ D) I, II and III

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement I: Intermediate Panchayats don't exist in all states (absent in small states like Goa, Manipur). Statement III: State Finance Commission (not Chief Minister) reviews Panchayat finances. All three are NOT correct.
  💡 **Eliminate:** I is wrong (not all states have intermediate level Panchayats). II is wrong (minimum age is 21, not 30). III is wrong (State Finance Commission, not CM, reviews Panchayat finances). Answer: D (all three not correct).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Intermediate Panchayats not required in states with population below 20 lakh
     - Minimum age for Panchayat membership = 21 years (not 30)
     - State Finance Commission reviews Panchayat finances — not the CM

**[2025]** Q17. "In contemporary development models, decision-making and problem-solving responsibilities are not located close to the source of information and execution defeating the objectives of development." Critically evaluate.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHY | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Decentralization, Developmental governance, Federalism

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Contemporary top-down development models suffer from an information asymmetry — decision-makers are far from both the problem and its execution, undermining development effectiveness.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Information asymmetry: central planners lack granular local knowledge
      • Execution gap: implementation agencies not involved in design — mismatches in schemes
      • Subsidiarity: decisions at lowest effective level improves relevance and ownership
      • Counterarguments: scale economies in central procurement, uniform standards, anti-corruption oversight
      • Way forward: district-level planning, participatory design, Gram Sabha as planning unit
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Effective development requires decentralizing both decision-making and problem-solving to levels closest to beneficiaries, while maintaining central accountability frameworks.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: Federalism, GS3: Economic planning, Governance: local bodies
  🌐 Contemporary: SVAMITVA scheme, Mission Antyodaya, and PMGSY local planning elements reflect this approach.

**[2024]** Q5. Analyse the role of local bodies in providing good governance at local level and bring out the pros and cons merging the rural local bodies with the urban local bodies.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Local governance, Urban-rural integration, Decentralization

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Local bodies are the constitutionally mandated third tier of governance, crucial for delivering grassroots-level services and ensuring participatory democracy.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Role of local bodies: Provision of basic services (water, sanitation, roads), local planning, registration, social sector implementation
      • Good governance at local level: Better knowledge of local needs, accountability to local people, participatory decision-making
      • Pros of merging rural-urban bodies: Integrated planning for peri-urban areas, economies of scale, reduced duplication in expanding urban fringes
      • Cons of merger: Rural communities lose distinct representation; agricultural and urban priorities differ; risks marginalizing tribal and panchayat communities
      • Examples: Several states have seen friction when urban growth absorbs peri-urban panchayats
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Rather than wholesale merger, a coordinated planning framework under district planning committees (Article 243ZD) can better integrate rural and urban local governance.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 — Local self-governance, GS3 — Urbanization
  🌐 Contemporary: Smart Cities Mission and AMRUT have renewed debates about urban-rural local body coordination.

**[2023]** Q3. "The states in India seem reluctant to empower urban local bodies both functionally as well as financially." Comment.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Urban governance, Decentralization, Fiscal federalism at urban level

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Despite the 74th Constitutional Amendment's promise of empowered urban local bodies, most ULBs in India remain financially and functionally dependent on state governments — undermining true decentralization.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Functions: 12th Schedule lists 18 functions but most states have transferred only select functions; fire services, planning often retained
      • Finance: ULB own revenues negligible; property tax collection inefficient; State Finance Commission recommendations often not implemented
      • State reluctance: political competition — state politicians reluctant to empower urban bodies controlled by opposition
      • Reform needs: mandatory transfer of all 12th Schedule functions, independent SFC with binding recommendations, professionalization of municipal administration
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** True urban self-governance requires political will from states to treat ULBs as genuine third-tier governments — matching constitutional intent with financial and functional reality.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 Polity — local government, GS3 Economy — urban infrastructure
  🌐 Contemporary: Rapid urbanization and the urban infrastructure deficit make ULB empowerment an urgent priority.

**[2022]** Q3. To what extent, in your opinion, has the decentralisation of power in India changed the governance landscape at the grassroots?
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Decentralization, Grassroots democracy, Panchayati Raj effectiveness

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's constitutional decentralization through the 73rd Amendment created institutional architecture for grassroots governance, but the translation of this mandate into meaningful local empowerment has been uneven and state-dependent.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • 73rd Amendment: mandatory elections, reservation for SC/ST/women, Gram Sabha, SFCs, District Planning Committees
      • Success stories: Kerala, Rajasthan, Karnataka — genuine devolution of functions, funds and functionaries
      • Failures: most states retained control — Panchayats without funds, functions or functionaries
      • Changed governance landscape: increased local accountability, beneficiary identification, social audit (MGNREGA)
      • Women's participation: 33% reservation produced leadership pipeline — many states increased to 50%
      • Challenges: financial dependency on state, political interference, technical capacity gaps
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** True decentralization requires states to genuinely devolve the three Fs — functions, funds and functionaries — to Panchayats, which is more a matter of political will than constitutional mandate.
  🔗 Interlinkages: Polity — federalism and Centre-State relations, Governance — scheme implementation at grassroots, Society — women's political empowerment
  🌐 Contemporary: PM's emphasis on 'Vibrant Village Programme' and Panchayat-level implementation of flagship schemes keeps decentralization central to governance discourse.

**[2019]** Q13. "The reservation of seats for women in the institutions of local self-government has had a limited impact on the patriarchal character of the Indian Political Process." Comment.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: IMPACT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Women's political empowerment, Tokenism vs real power, Local self-government

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** While women's reservation in local bodies has transformed the numerical face of Indian democracy, its impact on the underlying patriarchal character of politics has been limited and contested.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Positive impact: women's issues (water, toilets, children's health) gain prominence on panchayat agendas; visibility effect inspires girls
      • Limitations: proxy representation ('sarpanch pati') — male family members exercise actual decision-making; women sarpanches often rubber-stamp male decisions
      • Structural barriers: illiteracy, lack of financial autonomy, social norms preventing public participation
      • Evidence of change: research shows second-generation women representatives perform far more independently — reservation's cumulative effect is positive
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Women's reservation is a necessary but insufficient condition for transforming patriarchal politics — it must be complemented by capacity building, financial autonomy, and social norm change.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 Polity — local government, GS1 Society — women, GS2 Governance — decentralization
  🌐 Contemporary: Women's Reservation Act 2023 for Parliament and State Assemblies reignites debate on substantive vs formal representation.

**[2017]** Q1. "The local self-government system in India has not proved to be effective instrument of governance." Critically examine the statement and give your views to improve the situation.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Local self-governance gaps, Decentralization challenges, Reform agenda for PRIs

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** While the 73rd and 74th Amendments constitutionally empowered local bodies, structural, financial and political constraints have hindered their evolution into effective instruments of governance.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Financial weakness: local bodies depend heavily on state grants; own revenue generation is very low
      • Functional deficit: states reluctant to devolve functions listed in 11th and 12th Schedules
      • Capacity: shortage of trained personnel, lack of technical expertise in rural local bodies
      • Political interference: bureaucratic control overrides elected representatives
      • Reforms needed: direct central grants, mandatory activity mapping, empowering Gram Sabhas, performance-linked devolution
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Genuine decentralization requires states to transfer the three Fs — Functions, Funds and Functionaries — with political will and institutional accountability.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 – Federalism, GS3 – Rural development
  🌐 Contemporary: 15th Finance Commission increased local body grants; implementation quality remains uneven.

**[2015]** Q3. In absence of a well-educated and organized local level government system, 'Panchayats' and 'Samitis' have remained mainly political institutions and not effective instruments of governance. Critically discuss.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: WHY | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Decentralization and local governance, Constitutional vs functional democracy, Capacity building in rural governance

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Despite three decades since the 73rd Amendment, India's Panchayati Raj institutions continue to function more as patronage-distribution bodies than as genuine self-governance institutions.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Political vs governance role: PRIs used for electoral mobilization rather than service delivery
      • Structural gaps: inadequate devolution of funds (State Finance Commissions underfunded), limited own-source revenue
      • Human resource deficits: lack of trained personnel, low educational levels of elected representatives
      • Bureaucratic resistance: parallel structures (line departments) bypass PRIs; DM/Collector dominance
      • Way forward: mandatory devolution of 29 subjects, capacity-building programs, linking funds to performance, strengthening Gram Sabhas
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Transforming PRIs from political entities into effective governance instruments requires structural devolution backed by administrative capacity and community participation.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3: Rural development, GS4: Ethics in public administration, Constitutional framework (Article 243)
  🌐 Contemporary: 15th Finance Commission's recommendations for direct grants to local bodies reflect ongoing decentralization challenges.
