**[Q2215]** [2009]
> Consider the following statements : 1. The Baglihar Power Project had been constructed within the parameters of the Indus Water Treaty. 2. The project was completely built by the Union Government with loans from Japan and the World Bank. Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

  ✅ A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
     C) Both 1 and 2
     D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 claims World Bank and Japan funded Baglihar; this is incorrect — Baglihar was built by J&K state with domestic resources, not by Union Government or World Bank.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Statement 1 is correct — Baglihar was built within Indus Water Treaty parameters (confirmed by World Bank neutral expert). Statement 2 is false. Answer: A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Baglihar dam was ruled compliant with Indus Waters Treaty by World Bank neutral expert Raymond Lafitte (2007)
     - Baglihar is on the Chenab River in J&K

**[Q1256]** [2003]
> Consider the following statements regarding the relations between India and Pakistan: 1. During Shimla Agreement, Indira Gandhi and Zulfikar Bhutto agreed to maintain the sanctity of LoC 2. Lahore Summit took place in the year 1997 3. Islamabad Summit was held between Rajiv Gandhi and Nawaz Sharif Which of these statements is/are correct?

     A) 1, 2 and 3
     B) 1 and 3
     C) Only 2
  ✅ D) Only 1

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may believe the Lahore Summit was in 1997 (it was 1999) and that Shimla Agreement sanctity of LoC was explicitly agreed (it was, but called 'Line of Control' from ceasefire line).
  💡 **Eliminate:** Lahore Summit = 1999 (Vajpayee-Nawaz Sharif), not 1997 (II is wrong). Islamabad Summit was between Vajpayee and Musharraf, not Rajiv-Nawaz (III wrong). Only Statement I (Shimla LoC sanctity) is correct.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Shimla Agreement (1972): India-Pakistan agreed to respect LoC sanctity
     - Lahore Declaration: February 1999, Vajpayee-Nawaz Sharif
     - Islamabad Summit 2004: Vajpayee-Musharraf, not Rajiv-Nawaz

### GS1

**[2016]** Q18. Present an account of the Indus Water Treaty and examine its ecological, economic and political implications in the context of changing bilateral relations.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: IMPACT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Water diplomacy, Transboundary water conflicts, India-Pakistan relations

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The Indus Waters Treaty, long hailed as a model of water diplomacy, is now under strain from climate change, growing water scarcity, and deteriorating bilateral relations.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Ecological: Glacial melt in Himalayas altering seasonal flow of Indus system — treaty not designed for such variability
      • Economic: India's hydropower projects on western rivers (Kishanganga, Ratle) contested by Pakistan
      • Political: India's occasional threats to abrogate/modify treaty as leverage in bilateral tensions
      • India's position: treaty allows run-of-the-river projects; Pakistan claims storage violations
      • World Bank's dual-track arbitration created diplomatic complications
      • Pakistan's existential water stress: agriculture dependent on Indus rivers; any reduction catastrophic
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The Indus Waters Treaty requires renegotiation to account for climate change impacts and new infrastructure needs, but this demands diplomatic goodwill that the current India-Pakistan relationship cannot easily provide.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2: India-Pakistan relations, GS3: Water resources, Environment: Climate change
  🌐 Contemporary: India's suspension of IWT participation following Pahalgam attack (2025) has made treaty's future deeply uncertain.

### GS2

**[2016]** Q19. "Increasing cross-border terrorist attacks in India and growing interference in the internal affairs of several member-states by Pakistan are not conducive for the future of SAARC (South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation)." Explain with suitable examples.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: IMPACT | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: SAARC's future, Cross-border terrorism, Regional cooperation

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Pakistan's continued export of cross-border terrorism and interference in SAARC member states' affairs has fundamentally undermined the regional organization's functionality, raising serious questions about South Asian regional cooperation's viability.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Pakistan's cross-border terrorism: Mumbai (2008), Uri (2016), Pulwama (2019) — state-sponsored attacks
      • SAARC paralysis: India boycotted 2016 Islamabad summit; SAARC summits suspended since
      • Interference in internal affairs: Pakistan's support for separatism in Kashmir, Afghanistan Taliban
      • SAFTA (South Asian Free Trade Area) remains underutilized — India-Pakistan trade minimal
      • India's pivot: BIMSTEC as alternative regional forum excluding Pakistan
      • Bangladesh, Nepal, Maldives, Sri Lanka — frustrated by SAARC paralysis
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** SAARC's revival is hostage to India-Pakistan normalization, and absent fundamental changes in Pakistan's security policy, India's strategic investment in BIMSTEC and bilateral connectivity with individual neighbors is a more productive regional approach.
  🔗 Interlinkages: IR — India-Pakistan relations, Internal Security — cross-border terrorism, IR — India's neighborhood diplomacy
  🌐 Contemporary: SAARC has not met since 2016; BIMSTEC increasingly active — making this SAARC vs BIMSTEC choice a live strategic decision.

**[2015]** Q20. Terrorist activities and mutual distrust have clouded India-Pakistan relations. To what extent the use of soft power like sports and cultural exchanges could help generate goodwill between the two countries? Discuss with suitable examples.
  *Paper: GS2 | Dim: HOW | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: India-Pakistan soft power, Terrorism vs diplomacy, Cultural diplomacy

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India-Pakistan relations operate simultaneously on two levels — official hostility and unofficial cultural affinity — creating unique space for soft power diplomacy.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Cricket: 2004 India's tour to Pakistan — mass goodwill; 1987 series; shared cricket culture transcends borders
      • Cultural exchanges: Bollywood universally watched in Pakistan; shared Urdu/Punjabi literature; Sufi music
      • Track II: Pugwash-style academic dialogues, business chamber exchanges, journalist networks
      • Limits: 26/11 froze cricketing ties; Pulwama 2019 ended air links; military-intelligence nexus controls final decisions
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Soft power creates goodwill but cannot override hard security — sustainable normalization requires dismantling state-sponsored terrorism as a policy instrument.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS2 IR — India-Pakistan, GS1 Culture, GS3 — Internal Security
  🌐 Contemporary: ICC World Cup 2023 India-Pakistan match drew 35 crore viewers — reflecting enduring people-to-people cricket connect.
