**[Q4928]** [2025]
> The famous female figurine known as 'Dancing Girl', found at Mohenjo-daro, is made of

     A) carnelian
     B) clay
  ✅ C) bronze
     D) gold

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Carnelian is a stone used extensively in IVC jewellery, making it a plausible distractor; clay is used for pottery and terracotta figurines, also a trap.
  💡 **Eliminate:** The Dancing Girl figurine from Mohenjo-daro is made of bronze (copper-tin alloy) — a hallmark example of IVC metallurgy. Answer: C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Dancing Girl (Mohenjo-daro) = bronze figurine, 10.5 cm tall
     - Demonstrates lost-wax (cire perdue) casting technique of IVC

**[Q4140]** [2021]
> Which one of the following ancient towns is well-known for its elaborate system of water harvesting and management by building a series of dams and channelizing water into connected reservoirs?

  ✅ A) Dholavira
     B) Kalibangan
     C) Rakhigarhi
     D) Ropar

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Rakhigarhi is famous as the largest IVC site and could mislead; Kalibangan is known for its ploughed field — neither is noted for elaborate water harvesting dams.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Dholavira in Gujarat is uniquely known for its sophisticated water harvesting system with reservoirs and dams — this is its most cited distinguishing feature in UPSC sources.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Dholavira had a series of 16+ reservoirs connected by channels for water harvesting
     - Dholavira's water management system was sophisticated enough to sustain the settlement in an arid zone

**[Q3741]** [2019]
> Which one of the following is not a Harappan site?

     A) Chanhudaro
     B) Kot Diji
  ✅ C) Sohgaura
     D) Desalpur

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Sohgaura is an obscure name — candidates may be unfamiliar and guess it is Harappan. All other options (Chanhudaro, Kot Diji, Desalpur) are well-known Harappan sites.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Sohgaura is an ancient site in UP associated with a Mauryan copper plate inscription — it is NOT a Harappan site. Answer: C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Sohgaura (Gorakhpur, UP) contains a famous Mauryan-era copper plate edict — not Harappan
     - Chanhudaro (Sindh), Kot Diji (Sindh), Desalpur (Gujarat) are all confirmed Harappan sites

**[Q3362]** [2017]
> With reference to the difference between the culture of Rigvedic Aryans and Indus Valley people, which of the following statements is/are correct?  1. Rigvedic Aryans used the coat of mail and helmet in warfare whereas the people of Indus Valley Civilization did not leave any evidence of using them. 2. Rigvedic Aryans knew gold, silver and copper whereas Indus Valley people knew only copper and iron. 3. Rigvedic Aryans had domesticated the horse whereas there is no evidence of Indus Valley people having been aware of this animal.  Select the correct answer using the code given below:

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 and 3 only
  ✅ C) 1 and 3 only
     D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 is the key trap — IVC people knew gold, silver, and copper but NOT iron; Rigvedic Aryans did not know iron either. Candidates may incorrectly assign iron to IVC or Aryans.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Iron Age came after both IVC and early Rigvedic period; eliminate any option that includes statement 2. Statements 1 and 3 are correct, pointing to option C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - IVC people did not use iron — it was a Bronze Age civilization
     - Horse evidence absent in IVC; domesticated horses are Aryan markers
     - Rigvedic Aryans used armor and helmets (coats of mail)

**[Q2636]** [2013]
> Which of the following characterizes/ characterize the people of Indus Civilization?  1. They possessed great palaces and temples. 2. They worshipped both male and female deities. 3. They employed horse-drawn chariots in warfare.  Select the correct statement/ statements using the codes given below.

     A) 1 and 2 only
  ✅ B) 2 only
     C) 1, 2 and 3
     D) None of the statements given above is correct

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 1 (great palaces and temples) is a common misconception — IVC had no confirmed palaces or temples. Statement 3 (horse-drawn chariots) is also debated; horses were not well attested in IVC.
  💡 **Eliminate:** IVC: no confirmed palaces or temples (1 wrong); both male (proto-Shiva, phallic symbols) and female (Mother Goddess) deities worshipped (2 correct); no confirmed horse-drawn war chariots (3 wrong). Answer is B (2 only).
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Indus Valley Civilisation worshipped both a proto-Shiva figure (Pashupati seal) and Mother Goddess figurines
     - No palaces or temples have been conclusively identified in Harappan sites; Great Bath at Mohenjo-daro is a civic structure

**[Q2483]** [2011]
> Regarding the Indus Valley Civilization, consider the following statements:  1. It was predominantly a secular civilization and the religious element, though present, did not dominate the scene. 2. During this period, cotton was used for manufacturing textiles in India.  Which of the statements given above is/are correct?

     A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
  ✅ C) Both 1 and 2
     D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may doubt Statement 1 thinking IVC was highly religious (based on 'priest-king' figurines and 'mother goddess'), but archaeologists note religion did not dominate public life unlike later civilizations.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Both statements are correct: IVC shows secular urban planning (no dominant temple structures); cotton textiles evidence found at Mohenjo-daro. Answer: C.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Indus Valley Civilization is notable for absence of dominant temple structures suggesting secular orientation
     - Cotton (Gossypium arboreum) was first cultivated and used for textiles in IVC — oldest evidence worldwide

**[Q927]** [2001]
> Which one of the following animals was NOT represented on the seals and terracotta art of the Harappan culture ?

     A) Cow
     B) Elephant
     C) Rhinoceros
  ✅ D) Tiger

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Tiger IS found on Harappan seals (the famous Pashupati seal has tiger); students often confuse 'NOT represented' and select an animal that is represented; the cow is absent while bull/unicorn bull is present.
  💡 **Eliminate:** The question asks what was NOT represented; tiger appears on Harappan seals (Pashupati seal); elephant and rhinoceros appear on Harappan terracotta; cow is famously absent while the bull/unicorn is common.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Harappan seals feature unicorn bull, elephant, rhinoceros, tiger (Pashupati seal), but NOT the domestic cow
     - The sacred cow absent from Harappan iconography is significant contrast with later Vedic culture

**[Q338]** [1997]
> Which one of the following scripts of ancient India was written from right to left?

     A) Brahmi
     B) Nandnagari
     C) Sharada
  ✅ D) Kharoshti

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Brahmi (left to right) is the most well-known ancient script and is often defaulted to. Sharada and Nandnagari are variants of Devanagari tradition — all written left to right.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Kharoshti is written from right to left — it is the only major ancient Indian script with this property. Used in northwest India (Gandhara), influenced by Aramaic. Select D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Kharoshti — right to left script; used in NW India (Gandhara); Aramaic influence
     - Brahmi — left to right; parent script of most South and Southeast Asian scripts

**[Q485]** [1998]
> What is the correct chronological order in which the following appeared in India ? I. Gold coins II. Punch-marked silver coins III. Iron plough IV. Urban culture Select the correct answer using the codes given below : Codes :

     A) III, IV, I, II
     B) III, IV, II, I
     C) IV, III, I, II
  ✅ D) IV, III, II, I

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Urban culture appears first intuitively (cities = civilization), but IVC predates iron; iron plough and urban culture both exist in IVC but sequence is nuanced.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Sequence: Urban culture (IVC ~2500 BC) → Iron plough (early Vedic ~1000 BC) → Punch-marked silver coins (700 BC) → Gold coins (post-Mauryan, Kushana era ~1st century BC/AD); answer is IV, III, II, I.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Urban culture: Indus Valley Civilization (~2500 BC)
     - Iron plough: Vedic Age (~1000 BC)
     - Punch-marked coins: ~600 BC (pre-Mauryan)
     - Gold coins: ~1st century BC (Indo-Greek period)

**[2025]** Q1. Discuss the salient features of the Harappan architecture.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Harappan urban planning, Ancient Indian architecture, IVC civilization

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Harappan architecture stands as remarkable testimony to the urban sophistication of one of the world's earliest civilizations, exhibiting town-planning principles millennia ahead of their time.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Grid plan: streets oriented cardinal directions; residential and commercial separation — true urban zoning
      • Standardized bricks: ratio 1:2:4 maintained across all Harappan sites — evidence of centralized control or shared standard
      • Drainage system: covered brick drains connecting houses to main sewers — more advanced than contemporary Mesopotamia
      • Public architecture: Great Bath (Mohenjo-daro), Granary, Assembly Hall — suggest civic and religious public life
      • Dholavira: unique water conservation system with reservoirs — adapted to arid environment
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Harappan architecture's emphasis on sanitation, standardization, and civic infrastructure reflects a civilization that prioritized collective wellbeing over monumental display.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 History — Ancient India, GS1 Art & Culture, GS1 — World History comparison
  🌐 Contemporary: Rakhigarhi excavations (ongoing) continue to reveal new dimensions of Harappan urbanism.

**[2023]** Q1. Explain the role of geographical factors towards the development of Ancient India.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Geography and civilization development, Environmental determinism in ancient India, River civilizations

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's ancient civilizations flourished in response to its diverse geography — from the river plains to mountain barriers — which shaped settlement patterns, economies, and political formations.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Indus Valley — fertile floodplains of Indus river system enabled agriculture and trade
      • Himalayas — barrier against invasions but also conduits via passes (Khyber, Bolan) for cultural exchange
      • Gangetic plains — alluvial soil supporting dense population and Mahajanapadas
      • Deccan plateau — basalt soil supported Chalcolithic cultures; Godavari-Krishna delta for South Indian kingdoms
      • Monsoon geography shaped agricultural calendar and water management (tanks, wells)
      • Sea coastline — maritime trade with Mesopotamia, Southeast Asia from early times
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Geographical factors were not merely passive backdrop but active forces that shaped the trajectory of ancient Indian civilization, trade, and cultural exchange.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 — Indian Geography and History interaction, GS2 — historical administrative boundaries
  🌐 Contemporary: Understanding geography-history linkages informs contemporary resource distribution and border issues.

**[2015]** Q1. The ancient civilization in Indian sub-continent differed from those of Egypt, Mesopotamia and Greece in that its culture and traditions have been preserved without a breakdown to the present day. Comment.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Indian civilizational uniqueness, Cultural continuity, Ancient to modern India

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Unlike other ancient civilizations that experienced definitive cultural ruptures, Indian civilization has maintained a remarkable thread of continuity from antiquity to the present day.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Egypt: ancient pharaonic culture replaced by Islamization — no direct cultural continuity with present Egypt
      • Mesopotamia: Babylonian-Assyrian civilization completely displaced by Arab-Islamic culture
      • Greece: Hellenistic culture largely displaced post-Byzantine by Ottoman rule; modern Greece is a cultural reconstruction
      • India: Vedic traditions, Sanskrit literature, yoga, temple worship, caste structures, languages (Sanskrit to Hindi/Tamil) show unbroken continuity
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's civilizational continuity, rooted in adaptive religious and social structures, represents a unique achievement in human history — simultaneously ancient and living.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 History — Ancient India, GS1 Art & Culture, GS1 Society

**[2014]** Q1. To what extent has the urban planning and culture of the Indus Valley Civilization provided inputs to the present day urbanization? Discuss.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Urban planning legacy, Ancient engineering, Continuity in Indian civilization

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The urban planning genius of the Indus Valley Civilization, evident in grid-pattern streets, sophisticated drainage, and standardized construction, offers enduring lessons for modern urbanization.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Grid layout with main and subsidiary streets — applicable to modern planned townships
      • Underground closed drainage system — precursor to modern sewerage; relevant for smart cities
      • Standardized bricks (3:2:1 ratio) show quality control — modern construction standards echo this
      • Citadel and lower town division — zoning concept applicable to modern urban planning
      • Water conservation: reservoirs, wells — relevant for urban water management today
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** IVC's urban heritage demonstrates that planned urbanization is not a modern invention but a civilizational tradition that can guide contemporary smart city initiatives.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 – Ancient History, GS1 – Indian culture, GS2 – Urban governance
  🌐 Contemporary: Smart Cities Mission echoes IVC urban planning principles of integrated infrastructure and civic amenities.
