**[Q2417]** [2011]
> Karl Marx explained the process of class struggle with the help of which one of the following theories ?

     A) Empirical liberalism
     B) Existentialism
     C) Darwin's theory of evolution
  ✅ D) Dialectical materialism

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may associate Darwin's evolution with Marxism superficially — Marx admired Darwin but his analytical tool for class struggle is dialectical materialism, not evolution theory.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Dialectical materialism: Marx's method analyzing historical change through thesis-antithesis-synthesis of material (economic) forces — specifically underpins his class struggle theory.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Dialectical materialism: Marx's framework — history driven by material/economic contradictions
     - Marx applied Hegel's dialectics to material (economic) conditions rather than ideas

**[Q1651]** [2006]
> Who among the following wrote “The Communist Manifesto” along with Karl Marx?

     A) Emile Durkheim
  ✅ B) Friedrich Engeles
     C) Robert Owen
     D) Max Weber

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may confuse Friedrich Engels with Emile Durkheim (sociologist) or Max Weber (sociologist) — all were European social theorists but only Engels co-authored the Communist Manifesto.
  💡 **Eliminate:** The Communist Manifesto (1848) was co-authored by Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels. Durkheim and Weber were sociologists, not co-authors of Marxist texts.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Communist Manifesto (1848): co-authored by Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels
     - Engels also co-wrote 'Das Kapital' volume 2 and 3 after Marx's death

> One common agreement between Gandhism and Marxism is

  ✅ A) the final goal of a stateless society
     B) class struggle
     C) abolition of private property
     D) economic determinism

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Class struggle (B) and abolition of private property (C) are purely Marxist; economic determinism (D) is also Marxist; only stateless society is a shared end-goal though through different means.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Both Gandhism (Ram Rajya/village swaraj) and Marxism (communist society) ultimately envision a stateless society as the final goal, though their paths differ fundamentally.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Both Gandhi (Gram Swaraj) and Marx (communist utopia) envisioned stateless societies as their ultimate goal
     - Class struggle and economic determinism are exclusively Marxist concepts

**[2025]** Q13. The French Revolution has enduring relevance to the contemporary world. Explain.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: French Revolution, Democracy, Contemporary relevance

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The French Revolution's ideals of liberty, equality, and fraternity transcend the late 18th-century context of their birth — they continue to animate democratic movements, constitutional frameworks, and social justice struggles across the contemporary world.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Liberty: Enlightenment-rooted civil liberties became universal human rights — foundation of modern constitutionalism
      • Equality: challenge to aristocratic hierarchy planted seeds of democratic egalitarianism — still incomplete globally
      • Fraternity: solidarity as political value — resonates in welfare state debates, social movements
      • Nationalism: French Revolution invented modern civic nationalism — still shapes both liberating and dangerous nationalisms
      • Secularism: revolutionary laicité — separation of church and state — influences contemporary secularism debates
      • Contemporary: Arab Spring protesters, Black Lives Matter, pro-democracy movements invoke Revolutionary language
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The French Revolution's enduring relevance lies not in its specific historical outcomes but in the universal ideals it proclaimed — ideals that continue to inspire and challenge every generation's struggle for a more just and free world.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: World history, GS2: Polity — secularism, IR: Human rights
  🌐 Contemporary: Current debates about rising authoritarianism, majoritarianism, and inequality everywhere invoke the Revolutionary promise of liberty, equality, and solidarity.

**[2019]** Q13. Explain how the foundations of the modern world were laid by the American and French Revolutions.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Modern world foundations, Democracy and revolution, Enlightenment legacy

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The American and French Revolutions were transformative events that dismantled the edifice of monarchy and feudalism, replacing them with the principles of popular sovereignty, individual rights, and constitutional governance that define the modern world.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • American Revolution: established first modern democratic republic; federal constitution with separation of powers became a template globally; Bill of Rights enshrined individual liberties
      • French Revolution: spread ideals of Liberty, Equality, Fraternity worldwide; abolished feudalism and privilege; inspired nationalist movements across Europe and Latin America
      • Shared Enlightenment foundations: Locke (social contract), Montesquieu (separation of powers), Rousseau (popular sovereignty)
      • Economic impact: French Revolution led to capitalist property rights; American Revolution preceded Industrial Revolution
      • Legacy: both inspired colonial independence movements including India's — Gandhi drew on Thoreau (American) and revolutionary French thought
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Together, these revolutions constituted the intellectual and political revolution that replaced divine authority with human reason as the basis of governance, permanently reshaping world history.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 Modern India — impact on Indian nationalism, GS2 Polity — constitutional principles
  🌐 Contemporary: French and American revolutionary ideals continue to anchor global democracy debates.

**[2014]** Q10. The New Economic Policy – 1921 of Lenin had influenced the policies adopted by India soon after independence. Evaluate.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: NEP influence on India, Mixed economy, Comparative economic policy

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Lenin's New Economic Policy of 1921 — a tactical retreat from pure communism that blended state control with market pragmatism — found a remarkable echo in India's post-independence economic framework.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • NEP features: allowed private trade, small businesses, market pricing in agriculture while state retained commanding heights (heavy industry, banking, foreign trade)
      • Nehru's mixed economy parallels: Industrial Policy Resolution 1948/1956 — public sector in strategic industries, private sector in consumer goods; Planning Commission modelled on Soviet Gosplan but with market coexistence
      • Direct influences: state capitalism, Five Year Plans, public sector as engine of growth, cooperative farming experiments all reflect NEP-type thinking
      • Key differences: India retained democracy and private property; NEP was temporary in USSR (replaced by Stalin's collectivization); India's mixed economy institutionalized
      • Critical assessment: Mahalanobis model (2nd Plan) combined Soviet-style heavy industry focus with democratic framework — NEP's pragmatism but democratic legitimacy
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Lenin's NEP demonstrated that ideological flexibility in economic policy is not betrayal but pragmatism — a lesson Nehru absorbed and adapted for India's democratic developmental experiment.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 World History, GS3 Economy, Planning
  🌐 Contemporary: India's post-1991 liberalization echoes NEP logic — market pragmatism over ideological rigidity.
