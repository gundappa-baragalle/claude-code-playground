### GS3

**[2024]** Q20. Social media and encrypting messaging services pose a serious security challenge. What measures have been adopted at various levels to address the security implications of social media? Also suggest any other remedies to address the problem. (250 words) 15
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: HOW | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Cyber security, Social media regulation, Privacy vs security

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Social media and encrypted messaging services present a dual challenge — vectors for security threats while also being tools of free expression and privacy that require nuanced regulation.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Security implications: terrorist coordination (IS, Maoist), disinformation campaigns, election interference, deepfakes
      • Measures taken: IT Rules 2021 — first originator traceability requirement, grievance appellate committees
      • Section 69 IT Act: government interception powers; CERT-In reporting mandates
      • State level: cyber cells in state police, social media monitoring units
      • Encryption debate: first originator compromises E2E encryption — platform resistance (WhatsApp litigation)
      • Remedies: Budapest Convention accession, AI-based content moderation, digital media literacy
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** A risk-proportionate regulatory framework that enables lawful interception for serious crimes while protecting privacy for ordinary communications is needed to address social media security threats.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3: Internal security, Polity: Fundamental Rights, GS2: Governance
  🌐 Contemporary: WhatsApp's challenge to IT Rules 2021, Pahalgam attack social media narratives, and deepfake election interference are directly relevant.

**[2016]** Q20. Use of Internet and social media by non-state actors for subversive activities is a major security concern. How have these been misused in the recent past? Suggest effective guidelines to curb the above threat. (200 words) 12.5
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: HOW | 12.5m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Cyber security, Information warfare, Counter-terrorism

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The weaponization of social media by non-state actors represents a serious asymmetric security challenge requiring a balanced regulatory response.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Misuse examples: ISIS social media recruitment (2014-2016), Rohingya crisis Facebook-fuelled violence, Pulwama anniversary online violence incitement
      • Counter-measures: IT Rules 2021 — significant social media intermediary obligations, grievance officers
      • National Cyber Security Policy 2013; CERT-In incident response
      • Section 69A IT Act: blocking orders for security-related content
      • Challenges: encrypted messaging (end-to-end encryption), jurisdiction, speed of spread
      • Guidelines: promote media literacy, international cooperation (Budapest Convention accession)
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Regulating non-state actors' use of social media requires a multi-stakeholder approach balancing platform accountability, law enforcement capabilities, and fundamental rights protection.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3: Internal security, GS2: IR, Polity: Fundamental Rights
  🌐 Contemporary: IT Rules 2021 litigation, fact-checking mechanisms, and deepfake regulation proposals are directly relevant.

**[2013]** Q22. What are social networking sites and what security implications do these sites present ? [200 words] 10
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Social media as security threat vector, Privacy vs surveillance debate, Regulatory framework for social media

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Social networking sites have transformed communication but simultaneously created significant security vulnerabilities for individuals, institutions, and nations.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Definition: platforms enabling user-generated content sharing, networking, and real-time communication (Facebook, Twitter/X, Instagram, LinkedIn)
      • Security implications: (a) Data privacy breaches — Cambridge Analytica case; (b) Phishing and identity theft; (c) Radicalization and recruitment by terrorist groups; (d) Misinformation/disinformation campaigns affecting elections
      • National security implications: foreign interference in elections (Russia-US 2016); coordinated inauthentic behavior
      • India's response: IT Amendment Rules 2021; CERT-In mandatory reporting; intermediary liability framework
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Balancing free expression and security requires robust regulation, digital literacy, and international cooperation on cyber norms.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 Internal Security, GS2 Governance/IT Policy, SEC.CYB
  🌐 Contemporary: Social media manipulation remains a live concern in India's election ecosystem and internal security landscape.
