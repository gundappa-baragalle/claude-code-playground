**[Q3605]** [2018]
> The terms 'WannaCry, Petya and EternalBlue' sometimes mentioned in the news recently are related to

     A) Exoplanets
     B) Cryptocurrency
  ✅ C) Cyber attacks
     D) Mini satellites

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Cryptocurrency and exoplanets are trendy terms that could confuse students unfamiliar with malware names; WannaCry/Petya are ransomware, EternalBlue is an NSA exploit tool.
  💡 **Eliminate:** WannaCry and Petya are ransomware attacks (2017); EternalBlue is the NSA-developed exploit used in WannaCry — all are related to cyber attacks.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - WannaCry: 2017 global ransomware attack using EternalBlue exploit
     - Petya/NotPetya: 2017 destructive ransomware targeting Ukraine and globally
     - EternalBlue: NSA cyberweapon leaked by Shadow Brokers, exploits Windows SMB vulnerability

**[Q3445]** [2017]
> In India, it is legally mandatory for which of the following to report on cyber security incidents?  1. Service providers 2. Data centres 3. Body corporate  Select the correct answer using the code given below:

     A) 1 only
     B) 1 and 2 only
     C) 3 only
  ✅ D) 1, 2 and 3

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Students may assume only telecom service providers or only corporations need to report; all three — service providers, data centres, and body corporates — are mandated under IT Act/CERT-In rules.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Under CERT-In directions (IT Act Section 70B), service providers, intermediaries, data centres, and body corporates are ALL mandated to report cyber security incidents — answer is all three.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - CERT-In mandates cyber incident reporting by service providers, data centres, and body corporates
     - IT Amendment Act 2008 and CERT-In rules make this legally mandatory

**[2022]** Q19. What are the different elements of cyber security? Keeping in view the challenges in cyber security, examine the extent to which India has successfully developed a comprehensive National Cyber Security Strategy.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Cyber security framework in India, Critical infrastructure protection

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** As India accelerates its digital transformation, cyber security has emerged as a critical pillar of national security requiring a comprehensive strategic framework.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Elements: network security, application security, information security, operational security, disaster recovery, end-user education
      • Challenges: shortage of skilled professionals, outdated infrastructure, proliferation of IoT devices, state-sponsored attacks, ransomware
      • India's measures: CERT-In, NCIIPC, Cyber Surakshit Bharat, Cyber Swachhta Kendra
      • Gaps: absence of a comprehensive data protection law (until DPDP Act 2023), fragmented institutional framework, limited cyber forensics capacity
      • Way forward: whole-of-government approach, public-private partnership, international cooperation
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India needs to translate its draft National Cyber Security Strategy into an operationally robust policy backed by legislative teeth, institutional coordination and capacity building.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 Internal Security, GS2 Governance — E-Governance, Science & Technology — IT
  🌐 Contemporary: Rising cyber attacks on critical infrastructure (power grids, banking) and the passage of the Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 make this highly relevant.

**[2021]** Q9. Discuss how emerging technologies and globalisation contribute to money laundering. Elaborate measures to tackle the problem of money laundering both at national and international levels.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Emerging technology and money laundering, International AML frameworks, India's PMLA and enforcement

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Emerging technologies and globalisation have exponentially expanded the avenues for money laundering, challenging both national and international regulatory frameworks.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • How technology enables laundering: cryptocurrencies provide anonymity; NFTs used for value transfer; digital wallets bypass banking KYC; dark web facilitates layering
      • Globalisation's role: offshore tax havens; shell companies in multiple jurisdictions; trade-based money laundering via over/under invoicing
      • National measures: PMLA 2002; Enforcement Directorate; Financial Intelligence Unit-India (FIU-IND); PML Rules requiring reporting
      • International measures: FATF recommendations and mutual evaluations; Egmont Group of FIUs; UN Convention Against Corruption (UNCAC); Basel AML Index
      • Challenges: Jurisdictional issues with crypto; regulatory arbitrage across countries; beneficial ownership opacity; emerging DeFi (decentralised finance)
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Tackling money laundering requires harmonising domestic laws with FATF standards while adapting rapidly to technological innovations that outpace regulatory frameworks.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3: Internal Security — terror financing, GS3: Economy — black money and tax evasion, GS2: International Relations — FATF and India
  🌐 Contemporary: India exited FATF's 'grey list' monitoring; cryptocurrency regulation remains a key challenge.

**[2021]** Q10. Keeping in view India’s internal security, analyse the impact of cross-border cyber attacks. Also discuss defensive measures against these sophisticated attacks.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Cyber security, Internal security, State-sponsored attacks

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Cross-border cyberattacks targeting India's critical infrastructure, financial systems, and defence networks represent an evolving asymmetric threat requiring sophisticated defensive architecture.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Impact: attacks on power grids, banking systems, defence networks, government portals
      • State-sponsored APTs from China, Pakistan targeting critical systems
      • Economic espionage, data theft, disinformation campaigns
      • Defensive measures: CERT-In incident response, NCCC for coordination, Zero Trust architecture
      • Capacity building: empanelled security auditors, security operations centres
      • Legal framework: IT Act 2000, amendments; need for dedicated cybercrime legislation
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India's cyber defence strategy must evolve from reactive incident response to proactive threat intelligence, deterrence, and international cyber norms advocacy.
  🔗 Interlinkages: China relations, Digital India, National Security
  🌐 Contemporary: India reported 13.91 lakh cyber incidents in 2022, highlighting the growing threat.

**[2020]** Q9. Discuss different types of cyber crimes and measures required to be taken to fight the menace.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Cybercrime threat landscape, Legal and technical countermeasures, Cyber governance

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Cybercrime has emerged as one of the fastest-growing threats to individuals, businesses, and national security in India's digital transformation era.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Types: financial fraud (phishing, vishing), ransomware, data breach, identity theft, cyberstalking, child pornography, cyber espionage
      • Critical infrastructure attacks: power grid, banking systems, hospital networks — high impact targets
      • Measures: IT Act 2000 (Sec 66 series), CERT-In for incident response, NIA for national security cyber cases
      • I4C: Indian Cyber Crime Coordination Centre under MHA for coordination among law enforcement
      • Cyber hygiene: National Cyber Security Awareness Month, Cyber Surakshit Bharat
      • International: Budapest Convention (India not member); bilateral CERTs; INTERPOL cooperation
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** An effective cyber crime response requires legal modernization, capacity building, international cooperation, and public awareness working in concert.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Internal Security, GS2 – Governance, GS3 – Science & Technology
  🌐 Contemporary: AIIMS ransomware attack 2022 and growing financial cyber fraud make this a top governance priority.

**[2018]** Q19. Data security has assumed significant importance in the digitized world due to rising cyber crimes. The Justice B. N. Srikrishna Committee Report addresses issues related to data security. What, in your view, are the strengths and weaknesses of the Report relating to protection of personal data in cyberspace?
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: IMPACT | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Data privacy law, Cyber security governance, State vs individual data rights

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The Srikrishna Committee Report laid the intellectual foundation for India's personal data protection framework, addressing the nexus between cybercrime and data security.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Strengths: comprehensive data principal rights, data fiduciary obligations, DPA establishment, sensitive data classification
      • Weaknesses: broad government exemption (Sec 35 equivalent); ambiguous 'legitimate purpose' definition; weak enforcement mechanism
      • Consent framework: robust consent architecture but 'deemed consent' dilutes protection
      • Cross-border data flows: localization requirement controversial; opposed by tech industry
      • Cyber crime linkage: data breaches enable identity theft, financial fraud — direct security implications
      • Comparison with GDPR: Srikrishna Report closer to GDPR; enacted DPDP Act 2023 diluted several provisions
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Effective personal data protection requires balancing individual privacy rights against state surveillance needs and industry innovation imperatives.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Internal Security, GS2 – Governance, GS3 – Science & Technology
  🌐 Contemporary: DPDP Act 2023 now enacted; its rules and implementation remain under active policy scrutiny.

  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Cyber attack threats, Security framework India, Critical infrastructure protection

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** As India's digital footprint expands, cyber attacks on critical infrastructure, financial systems and government networks represent one of the most potent asymmetric threats to national security.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Threat landscape: ransomware (WannaCry, Pegasus), DDoS attacks, state-sponsored hacking (APTs), phishing
      • Critical infrastructure: power grids, nuclear plants, banking systems, defence networks most vulnerable

  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 – Internal security, Science and Technology, Foreign policy
  🌐 Contemporary: India's Digital India expansion exponentially increases cyber attack surface requiring urgent framework upgrades.

**[2013]** Q21. Money laundering poses a serious security threat to a country's economic sovereignty. What is its significance for India and what steps are required to be taken to control this menace ?
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: IMPACT | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Economic security, Financial crime, Counter-terrorism financing

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Money laundering undermines India's economic sovereignty by enabling parallel shadow economies, funding terrorism, and corrupting financial institutions.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Significance: erodes tax base, funds terrorism (narco-terrorism nexus), enables regulatory capture
      • India's vulnerability: hawala networks, real estate and gold as laundering channels, benami properties
      • Steps taken: PMLA 2002 and amendments, Benami Property Act 2016, FATF mutual evaluation 2023-24
      • Institutional measures: ED, FIU-IND, SFIO — coordination challenges
      • International cooperation: MLA treaties, FATF membership, Egmont Group
      • Technology: crypto AML — VASP regulation under PMLA (2023 amendment)
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Combating money laundering requires strengthening inter-agency coordination, closing regulatory gaps in emerging financial technologies, and deepening international cooperation.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3: Internal security, Economy: Banking, IR: Global financial governance
  🌐 Contemporary: India's FATF mutual evaluation (2023-24) and ED's expanded PMLA jurisdiction under SC scrutiny are extremely current.

**[2013]** Q23. Cyber warfare is considered by some defense analysts to be a larger threat than even Al Qaeda or terrorism. What do you understand by Cyber warfare ? Outline the cyber threats which India is vulnerable to and bring out the state of the country's preparedness to deal with the same.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Cyber warfare as modern security threat, India's cyber vulnerabilities, Preparedness and institutional response

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Cyber warfare — the use of digital attacks to disrupt, damage, or destroy adversary systems — has emerged as a critical dimension of 21st century conflict.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Definition: Cyber warfare involves state-sponsored offensive operations targeting adversary's digital infrastructure — power grids, financial systems, defence networks
      • India's vulnerabilities: critical infrastructure poorly protected; rising incidents (AIIMS Delhi ransomware 2022); border tensions creating cyber espionage risk
      • Threats: (a) State actors (China, Pakistan); (b) Non-state actors and proxies; (c) Ransomware on critical services
      • Preparedness: CERT-In (incident response); NCIIPC (critical infrastructure); National Cyber Security Policy 2013; Cyber Surakshit Bharat initiative; but gaps remain in skilled workforce and attribution capability
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** India must urgently strengthen its cyber defence architecture through institutional capacity, international cooperation, and a comprehensive cyber doctrine.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 Internal Security, GS2 Governance/IT, International Relations
  🌐 Contemporary: India faces escalating cyber threats from state and non-state actors, underscored by the 2022 AIIMS ransomware attack.
