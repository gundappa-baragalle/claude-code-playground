**[Q4866]** [2025]
> Consider the following statements :  I. Capital receipts create a liability or cause a reduction in the assets of the Government. II. Borrowings and disinvestment are capital receipts. III. Interest received on loans creates a liability of the Government.  Which of the statements given above are correct?

  ✅ A) I and II only
     B) II and III only
     C) I and III only
     D) I, II and III

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement III is a trap — interest received on loans is a REVENUE receipt (not capital receipt) for the government; it creates no liability.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Only I (capital receipts create liability) and II (borrowings and disinvestment are capital receipts) are correct. Interest received is a revenue receipt. Answer: A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Capital receipts: borrowings (liability) + disinvestment (asset reduction)
     - Interest received on loans = revenue receipt, not capital receipt

**[Q4917]** [2025]
> Suppose the revenue expenditure is ₹ 80,000 crores and the revenue receipts of the Government are ₹ 60,000 crores. The Government budget also shows borrowings of ₹ 10,000 crores and interest payments of ₹6,000 crores. Which of the following statements are correct?  I. Revenue deficit is ₹ 20,000 crores. II. Fiscal deficit is ₹ 10,000 crores. III. Primary deficit is ₹ 4,000 crores.  Select the correct answer using the code given below.

     A) I and II only
     B) II and III only
     C) I and III only
  ✅ D) I, II and III

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may miscalculate Primary Deficit by forgetting it equals Fiscal Deficit minus Interest Payments (10,000 - 6,000 = 4,000), or confuse Revenue Deficit with Fiscal Deficit.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Revenue Deficit = Rev Exp - Rev Receipts = 80,000 - 60,000 = 20,000 (I correct). Fiscal Deficit = Total Expenditure - Total Receipts excl. borrowings = borrowings = 10,000 (II correct). Primary Deficit = FD - Interest = 10,000 - 6,000 = 4,000 (III correct). Answer: D.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Revenue Deficit = Revenue Expenditure - Revenue Receipts
     - Primary Deficit = Fiscal Deficit - Interest Payments

**[Q4921]** [2025]
> A country's fiscal deficit stands at ₹ 50,000 crores. It is receiving ₹ 10,000 crores through non-debt creating capital receipts. The country's interest liabilities are ₹ 1,500 crores. What is the gross primary deficit?

  ✅ A) ₹ 48,500 crores
     B) ₹ 51,500 crores
     C) ₹ 58,500 crores
     D) None of the above

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Candidates may subtract non-debt capital receipts from fiscal deficit to get primary deficit, confusing the formula. Primary Deficit = Fiscal Deficit - Interest Payments.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Primary Deficit = Fiscal Deficit - Interest Payments = 50,000 - 1,500 = 48,500 crores. Non-debt capital receipts are not deducted for primary deficit calculation. Answer: A.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Primary Deficit = Fiscal Deficit minus Interest Payments
     - Non-debt capital receipts affect net borrowing, not the primary deficit formula

**[Q4304]** [2022]
> With reference to the expenditure made by an organisation or a company, which of the following statements is/are correct?  1. Acquiring new technology is capital expenditure. 2. Debt financing is considered capital expenditure, while equity financing is considered revenue expenditure.  Select the correct answer using the code given below:

  ✅ A) 1 only
     B) 2 only
     C) Both 1 and 2
     D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 2 incorrectly reverses the classification — debt financing (borrowing) is capital expenditure is wrong; acquiring technology (Statement 1) is correctly capital expenditure.

     B) 2 only
  ✅ C) Both 1 and 2
     D) Neither 1 nor 2

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Statement 1 about household savings funding government borrowings may seem counter-intuitive — but government securities sold in market are purchased partly by household savings through banks.

**[Q4118]** [2021]
> Which one of the following is likely to be the most inflationary in its effects?

     A) Repayment of public debt
     B) Borrowing from the public to finance a budget deficit
     C) Borrowing from the banks to finance a budget deficit
  ✅ D) Creation of new money to finance a budget deficit

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Option B (borrowing from public) sounds inflationary since it's debt financing, but borrowing from the public is non-inflationary as it transfers existing money; new money creation is most inflationary.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Creating new money (deficit monetization/seigniorage) directly increases money supply and is most inflationary; borrowing from public or banks uses existing money; debt repayment is deflationary.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Monetization of deficit (printing money to finance deficit) is directly inflationary as it increases money supply
     - Borrowing from public (selling bonds) transfers purchasing power without increasing money supply

### GS3

**[2021]** Q2. Distinguish between Capital Budget and Revenue Budget. Explain the components of both these Budgets.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Budget classification, Fiscal management, Public finance basics

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The Union Budget is divided into Capital Budget and Revenue Budget, each serving distinct fiscal functions in managing government finances.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Revenue Budget: Revenue receipts (tax + non-tax) and revenue expenditure (salaries, subsidies, interest); deficit = Revenue Deficit
      • Capital Budget: Capital receipts (loans, disinvestment, market borrowings) and capital expenditure (infrastructure, loans to states); deficit contributes to Fiscal Deficit
      • Key distinction: Revenue expenditure is consumptive; capital expenditure creates assets or reduces liabilities
      • Components of capital receipts: market borrowings, external borrowings, disinvestment proceeds, small savings
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** A sound budget balances revenue management with capital investment to ensure both short-term stability and long-term developmental outcomes.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3: Indian Economy, GS2: Government Budgeting & Accountability
  🌐 Contemporary: Distinction between capital and revenue spending is central to debates on infrastructure push vs subsidy rationalization.

**[2019]** Q12. The public expenditure management is a challenge to the Government of India in the context of budget making during the post-liberalization period. Clarify it.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHY | 15m | ~250w*
  🏷️ Themes: Fiscal discipline, Public expenditure management, Post-liberalization economic governance

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~250 words: intro 37w / body 162w / conclusion 50w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Public expenditure management in post-liberalization India is beset by structural challenges arising from competing fiscal priorities, subsidy burdens, and constitutional obligations.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Challenge 1: Rising subsidy burden (food, fuel, fertilizer) crowding out capital investment
      • Challenge 2: Revenue-capital expenditure imbalance — revenue expenditure dominates, reducing productive investment
      • Challenge 3: Off-budget financing and extra-budgetary liabilities undermining transparency
      • Challenge 4: FRBM targets frequently missed due to populist pressures and economic shocks
      • Way forward: Zero-based budgeting, output-outcome framework, and expenditure rationalization
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Effective public expenditure management requires institutionalizing fiscal rules, improving expenditure quality, and strengthening accountability mechanisms.

**[2013]** Q2. What were the reasons for the introduction of Fiscal Responsibility and Budget Management (FRBM) Act, 2003 ? Discuss critically its salient features and their effectiveness.
  *Paper: GS3 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~200w*
  🏷️ Themes: Reasons for FRBM introduction, Key features, Effectiveness and limitations

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~200 words: intro 30w / body 130w / conclusion 40w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The Fiscal Responsibility and Budget Management (FRBM) Act 2003 was enacted to institutionalize fiscal discipline, reduce India's burgeoning deficits, and restore macroeconomic stability.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Reasons for introduction: rising fiscal deficit (6%+ GDP), revenue deficit, monetization of deficit (RBI printing money), macroeconomic instability, credit rating concerns
      • Key features: fiscal deficit target — 3% of GDP; revenue deficit elimination; transparency — Medium-Term Fiscal Policy Statement; escape clause for natural disasters/national security
      • Effectiveness: India achieved near-FRBM targets by 2007-08 (before Global Financial Crisis); reduced monetization of deficit; improved fiscal transparency
      • Limitations: targets suspended during GFC (2008), again during COVID (2020); structural revenue shortfalls continue; off-budget borrowings circumvent targets
      • NK Singh Committee (2017) review: recommended a fiscal council; debt-to-GDP target (60%) alongside deficit targets; differentiated targets for Centre and States
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** FRBM represents a critical institutional mechanism for fiscal prudence, but its effectiveness depends on political commitment and flexible application during genuine macroeconomic crises.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3 — Public finance, economy, GS2 — Governance, Parliament
  🌐 Contemporary: India's fiscal consolidation path post-COVID has stretched FRBM targets; glide path remains debated.
