**[Q4488]** [2023]
> Ilmenite and rutile, abundantly available in certain coastal tracts of India, are rich sources of which one of the following?

     A) Aluminium
     B) Copper
     C) Iron
  ✅ D) Titanium

  ⚠️ **Trap:** Ilmenite (FeTiO3) and rutile (TiO2) sound metallic; students may associate with iron (Fe in ilmenite formula) rather than recognizing both as titanium ores.
  💡 **Eliminate:** Both ilmenite (iron titanate, FeTiO3) and rutile (titanium dioxide, TiO2) are titanium ores. India's Kerala coast (beach placer deposits) is rich in both. Option D (Titanium) is correct.
  📌 **Key facts:**
     - Ilmenite (FeTiO3) and Rutile (TiO2) are the primary commercial ores of Titanium
     - India's Chavara coast (Kerala) and Manavalakurichi (Tamil Nadu) are major ilmenite/rutile deposits

     - India has large reserves but mostly of non-coking/high-ash coal

  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHAT | 10m | ~150w*

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** The Deccan Trap, one of the world's largest volcanic features, is a significant natural resource region with diverse geological, agricultural, and mineral potential.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Formation: massive basaltic lava flows during Cretaceous-Palaeogene boundary (~66 million years ago), possibly linked to Chicxulub impact
      • Soil resources: Deccan Trap weathers to black cotton soil (regur) — fertile, moisture-retentive, ideal for cotton cultivation
      • Mineral resources: basalt for construction, zeolites (industrial uses), phosphorite, laterite (iron/aluminium oxides)
      • Water resources: basaltic aquifers support groundwater in Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat
      • Biodiversity: unique endemism in flora/fauna adapted to rocky Deccan terrain
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** The Deccan Trap's multi-dimensional resource potential calls for scientifically planned extraction to balance economic use with ecological conservation.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1 (Indian geography, soils), GS3 (minerals, energy)
  🌐 Contemporary: Deccan Trap basalt is increasingly used in mineral fibre production and construction; groundwater depletion in Deccan plateau is a growing concern.

**[2022]** Q7. Examine the potential of wind energy in India and explain the reasons for their limited spatial spread.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: HOW | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Wind energy geography, Renewable energy, Regional distribution

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** India's wind energy potential is substantial but geographically concentrated, limiting its spatial spread despite being the world's fourth-largest wind power producer.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Potential: estimated 695 GW at 100m height; 1,000+ GW offshore potential
      • Spatial concentration: coastal states and Rajasthan dominate — monsoon winds and Thar Desert thermals
      • Reasons for limited spread: interior peninsular plateau has inadequate wind speed; Himalayan and NE regions unsuitable
      • Offshore wind: potential off Gujarat and Tamil Nadu coasts largely untapped due to high capital costs
      • Land constraints: wind farms require large land areas — competing with agriculture in densely populated areas
      • Grid evacuation challenges: wind generation peaks in monsoon but grid capacity in windy regions limited
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Realising India's full wind energy potential requires developing offshore capacity, improving grid infrastructure in wind-rich states, and addressing the spatial imbalance through hybrid solar-wind projects.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS3: Energy, GS1: Indian geography, Environment: clean energy
  🌐 Contemporary: India's offshore wind ambition (30 GW by 2030) is now being actively pursued with first tenders issued.

**[2021]** Q5. Despite India being one of the countries of the Gondwanaland, its mining industry contributes much less to its Gross Domestic Product (GDP) in percentage. Discuss.
  *Paper: GS1 | Dim: WHY | 10m | ~150w*
  🏷️ Themes: Mining sector underperformance, Gondwana mineral heritage, Policy and regulatory barriers

  **Answer Framework:**
  *(~150 words: intro 30w / body 97w / conclusion 30w)*
  - 🔑 **Intro:** Despite inheriting Gondwana's rich geological legacy, India's mining sector contributes disproportionately little to GDP, reflecting deep structural and policy constraints.
  - 📋 **Body:**
      • Gondwana connection: India's coal, iron ore, manganese, bauxite deposits are Gondwana-era formations concentrated in peninsular India
      • Low GDP contribution: mining contributes ~2.5% of GDP despite vast reserves — far below countries like Australia (~8–10%)
      • Reasons: complex land acquisition, forest clearances under FRA and Environment Protection Act, infrastructure deficit, illegal mining displacing organized mining
      • Caste-based and tribal land issues: most mineral-rich areas fall in tribal districts with Fifth Schedule protections
      • Reform gap: MMDR amendments improved auction transparency but implementation remains challenging
  - 🎯 **Conclusion:** Unlocking India's Gondwana mineral wealth requires balancing environmental protection with regulatory streamlining and ensuring equitable benefit-sharing with affected communities.
  🔗 Interlinkages: GS1: Physical geography of India, GS3: Industry and infrastructure, GS2: Tribal rights and governance
  🌐 Contemporary: Mining sector reforms and the Critical Minerals Mission make this directly relevant.
